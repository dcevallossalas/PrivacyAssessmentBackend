"Category 0: While the Regulation defines applicability and roles (responsible/processor) and extraterritorial reach (2) and representative requirements (3), it does not require an organization to systematically determine and document its internal/external context as an input to a management system (e.g., governance, strategy, internal issues, contractual requirements) as ISO 27701 expects. It also does not address the \u201cclimate change relevance\u201d determination at all.\n\nCategory 1: The Regulation is centered on data subjects (\u201ctitulares\u201d) rights and the supervisory authority (12)-(16), but it does not require an organization to identify all \u201cinterested parties\u201d and their requirements (e.g., customers, partners, subcontractors, employees as internal interested parties) and decide which requirements are addressed through a PIMS. There is no explicit requirement to maintain a stakeholder/requirements register or equivalent management-system artifact.\n\nCategory 2: The Regulation sets legal scope of application (2), but it does not require each organization to define and maintain a documented scope for a privacy information management system (boundaries, applicability, interfaces, exclusions) as documented information. There is also no explicit requirement to ensure the scope \u201cincludes the processing of PII\u201d as a management-system scoping exercise (it is assumed legally, not operationally scoped).\n\nCategory 3: The Regulation imposes substantive compliance duties (e.g., accountability (58), privacy by design/default (59)-(60)), but it does not compel establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a formal PIMS with defined processes and interactions (management system architecture). In other words, it mandates controls, not the ISO-style system framework.\n\nCategory 4: There is no explicit \u201ctop management leadership and commitment\u201d requirement (e.g., integration into business processes, ensuring resources, promoting continual improvement) comparable to ISO clause 5.1. Some obligations imply organizational commitment (58)-(60), but they are not framed as top-management duties nor do they require demonstrable leadership behaviors.\n\nCategory 5: The Regulation does not require a documented privacy policy approved by top management, communicated internally, and made available to interested parties. Transparency/notice elements appear in consent and communications to data subjects (5)-(6), (28), but that is not a management policy framework.\n\nCategory 6: The Regulation defines roles like DPO (48) and representative (3)-(4)(5), and mentions joint controllers (37), but it does not require the organization to assign and communicate responsibilities/authorities for all relevant PIMS roles (including responsibility for PIMS conformance and reporting to top management). Even for the DPO, it does not establish an internal responsibility matrix beyond independence and resourcing (48).\n\nCategory 7: The Regulation requires risk-based thinking in several places (e.g., breach notification conditioned on risk (24), DPIA as preventive risk analysis (29)-(32), accountability considering risks (58), design/default considering risks (59)-(60)), but it does not require a defined privacy risk assessment process with risk criteria, acceptance criteria, consistent repeatability, statement of applicability, risk owners, and a documented risk treatment plan as ISO 27701 clause 6.1.2 expects. It also does not require explicit \u201crisks and opportunities\u201d planning for the PIMS as a system (beyond compliance risk).\n\nCategory 8: There is no requirement to set measurable privacy objectives, monitor them, communicate them, and keep them as documented information, nor to plan actions/resources/owners/timelines to achieve them. Some performance indicators are optional (\u201cpodr\u00e1\u201d) for demonstrating measures (36), but that is not an objectives framework.\n\nCategory 9: The Regulation does not require planned management of changes to the PIMS. It includes periodic review/update of measures (58) and continuous evaluation of state of the art (34), but it does not establish a controlled change planning process for privacy management system changes.\n\nCategory 10: The Regulation implies resourcing (e.g., DPO must be facilitated with resources (48); costs are not an excuse (35)), but it does not require the organization to determine and provide resources for establishing/maintaining/continually improving a PIMS as a system. It also lacks requirements to document resource planning and allocation for privacy management.\n\nCategory 11: Competence requirements are not established for personnel generally. The Regulation sets eligibility requirements for the DPO (55) but does not require competence determination, training, or evaluation for all persons doing work under the organization\u2019s control that affects privacy performance (e.g., engineers, customer support, HR). The public authority training obligation (Transitory Second) is not an organizational competence management requirement.\n\nCategory 12: There is no general requirement for awareness (privacy policy awareness, contribution to PIMS effectiveness, implications of nonconformity) for staff and contractors. The Regulation focuses on external rights handling (12)-(16) and DPO role (48), but not organization-wide awareness programs.\n\nCategory 13: The Regulation requires certain communications (e.g., breach notifications to authority and data subjects (24)-(28); channels for rights requests (12)), but it does not require the organization to determine and plan internal/external communications relevant to a PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a systematic communication plan. It also does not cover internal escalation/communication routines for privacy governance.\n\nCategory 14: Some recordkeeping is mandated (e.g., records of processing activities (38)-(39), records of rights requests (15), transfer registers (78), registry reporting (85)-(86)), but ISO 27701\u2019s documented information controls (creation/update, approval, versioning, access control, retention/disposition for PIMS documentation) are not required. The Regulation does not establish a general document control regime for privacy management documentation.\n\nCategory 15: The Regulation mandates operational controls for specific processing activities (consent (5)-(6), retention (8)-(11), transfers (21)-(23), processor contracts (41), deletion by processor (46)), but it does not require \u201coperational planning and control\u201d in the ISO sense: defining process criteria, controlling processes against criteria, controlling unintended changes, and ensuring externally provided processes are controlled as part of a PIMS. Vendor/processor control exists contractually (41), (45), (47) but not as a broader operational control framework.\n\nCategory 16: DPIA is required in certain cases (31)-(32) and breach notification includes risk evaluation (26)(7), but the Regulation does not require periodic privacy risk assessments at planned intervals or upon significant changes, nor retention of risk assessment results as part of a defined risk assessment program. DPIA is narrower than ISO\u2019s ongoing risk assessment requirement and is triggered only in specified cases.\n\nCategory 17: The Regulation requires measures to mitigate risks (29)-(32), accountability measures (58)-(60), and remediation measures in breach notification content (26)(6), but it does not require implementing a documented privacy risk treatment plan with approvals by risk owners and retention of treatment results. It also lacks the ISO concept of selecting controls and justifying inclusions/exclusions (statement of applicability).\n\nCategory 18: Aside from optional KPIs/metrics to demonstrate measures (36) and authority statistical purposes (25), the Regulation does not require the organization to define what to monitor/measure, methods, frequency, analysis/evaluation, and to evaluate privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness. There is no requirement for a monitoring and measurement program with documented evidence.\n\nCategory 19: The Regulation mentions audits only as a possible mechanism within codes of conduct supervision (70) and allows controller review of processor records/processes (47), but it does not require internal audits of a PIMS at planned intervals, an audit program, auditor independence, audit criteria/scope, and documented audit results. Processor \u201creview\u201d (47) is not an internal audit of the organization\u2019s privacy management system.\n\nCategory 20: There is no requirement for top management to conduct management reviews of a PIMS, with specified inputs (audit results, monitoring trends, changes in context/requirements) and documented outputs. The Regulation provides for authority oversight and plans (81) but that is the regulator\u2019s management, not the organization\u2019s management review.\n\nCategory 21: The Regulation implies ongoing improvement by requiring review/update of measures (58) and continuous evaluation of state of the art (34), but it does not require continual improvement of the suitability, adequacy, and effectiveness of a PIMS as a management system. Improvement is framed as compliance maintenance rather than a structured continual improvement cycle.\n\nCategory 22: The Regulation establishes an administrative sanctioning regime (90) and a public register of infringers (87)-(89), but it does not require organizations to manage nonconformities and corrective actions internally (root cause analysis, preventing recurrence, effectiveness review, documented evidence). Regulatory enforcement is not equivalent to an ISO corrective action process embedded in the PIMS."
