"Category 0: Understanding the organization and its context (4.1)  \nThe Regulation defines applicability and roles at a legal level (e.g., responsible/processor and extraterritorial reach in (2) and representative requirement in (3)), but it does not require an organization to *systematically determine and document* its internal/external context as an input to a management system (e.g., governance model, internal policies, strategic direction, contractual landscape, relevant judicial/administrative decisions). The text is prescriptive about compliance duties, not about establishing a structured \u201ccontext analysis\u201d process as ISO expects.  \nAlso, ISO/IEC 27701:2025 explicitly requires determining whether *climate change* is a relevant issue; the Regulation contains no equivalent requirement or linkage between environmental/climate factors and privacy context (no mention across (1)\u2013(90)).\n\nCategory 1: Understanding the needs and expectations of interested parties (4.2)  \nThe Regulation focuses mainly on the data subject (\u201ctitular\u201d) rights exercise channels and complaints ((12)\u2013(16)), and on authority functions ((79)\u2013(83)), but it does not require organizations to identify *all relevant interested parties* (e.g., customers, suppliers, subcontractors, joint controllers, regulators, internal stakeholders) and determine their requirements as a managed input to the PIMS. It addresses some relationships (joint controllers in (37), controller\u2013processor contract in (41), subprocessing in (45)), yet not as a systematic stakeholder-requirements determination process.  \nISO also expects deciding which interested-party requirements will be addressed through the PIMS; the Regulation does not impose a governance step to prioritize/commit to stakeholder requirements beyond legal compliance (it is silent on this management-system selection logic beyond compliance obligations in (58)).\n\nCategory 2: Determining the scope of the privacy information management system (4.3)  \nThe Regulation defines legal scope of applicability ((2)) and requires certain records (e.g., processing activities in (38)\u2013(39)), but it does not require an organization to define and maintain a *documented PIMS scope statement* (boundaries, interfaces, exclusions) as ISO requires. Legal applicability is not the same as management-system scope definition.  \nThere is also no explicit requirement to ensure the scope includes all PII processing in a structured way (e.g., mapping business units, products, services, outsourced processes) beyond the record-keeping triggers in (38)\u2013(39).\n\nCategory 3: Privacy information management system (4.4)  \nThe Regulation contains accountability and proactive responsibility concepts ((58)\u2013(60)) and operational obligations (e.g., DPIA in (29)\u2013(32), breach notification in (24)\u2013(28)), but it does not require establishing, implementing, maintaining, and *continually improving a PIMS* with defined processes and interactions. It is a compliance regulation, not a management system standard.  \nIn particular, it does not require defining process interactions, system-level governance, or a PDCA-style structure; it only implies updating measures \u201cwhen necessary\u201d ((58)) without the full PIMS architecture.\n\nCategory 4: Leadership and commitment (5.1)  \nThe Regulation assigns obligations to controllers/processors and introduces the DPO role ((48)\u2013(52)), but it does not require \u201ctop management\u201d to demonstrate leadership/commitment, integrate PIMS into business processes, ensure resources, or promote continual improvement as explicit governance duties. The closest is the general obligation to apply measures and be able to demonstrate compliance ((58)), but it is not framed as leadership accountability.  \nThere is no explicit requirement for top management to ensure the PIMS achieves intended results or to communicate the importance of privacy management internally (no equivalent across (1)\u2013(90)).\n\nCategory 5: Privacy policy (5.2)  \nThe Regulation requires informing data subjects for consent ((5)) and sets operational rules, but it does not require top management to establish a *privacy policy* as documented information, communicate it internally, and make it available to interested parties. Transparency to data subjects is not the same as an organizational privacy policy framework.  \nWhile codes of conduct may exist ((68)\u2013(70)) and certification schemes ((64)\u2013(67)) can support governance, they are optional mechanisms and not a mandatory privacy policy requirement aligned to ISO 5.2.\n\nCategory 6: Roles, responsibilities and authorities (5.3)  \nThe Regulation defines controller/processor responsibilities ((33), (40)\u2013(47)), joint controllers ((37)), and DPO requirements ((48)\u2013(57)), but it does not require top management to assign and communicate *PIMS-specific* responsibilities (e.g., responsibility for ensuring PIMS conformity to ISO requirements and reporting PIMS performance to top management).  \nThe DPO is defined as an advisor/supervisor ((48)), yet ISO requires explicit internal governance assignments for the management system itself; the Regulation does not establish that internal reporting line or accountability structure.\n\nCategory 7: Actions to address risks and opportunities (6.1)  \nThe Regulation includes risk-based concepts (e.g., \u201cprobability and severity of risks\u201d in (33), DPIA risk evaluation in (32)(3), breach risk evaluation in (26)(7)), but it does not require a formal process to identify *risks and opportunities* for the PIMS, plan actions, integrate them into processes, and evaluate effectiveness as ISO 6.1.1 requires.  \nIt also does not require defining privacy risk criteria, risk acceptance criteria, consistent repeatable assessments, statement of applicability, risk owners\u2019 approval, and documented risk treatment process as described in ISO 6.1.2; DPIA obligations ((29)\u2013(32)) are narrower and triggered by certain processing, not a full PIMS risk framework.\n\nCategory 8: Privacy objectives and planning to achieve them (6.2)  \nThe Regulation does not require organizations to set measurable privacy objectives, monitor them, communicate them, update them, and document them. It focuses on compliance duties (e.g., (58)\u2013(60)) and evidence of measures ((36)), but not on objective-setting and planning.  \nEven where KPIs are mentioned ((36)), they are optional (\u201cpodr\u00e1 determinar\u201d) and not tied to a structured objectives framework with responsibilities, timelines, and evaluation methods as ISO requires.\n\nCategory 9: Planning of changes (6.3)  \nThe Regulation requires updating measures when necessary ((58)) and considering state of the art continuously ((34)), but it does not require planned change management for the PIMS (e.g., assessing consequences of changes, ensuring integrity of the system, planned implementation).  \nChange control is not addressed as a management-system discipline; references to periodic review (e.g., data retention review in (9)) are operational and not a PIMS change-planning requirement.\n\nCategory 10: Resources (7.1)  \nThe Regulation states that lack of resources/cost is not an excuse ((35)) and that controllers must apply appropriate measures ((33)), but it does not require determining and providing resources for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and improving a PIMS.  \nISO expects explicit resource planning for the management system; the Regulation imposes outcomes (compliance) without requiring a resource management process.\n\nCategory 11: Competence (7.2)  \nThe Regulation sets competence-like requirements only for the DPO (education/experience in (55)) and mentions training initiatives by the authority in a transitional provision, but it does not require organizations to determine competence needs for all personnel affecting privacy performance, ensure competence via training/experience, and keep documented evidence.  \nThus, competence management is partial and role-specific (DPO), not organization-wide as ISO requires.\n\nCategory 12: Awareness (7.3)  \nThe Regulation does not require personnel awareness of a privacy policy, their contribution to PIMS effectiveness, or implications of nonconformity. It focuses on external transparency and rights channels ((12)\u2013(16)) and compliance measures ((58)\u2013(60)).  \nEven though the authority may conduct public trainings (Transitory Second), that is not an organizational awareness program requirement.\n\nCategory 13: Communication (7.4)  \nThe Regulation mandates certain external communications (e.g., breach notifications to authority and data subjects in (24)\u2013(28); rights request channels in (12)), but it does not require an organization to determine *internal and external communications* for the PIMS in a structured way (what/when/with whom/how) beyond these specific legal events.  \nISO expects a communication plan covering the management system; the Regulation provides event-driven communications only.\n\nCategory 14: Documented information (7.5)  \nThe Regulation requires certain records (e.g., rights requests register in (15), processing activities register in (38)\u2013(39), international transfer registry info in (78)), but it does not establish a general documented information control framework (creation, updating, version control, retention/disposition, protection, control of external documents) for the PIMS.  \nIt also does not require documented information \u201cdetermined by the organization\u201d for PIMS effectiveness, nor does it define controls for document lifecycle management as ISO 7.5.2\u20137.5.3 require.\n\nCategory 15: Operational planning and control (8.1)  \nThe Regulation imposes operational obligations (consent in (5)\u2013(6), retention in (8)\u2013(11), transfers in (21)\u2013(23), breach handling in (24)\u2013(28), DPIA in (29)\u2013(32), controller/processor controls in (33)\u2013(47)), but it does not require organizations to define process criteria, control processes against criteria, and maintain documented information to ensure operations are carried out as planned (as a management-system requirement).  \nControl of externally provided processes is partially addressed via processor/subprocessor contracting ((41), (45)), but ISO expects broader operational control across all PIMS processes, including planned/unintended change handling, which is not systematically required.\n\nCategory 16: Privacy risk assessment (8.2)  \nThe Regulation requires DPIAs in certain cases ((31)\u2013(32)) and risk evaluation for breaches ((26)(7)), but it does not require performing privacy risk assessments at planned intervals or upon significant changes for the entire PIMS scope, nor retaining documented results of those periodic risk assessments as a general rule.  \nDPIA is a specific instrument for high-risk processing; ISO 8.2 is a recurring, system-wide risk assessment requirement.\n\nCategory 17: Privacy risk treatment (8.3)  \nThe Regulation requires adopting measures to mitigate risks (e.g., (33), (58)\u2013(60)) and DPIA to describe measures to address risks ((32)(4)), but it does not require a formal privacy risk treatment plan implementation and retention of documented results of risk treatment as a management-system artifact.  \nThere is no explicit requirement for risk treatment tracking, residual risk acceptance, or risk owner approvals (contrast with ISO 6.1.2(g)\u2013(h)), beyond general accountability language.\n\nCategory 18: Monitoring, measurement, analysis and evaluation (9.1)  \nThe Regulation allows the controller to define KPIs to demonstrate compliance ((36)), but it is optional and not a requirement to define what to monitor/measure, methods, timing, analysis, and evaluation of PIMS effectiveness.  \nAuthority-level statistics publication ((83)(2)) is not equivalent to organization-level monitoring and measurement of privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness.\n\nCategory 19: Internal Audit (9.2)  \nThe Regulation mentions that codes of conduct may include audits ((70), last paragraph) and that processors must allow review by controllers ((47)), but it does not require organizations to conduct *internal audits* of the PIMS at planned intervals, define audit programs, ensure auditor impartiality, and retain audit evidence.  \nController reviews of processor records ((47)) are contractual/operational oversight, not an internal audit program covering the organization\u2019s PIMS.\n\nCategory 20: Management review (9.3)  \nThe Regulation does not require top management to review the PIMS at planned intervals with defined inputs/outputs (audit results, nonconformities, performance trends, changes in context, improvement opportunities).  \nAlthough it requires updating measures when necessary ((58)) and continuous evaluation of state of the art ((34)), it does not establish a formal management review mechanism or documented evidence of such reviews.\n\nCategory 21: Continual improvement (10.1)  \nThe Regulation implies updating measures ((58)) and considering state of the art ((34)), but it does not require continual improvement of the *PIMS* suitability, adequacy, and effectiveness as a structured requirement.  \nIts improvement drivers are compliance- and technology-driven, not a management-system continual improvement cycle with defined evaluation and improvement actions.\n\nCategory 22: Nonconformity and corrective action (10.2)  \nThe Regulation provides for complaints to the authority ((16)) and sanctions procedures ((90)), and requires breach response measures ((26)(6)), but it does not require an internal nonconformity/corrective action process: identifying nonconformities, root-cause analysis, implementing corrective actions, reviewing effectiveness, and updating the PIMS with documented evidence.  \nEnforcement and incident response are not equivalent to ISO\u2019s internal corrective action system; the Regulation lacks explicit requirements for internal CAPA governance and records (beyond specific registers like (15))."
