"Category 0: The framework defines applicability and roles (responsible/processor) and extraterritorial reach (2), plus representative requirements (3), but it does not require an organization to systematically determine and document its internal/external context as a management-system input (e.g., governance, internal policies, contractual requirements, judicial/administrative decisions) as ISO expects. It also does not include the ISO 27701:2025 explicit requirement to determine whether climate change is a relevant issue for the PIMS context.\n\nCategory 1: While it strongly centers \u201ctitulares\u201d and the supervisory authority (12)-(16), (79)-(83), it does not require a structured process to identify *all* relevant interested parties and their requirements (e.g., customers, suppliers, subcontractors, joint controllers, employees as internal interested parties) and decide which requirements are addressed through the PIMS. The regulation provides channels for rights requests (12) and complaint escalation (16), but not a broader stakeholder-expectations determination process as a recurring management activity.\n\nCategory 2: The regulation sets broad legal scope and territorial applicability (2) but does not require each organization to define and maintain a documented PIMS scope statement with boundaries, interfaces, exclusions, and applicability criteria (including which processing activities, sites, systems, and business units are in-scope). It also does not require scope review when context/requirements change, beyond specific obligations like records (38)-(39).\n\nCategory 3: The regulation imposes many operational privacy obligations (e.g., accountability (58), privacy by design/default (59)-(60), records (38)-(39)), but it does not require establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a *privacy information management system* with defined processes and interactions (i.e., a management system architecture). There is no explicit requirement for a PDCA-style system, process mapping, or system-level continual improvement mechanisms beyond general \u201creview/update measures\u201d (58).\n\nCategory 4: There is no explicit \u201ctop management leadership and commitment\u201d requirement comparable to ISO (e.g., integrating PIMS into business processes, ensuring resources, promoting continual improvement). The closest elements are accountability obligations on the controller (58)-(60) and DPO independence/resources (48), but these do not equate to top management duties, nor do they require leadership demonstration.\n\nCategory 5: The regulation does not require a documented privacy policy approved by top management, communicated internally, and made available to interested parties. Some transparency/consent information duties exist (5) and breach communications (28), but these are not a policy framework for objectives and system commitments.\n\nCategory 6: Although it defines DPO role and independence (48)-(52) and representative (3)-(4)(5), it does not require an organization-wide assignment and communication of responsibilities/authorities for all PIMS roles (e.g., risk owners, internal audit, document control, incident response ownership). It also does not require appointing a management-system responsible person to report PIMS performance to top management.\n\nCategory 7: The regulation requires risk-based thinking in several places\u2014legitimate interest balancing (7)(3), breach notification based on risk (24), DPIA as preventive risk analysis (29)-(32), and accountability considering risks (58)-(60)\u2014but it does not require a formal, repeatable privacy risk assessment process with defined risk criteria, risk acceptance criteria, consistent/comparable results, and a statement of applicability as in ISO 27701. It also does not require a documented risk treatment plan approved by risk owners, even though DPIA must include measures (32)(4).\n\nCategory 8: There is no requirement to set measurable privacy objectives, monitor them, communicate them, and maintain documented information about objectives and plans. The regulation focuses on compliance duties (e.g., (58)-(60)) and metrics are optional (\u201cpodr\u00e1 determinar indicadores\u201d (36)), but ISO requires objectives as a management-system element.\n\nCategory 9: The regulation does not require planned management of changes to the PIMS (e.g., change control for processes, systems, vendors, or purposes) as a general requirement. Some change-related ideas appear indirectly (e.g., review/update measures (58), evaluate state of the art (34)), but not a structured \u201cplanning of changes\u201d clause.\n\nCategory 10: The regulation does not require determining and providing resources for establishing/maintaining/improving a PIMS. It requires providing resources to the DPO (48) and implies costs must be assumed (35), but ISO expects a broader resource planning requirement (people, tools, budget, time) for the management system.\n\nCategory 11: Competence requirements (training, education, experience, evaluation of effectiveness) are not imposed generally. The only competence-like provisions are DPO qualification requirements (55) and the authority\u2019s plan to provide training to the public/DPOs (Transitory Second), but ISO requires competence management for all personnel affecting privacy performance.\n\nCategory 12: The regulation does not require awareness programs ensuring personnel understand the privacy policy, their contribution, and implications of nonconformity. It includes obligations to provide clear information to adolescents (19) and to notify in clear language (28), but nothing about internal staff awareness.\n\nCategory 13: While it mandates specific communications (consent information (5), breach notifications (24)-(28), rights channels (12), joint controller transparency (37)), it does not require an organization to determine and manage internal/external communications for the PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a systematic plan. Communications are obligation-driven rather than management-system planned.\n\nCategory 14: The regulation requires certain records (e.g., rights request logs (15), processing activity records (38)-(39), international transfer registry info (78), breach notification content (26)) but it does not establish a general documented-information control framework (creation, review/approval, versioning, retention/disposition, access control) for all PIMS documents. Some retention rules exist for personal data (8)-(11) and for the authority\u2019s registry (89), but not for PIMS documentation lifecycle control.\n\nCategory 15: It does not require operational planning and control of PIMS processes with criteria, controlled changes, and control of externally provided processes as a management-system requirement. There are processor contract requirements (41) and audit/review rights over processors (47), but not a general operational control framework for all privacy processes.\n\nCategory 16: The regulation includes DPIA obligations (29)-(32) and risk evaluation for breaches (26)(7), but it does not require periodic privacy risk assessments at planned intervals or upon significant changes, nor retention of risk assessment results as a general requirement. DPIA is triggered only in cases established by law (31), leaving a gap versus ISO\u2019s broader, cyclical risk assessment expectation.\n\nCategory 17: The regulation requires implementing measures to mitigate risks (e.g., DPIA measures (32)(4), accountability measures (58)-(60)) but it does not require a formal privacy risk treatment plan, implementation tracking, and documented results of risk treatment. It also lacks ISO\u2019s requirement for residual risk acceptance by risk owners.\n\nCategory 18: The regulation allows/encourages indicators (36) and requires some registries/statistics by the authority (25), (83)(2), but it does not require organizations to define what to monitor/measure, methods, frequency, analysis/evaluation, and to evaluate privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness. The focus is compliance evidence rather than performance monitoring.\n\nCategory 19: There is no requirement for internal audits of a PIMS, audit programs, auditor independence, audit criteria/scope, or documented audit results. Some audit-like mechanisms exist externally (authority control mechanisms (81)-(82)) and controller\u2019s right to review processor records (47), but ISO requires internal audit as a management-system function.\n\nCategory 20: The regulation does not require top management to conduct management reviews at planned intervals with specified inputs/outputs and documented evidence. It includes periodic review of adequacy decisions by the authority (72) and controller\u2019s review/update of measures (58), but not management review of the organization\u2019s PIMS.\n\nCategory 21: Continual improvement of a PIMS is not required. The closest is the obligation to \u201crevisar y actualizar las medidas cuando sea necesario\u201d (58) and to \u201cevaluar continuamente el estado de la t\u00e9cnica\u201d (34), but ISO expects continual improvement of the management system\u2019s suitability/adequacy/effectiveness, not only technical/organizational measures.\n\nCategory 22: The regulation provides for sanctions and administrative procedures (90) and complaint mechanisms (16), but it does not require an organization to manage nonconformities and corrective actions in a structured way (root cause analysis, preventing recurrence, effectiveness review, documented evidence). External enforcement exists, yet ISO requires internal corrective action processes and records."
