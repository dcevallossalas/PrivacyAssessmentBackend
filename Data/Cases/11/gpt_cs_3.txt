"Below is the mapping of **Supreme Decree 016-2024-JUS (Reglamento de la Ley 29733)** items that align with each **ISO/IEC 27701:2025 (Clauses 4\u201310) category (0\u201322)**. I cite items as requested (article/subarticle/literal). I avoid listing every subparagraph when the whole article clearly covers the category.\n\n---\n\n### Category 0 \u2014 Understanding the organization and its context (4.1)\n**Items:** (I), (II), (IV), (V), (VI), (III)(10), (III)(22)  \n**Why:** These provisions define the regulation\u2019s purpose and context (object/finality), its material and territorial scope, exceptions, and\u2014critically for ISO 27701 context\u2014define the roles of **controller/responsible** and **processor/encargado**, which is central to determining the organization\u2019s role(s) and applicable obligations.\n\n---\n\n### Category 1 \u2014 Understanding needs and expectations of interested parties (4.2)\n**Items:** (IX)(1), (6), (7), (34), (37)(4), (42)(2), (43)(2), (60)(3), (60)(9), (64)\u2013(73), (88)\u2013(94), DCF(4)  \n**Why:** Interested parties include data subjects, the authority, and other recipients. The regulation sets transparency/information duties, contact points (representative/DPO), mechanisms for exercising rights, complaint/tutela procedures, and a citizen platform\u2014reflecting expectations for accessibility, responsiveness, and enforceability.\n\n---\n\n### Category 2 \u2014 Determining the scope of the PIMS (4.3)\n**Items:** (IV), (V), (VI), (I)  \n**Why:** ISO 27701 scope requires defining boundaries and applicability. These articles establish **what processing is covered**, **who is covered**, **exceptions**, and **territorial reach**, which are the legal determinants an organization must consider when setting PIMS scope.\n\n---\n\n### Category 3 None \n**Why:** While the decree does not mandate an ISO-style management system, it introduces **accountability (\u201cresponsabilidad proactiva\u201d)**, requires internal security documentation (security document, policies, inventory), and allows codes of conduct to demonstrate compliance\u2014elements analogous to establishing and maintaining a structured PIMS.\n\n---\n\n### Category 4 None \n**Why:** Leadership/commitment is reflected through accountability obligations requiring the controller to ensure compliance and demonstrate it, formal approval of the security document, ensuring staff awareness of consequences, and maintaining control/responsibility over outsourced processing.\n\n---\n\n### Category 5 \u2014 Privacy policy (5.2)\n**Items:** (7)(2)(1), (7), (IX)(1), (7), (47)(3), (60)(1), (60)(9)  \n**Why:** The regulation explicitly recognizes **privacy policies** as a transparency mechanism and requires internal policies in the security document. It also requires making key contact/representative information public and ensuring policies/codes are clear and accessible\u2014aligned with establishing and communicating a privacy policy.\n\n---\n\n### Category 6 \u2014 Roles, responsibilities and authorities (5.3)\n**Items:** (III)(10), (III)(17), (III)(20), (7), (31)\u2013(33), (36), (37)\u2013(39), (110)  \n**Why:** These provisions define controller/processor/representative roles, regulate outsourcing and subprocessing responsibilities, impose processor notification duties, require and define the DPO (Oficial) role and functions, and define authority roles in enforcement\u2014matching ISO\u2019s requirement to assign and communicate responsibilities.\n\n---\n\n### Category 7 \u2014 Actions to address risks and opportunities (6.1 incl. risk assessment process)\n**Items:** (IX)(2), (40), (41), (46)(2), (47)(3), (59)\u2013(60), (95)  \n**Why:** The decree embeds risk-based thinking via accountability, optional DPIA (explicitly tied to risk standards), disassociation/anon techniques based on risk factors, periodic review of security measures and training, and codes of conduct as proactive compliance tools\u2014together supporting risk/opportunity planning.\n\n---\n\n### Category 8 \u2014 Privacy objectives and planning to achieve them (6.2)\n**Items:** (IX)(2), (47)(3), (59)\u2013(60), DCF(5)  \n**Why:** Although it does not require \u201cprivacy objectives\u201d explicitly, it requires proactive accountability and internal policies/inventories (which operationalize objectives), supports structured commitments via codes of conduct, and mandates promotion of privacy culture\u2014consistent with setting and planning privacy goals.\n\n---\n\n### Category 9 None \n**Why:** Change planning is reflected in obligations to keep registrations updated, manage changes in provider privacy conditions (triggering consent if scope expands), and periodically review security measures and maintain an updated security document.\n\n---\n\n### Category 10 \u2014 Resources (7.1)\n**Items:** (3) [Decreto Supremo], (7)(1), (37), (47)  \n**Why:** The decree addresses resourcing indirectly: implementation is subject to budget availability (public sector framing), organizations must provide means to comply (including representative), designate a DPO in defined cases, and maintain security documentation\u2014implying allocation of people, tools, and budget.\n\n---\n\n### Category 11 \u2014 Competence (7.2)\n**Items:** (38), (39)(1)\u2013(2), (46)(2), DCF(5)  \n**Why:** Competence is supported by DPO qualification requirements, DPO duties to advise and verify compliance including training/sensitization, mandatory training plans tied to roles, and broader promotion of privacy culture.\n\n---\n\n### Category 12 \u2014 Awareness (7.3)\n**Items:** (39)(2), (46)(2), (47)(2), DCF(5)  \n**Why:** Awareness is addressed through requirements for staff sensitization/training, ensuring personnel understand consequences of noncompliance (security document), and DPO oversight of awareness activities.\n\n---\n\n### Category 13 \u2014 Communication (7.4)\n**Items:** (6), (7), (34), (37)(4)\u2013(5), (42)(2), (68)(2), (73), DCF(2), DCF(4), DCF(6)  \n**Why:** The regulation mandates communications to data subjects (information duties, breach notices, rights responses), to the authority (breach notification, DPO contact, cross-border flow registration), and provides channels/platforms and consultation mechanisms\u2014covering internal/external communication needs.\n\n---\n\n### Category 14 \u2014 Documented information (7.5)\n**Items:** (9), (14), (17), (35), (42)\u2013(45), (46)(3), (47), (83), (108), (112)(2), (115)  \n**Why:** ISO requires controlled documentation; the decree requires evidence/records for consent and transfers, incident documentation, registry filings, security logs retention, a formal security document, documentation of deletion actions, and formal enforcement records\u2014strong alignment with documented information controls.\n\n---\n\n### Category 15 \u2014 Operational planning and control (8.1)\n**Items:** (1), (6), (10)(4), (12)\u2013(17), (28)\u2013(33), (46)\u2013(57), (64)\u2013(69)  \n**Why:** These provisions operationalize privacy controls: consent collection and withdrawal mechanisms, information duties, transfer controls, processor/subprocessor governance, security controls (digital and physical), and procedures to handle rights requests\u2014i.e., planned and controlled processing operations.\n\n---\n\n### Category 16 \u2014 Privacy risk assessment (8.2)\n**Items:** (40), (41)(2), (46)(2)  \n**Why:** The DPIA article is the clearest risk assessment mechanism (even if \u201cfacultative\u201d), and disassociation requires selecting techniques based on risk factors; periodic review of security measures supports reassessment when changes/intervals occur.\n\n---\n\n### Category 17 \u2014 Privacy risk treatment (8.3)\n**Items:** (41), (46)\u2013(57), (34)(2)(4), (122)  \n**Why:** Risk treatment is reflected in mandated/expected safeguards (technical/organizational/physical), anonymization/disassociation, breach mitigation measures, and corrective measures ordered by the authority to reduce harm\u2014i.e., implementing controls to treat identified risks.\n\n---\n\n### Category 18 \u2014 Monitoring, measurement, analysis and evaluation (9.1)\n**Items:** (46)(2)\u2013(3), (35), (44)(1), (47)(1), (95), (108)  \n**Why:** Monitoring is supported by security monitoring/review, audit logs and traceability, incident documentation, keeping registry information updated, and the authority\u2019s risk-based supervision and resulting reports\u2014together enabling evaluation of privacy performance and control effectiveness.\n\n---\n\n### Category 19 \u2014 Internal audit (9.2)\n**Items:** (39)(2), (46)(2), (59)\u2013(60)  \n**Why:** The decree does not impose a formal internal audit program like ISO, but it explicitly includes \u201cauditor\u00edas\u201d within the DPO\u2019s compliance verification function and ties monitoring/training plans and code supervision mechanisms to ongoing compliance checking\u2014partial alignment.\n\n---\n\n### Category 20 \u2014 Management review (9.3)\n**Items:** (46)(2), (47)(1), (30)(1), (39)(2)  \n**Why:** Management review is not explicitly required, but periodic review of security measures, maintaining an updated formally approved security document, reviewing provider policy changes that may expand processing, and DPO reporting/verification functions collectively resemble management-level review inputs/outputs.\n\n---\n\n### Category 21 \u2014 Continual improvement (10.1)\n**Items:** (IX)(2), (59)(1), (108)(1)(2), (125)(2)  \n**Why:** Accountability and codes of conduct are framed as mechanisms to demonstrate and strengthen compliance; fiscalization reports can recommend improvements/corrections; and mitigation actions and collaboration are treated as attenuating factors\u2014encouraging continuous improvement of privacy practices.\n\n---\n\n### Category 22 \u2014 Nonconformity and corrective action (10.2)\n**Items:** (108)(1)(2)\u2013(5), (121), (122), (130), (132)\u2013(135)  \n**Why:** The enforcement framework provides the equivalent of nonconformity handling: detection via supervision, corrective measures, provisional/cautionary measures, coercive fines for noncompliance with corrective actions, classification of infringements, and recurrence/aggravation\u2014covering correction and prevention of recurrence.\n\n---"
