"Category 0: The Regulation defines roles such as \u201cresponsable\u201d and \u201cencargado\u201d ((III)(10), (III)(22)) and includes extraterritorial applicability criteria ((VI)), but it does not require an organization to systematically determine and document internal/external issues affecting a PIMS (e.g., governance, strategy, internal processes, contractual landscape) as a management-system \u201ccontext\u201d exercise. It also does not address ISO 27701\u2019s explicit expectation to consider whether climate change is a relevant issue (no equivalent anywhere).\n\nCategory 1: While it strongly recognizes data subjects and the supervisory authority (e.g., (6), (34), (88)-(94), (95)-(109)), it does not require an organization to identify all \u201cinterested parties\u201d relevant to a PIMS and their requirements (customers, partners, subcontractors, employees, etc.) as a structured input to the system. The framework is rights/obligations oriented, but not framed as a stakeholder-requirements determination process (no equivalent to ISO 4.2).\n\nCategory 2: The Regulation has an \u201c\u00e1mbito de aplicaci\u00f3n\u201d ((IV)-(VI)) but does not require each organization to define and maintain a documented scope statement for a privacy information management system that includes boundaries, applicability, interfaces, and included processing activities (ISO 4.3). It regulates processing broadly, but does not compel a PIMS scope document.\n\nCategory 3: There is no requirement to establish, implement, maintain, and continually improve a privacy information management system as a management system with defined processes and interactions (ISO 4.4). The Regulation imposes substantive compliance duties (consent, security, rights, etc.) but does not mandate a PIMS framework.\n\nCategory 4: The Regulation does not impose \u201ctop management leadership and commitment\u201d duties typical of ISO management systems (e.g., integration into business processes, ensuring resources, promoting continual improvement). It requires designation of an \u201cOficial de Datos Personales\u201d in certain cases ((37)) and defines functions ((39)), but that is not equivalent to top management accountability and leadership requirements.\n\nCategory 5: Although it references privacy policies as a means to comply with the duty to inform ((7)) and requires publication of certain contact details (e.g., representative (7), DPO contact (37.4)), it does not require top management to establish a privacy policy with ISO elements (commitment to meet requirements, continual improvement, framework for objectives, internal communication, availability as documented information). Policies are treated as transparency tools, not as a management policy instrument.\n\nCategory 6: The Regulation defines roles (controller/processor equivalents) and requires a DPO in certain scenarios ((37)-(39)), but it does not require an organization-wide assignment and communication of responsibilities and authorities for all relevant PIMS roles (ISO 5.3), nor does it require assigning responsibility for ensuring PIMS conformity and reporting PIMS performance to top management. The DPO\u2019s functions include advising and verifying compliance ((39)), but not the full ISO governance model.\n\nCategory 7: The Regulation introduces \u201cresponsabilidad proactiva\u201d ((IX)(2)) and defines DPIA as a proactive mechanism ((III)(13)), but it does not require a formal risk-and-opportunity planning process for a PIMS (ISO 6.1.1), nor a mandatory privacy risk assessment process with defined criteria, repeatability, statement of applicability, risk treatment plan, and risk owner acceptance (ISO 6.1.2). DPIA is explicitly optional ((40.1)), and while it references risk standards ((40.2)), it does not compel a comprehensive risk management lifecycle.\n\nCategory 8: There is no requirement to set measurable privacy objectives, monitor them, communicate them, and maintain documented information about them (ISO 6.2). The Regulation focuses on compliance obligations (e.g., (46)-(57), (34)-(36), (61)-(87)) but does not require objective-setting and planning to achieve objectives.\n\nCategory 9: The Regulation does not require planned management of changes to the PIMS (ISO 6.3). It contains operational requirements that may change (e.g., security controls (46)-(52)), but no clause requiring organizations to plan and control changes to the privacy management system itself.\n\nCategory 10: The Regulation does not require determining and providing resources for establishing/maintaining/improving a PIMS (ISO 7.1). It includes a public-sector budgeting clause for implementing the decree ((3)), but that is about government financing, not organizational resource planning for a PIMS.\n\nCategory 11: While it requires the DPO to have knowledge and practice in data protection ((38.1)) and mentions training/sensitization as part of DPO functions ((39.1)(2)), it does not require the organization to determine competence needs for all personnel affecting privacy performance, ensure competence, and retain evidence (ISO 7.2). The training aspect is indirect and not a general competence management requirement.\n\nCategory 12: The Regulation does not require awareness programs ensuring personnel are aware of the privacy policy, their contribution, and implications of nonconformity (ISO 7.3). It mandates certain behaviors and security measures ((46)-(57)) and requires the security document to be binding and known ((47.2)), but that is not the same as a structured awareness requirement tied to a PIMS.\n\nCategory 13: The Regulation contains many specific communication duties to data subjects and the authority (e.g., duty to inform (6), breach notification (34), DPO contact publication (37.4), representative disclosure (7.2)), but it does not require the organization to determine and manage internal/external communications for the PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a system requirement (ISO 7.4). Communications are prescribed case-by-case, not managed as a PIMS process.\n\nCategory 14: The Regulation requires documentation in specific areas (e.g., incident documentation (35), security document (47), logs (46)(3), evidence of consent burden (9), evidence of responses (68.4)), but it does not establish a general documented information control framework aligned to ISO 7.5 (creation/update controls, versioning, access, retention/disposition, control of external documents) for the PIMS as a whole. Requirements are fragmented and do not amount to a comprehensive document control system.\n\nCategory 15: The Regulation imposes operational controls (security measures (46)-(57), processor/subprocessor constraints (31)-(33), breach notification (34)), but it does not require \u201coperational planning and control\u201d in the ISO sense: defining process criteria, controlling processes against criteria, controlling planned changes, and ensuring control of externally provided processes as part of a PIMS (ISO 8.1). Third-party/outsourcing provisions exist ((28)-(33)), yet they are not framed as PIMS operational control requirements with documented process criteria and change control.\n\nCategory 16: The Regulation does not require periodic privacy risk assessments at planned intervals or upon significant changes, nor retention of documented results (ISO 8.2). It provides an optional DPIA mechanism ((40.1)) and references risk standards ((40.2)), but does not mandate recurring risk assessments or a schedule.\n\nCategory 17: The Regulation does not require implementing a privacy risk treatment plan and retaining documented results (ISO 8.3). It provides corrective measures in enforcement contexts ((122)) and security requirements ((46)-(57)), but these are not structured as risk treatment planning, approval, and tracking of residual risk.\n\nCategory 18: The Regulation does not require defining what to monitor/measure, methods, timing, analysis/evaluation, and documented evidence to evaluate privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness (ISO 9.1). It includes logging requirements ((46)(3)) and incident documentation ((35)), but those are security/traceability obligations, not a performance monitoring and evaluation program.\n\nCategory 19: The Regulation provides for regulatory audits/inspections by the authority ((95)-(109)) and DPO may support audits ((39.1)(2)), but it does not require organizations to conduct internal audits of their PIMS at planned intervals with an audit program, criteria, auditor impartiality, reporting, and retained evidence (ISO 9.2). External supervision is not a substitute for internal audit requirements.\n\nCategory 20: The Regulation does not require top management to perform management reviews of the PIMS at planned intervals with specified inputs/outputs and documented evidence (ISO 9.3). There is no equivalent governance cycle; the closest elements are enforcement procedures and DPO functions, which do not impose management review.\n\nCategory 21: The Regulation includes a \u201cresponsabilidad proactiva\u201d principle ((IX)(2)) and encourages mechanisms like codes of conduct ((59)-(60)) and optional DPIA ((40)), but it does not require continual improvement of a PIMS (ISO 10.1). Improvements appear as recommendations from authority inspections ((108.1)(2)) or as voluntary/attenuating measures ((59.4), (125.3)-(125.4)), not as a mandatory continual improvement cycle.\n\nCategory 22: The Regulation provides corrective measures imposed by the authority ((122)) and coercive fines for noncompliance with measures ((130)), but it does not require an organization\u2019s internal nonconformity and corrective action process (react, root cause, prevent recurrence, review effectiveness, update system, retain documented information) as per ISO 10.2. The framework is enforcement-driven rather than requiring internal CAPA management for a PIMS."
