"Category 0: The regulations define roles like \u201cbusiness,\u201d \u201cservice provider,\u201d \u201ccontractor,\u201d and \u201cthird party\u201d and allocate obligations accordingly (Article 4), but they do not require an organization to systematically determine and document its internal/external issues affecting a privacy management system (e.g., governance model, strategic direction, interfaces with other management systems) as a management-system \u201ccontext\u201d exercise. They also do not require determining and documenting controller/processor role per processing instance in the ISO sense; the CCPA role taxonomy is similar but not equivalent, and the text does not compel a formal role-mapping per processing activity.\n\nCategory 1: While consumers\u2019 expectations and stakeholder involvement appear in operational rules (e.g., \u201creasonable expectations\u201d in \u00a77002(b) and stakeholder involvement in risk assessments in \u00a77151), the regulations do not require a systematic identification of all \u201cinterested parties\u201d and their requirements for a PIMS (e.g., regulators, business customers, joint controllers, subcontractors) nor a decision of which requirements will be addressed through a management system. Stakeholder involvement is optional/limited (\u201cmay include external parties\u201d in \u00a77151(b)) and not framed as an ongoing requirements-management process.\n\nCategory 2: The regulations apply to CCPA-covered processing and impose many operational obligations, but they do not require an organization to define and maintain a documented scope statement for a privacy information management system that sets boundaries/applicability across the organization (sites, products, entities, processing contexts). \u201cTitle and Scope\u201d (\u00a77000) describes the regulation\u2019s scope, not the organization\u2019s PIMS scope.\n\nCategory 3: The text is a compliance regulation and does not require establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a privacy information management system with defined processes and interactions (PIMS lifecycle). It mandates specific controls (notices, requests handling, audits, risk assessments), but not the overarching management system architecture, process interaction mapping, or continual improvement mechanism as a system requirement.\n\nCategory 4: Some provisions require executive management attestations/certifications (e.g., cybersecurity audit certification \u00a77124; risk assessment submission attestation \u00a77157), but they do not require top management to demonstrate leadership and commitment to a PIMS (integration into business processes, ensuring resources, promoting continual improvement) as a general governance obligation. The regulations focus on compliance outputs rather than leadership behaviors and accountability for a management system.\n\nCategory 5: The regulations require a privacy policy and specify extensive content and accessibility requirements (\u00a77011), but ISO 27701\u2019s \u201cprivacy policy\u201d requirement is tied to a PIMS policy framework including commitments to meet applicable requirements and continual improvement. \u00a77011 is primarily consumer-facing disclosure; it does not compel an internal policy framework for objectives/continual improvement, nor internal communication/availability to all relevant interested parties beyond consumers.\n\nCategory 6: The regulations assign responsibilities in a functional way (e.g., training for individuals handling inquiries \u00a77100; executive management signatories \u00a77124, \u00a77157), but they do not require an organization-wide assignment and communication of roles, responsibilities, and authorities for a PIMS (including responsibility for ensuring PIMS conformity and reporting PIMS performance to top management). The required signatories are narrow (audit/risk submissions) and do not establish a full responsibility matrix.\n\nCategory 7: Risk assessments are mandated for certain processing (\u00a77150\u2013\u00a77157) and cybersecurity audits for certain businesses (\u00a77120\u2013\u00a77124), but ISO 27701 requires a general \u201crisks and opportunities\u201d planning process for the PIMS and a defined privacy risk assessment process with criteria, consistent repeatability, statement of applicability, risk treatment plan, risk owner approvals, and documented retention. The CCPA risk assessment regime (\u00a77152) is a balancing test and documentation set, but it does not require ISO-style risk criteria, risk acceptance criteria, SoA, or risk owner acceptance of residual risk.\n\nCategory 8: The regulations do not require an organization to set measurable privacy objectives at relevant functions/levels and plan to achieve them (what/resources/who/when/how evaluated). They require compliance metrics disclosure for large businesses (\u00a77102) and various timelines for request handling (\u00a77021), but those are not \u201cobjectives\u201d established and managed under a PIMS.\n\nCategory 9: There is no requirement to plan changes to a PIMS in a controlled manner. Some provisions require updating risk assessments upon material change (\u00a77155(a)(3)) and providing new Notice at Collection when purposes/categories change (\u00a77002(f)), but these are specific triggers, not a general change-management requirement for the privacy management system itself.\n\nCategory 10: The regulations do not require determining and providing resources for establishing/maintaining/improving a PIMS. They impose operational duties (e.g., provide methods for requests \u00a77020; audits \u00a77120; risk assessments \u00a77150) that imply resourcing, but do not compel a resource planning process for a management system.\n\nCategory 11: Training is required for certain personnel (\u00a77100), but ISO competence requires determining competence needs for all persons doing work under the organization\u2019s control affecting privacy performance, ensuring competence, taking actions to acquire competence, and retaining evidence. \u00a77100 is narrower (inquiries/requests handlers; threshold-based training policy) and does not require competence determination and evidence across all relevant roles (engineering, product, procurement, security, HR, etc.).\n\nCategory 12: The regulations do not require a general awareness program ensuring all persons under control are aware of the privacy policy, their contribution to PIMS effectiveness, and implications of nonconformity. Training obligations (\u00a77100) are limited to individuals responsible for handling inquiries/requests and do not establish broad awareness requirements.\n\nCategory 13: The regulations specify consumer-facing communications standards (\u00a77003) and methods for submitting requests/consent (\u00a77004, \u00a77020+), but ISO requires determining internal and external communications relevant to the PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a managed process. The CCPA rules do not require an organization-wide communications plan covering internal reporting, regulator engagement, processor/controller communications, etc.\n\nCategory 14: Record-keeping is required for consumer requests (\u00a77101) and retention of audit documents (\u00a77122(g)) and risk assessments (\u00a77155(c)), but ISO documented information requires control over creation, updating, approval, distribution, access, storage, retention, and disposition for all PIMS documentation. The regulations do not impose a comprehensive documented-information control system; they mandate certain records and security for them, not the full document control lifecycle.\n\nCategory 15: The regulations impose many operational controls (notices, request handling, contracts, audits, risk assessments), but ISO 27701 requires operational planning and control of PIMS processes with criteria, controlled changes, and control of externally provided processes relevant to the PIMS. While vendor/third-party contracting is addressed (Article 4), the regulations do not require defining process criteria and demonstrating process control across all PIMS processes as a management system requirement.\n\nCategory 16: Risk assessments are mandated for significant-risk processing (\u00a77150\u2013\u00a77157), but ISO requires performing privacy risk assessments at planned intervals and when significant changes occur, based on criteria established in the risk assessment process, and retaining results. The CCPA requires review/update at least every three years and within 45 days of material change (\u00a77155), but it does not require a formal risk assessment methodology with criteria and consistent comparable results (beyond the specified report elements), nor does it cover all processing within a PIMS scope\u2014only enumerated \u201csignificant risk\u201d activities.\n\nCategory 17: ISO requires implementing a privacy risk treatment plan and retaining results. The CCPA risk assessment requires identifying safeguards (\u00a77152(a)(6)) and documenting a plan to address cybersecurity gaps in audit reports (\u00a77123(e)(4)), but it does not require a formal risk treatment plan with risk owner approval/acceptance of residual risk, nor tracking treatment effectiveness as a defined PIMS process.\n\nCategory 18: The regulations require compiling and disclosing request metrics for very large businesses (\u00a77102) and require audits/risk assessments, but ISO requires determining what to monitor/measure, methods, timing, analysis/evaluation, and evaluating privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness. The CCPA does not require a comprehensive monitoring and measurement program for privacy performance across the organization, nor evaluation of PIMS effectiveness (it focuses on compliance artifacts and specific metrics).\n\nCategory 19: The regulations require \u201ccybersecurity audits\u201d for certain businesses (Article 9) and allow \u201cAgency audits\u201d (\u00a77304), but ISO internal audit requires the organization to conduct internal audits of the PIMS at planned intervals, define an audit program, objectives, criteria, scope, auditor selection for objectivity, and retain evidence. Article 9 is a cybersecurity program audit (not a PIMS internal audit) and is scoped to security controls; it does not require internal audits of privacy management system conformity to ISO-like requirements.\n\nCategory 20: There is no requirement for top management to conduct periodic management reviews of a PIMS with specified inputs/outputs and documented evidence. Executive attestations (\u00a77124, \u00a77157) and approvals in risk assessments (\u00a77152(a)(9)) are not equivalent to a management review of the overall system\u2019s suitability/adequacy/effectiveness.\n\nCategory 21: The regulations do not require continual improvement of a PIMS. They require updates in certain cases (e.g., update risk assessments \u00a77155; new Notice at Collection when changes \u00a77002(f)), but do not establish a continual improvement obligation for the management system as a whole.\n\nCategory 22: The regulations provide enforcement mechanisms (Article 13) and require record-keeping (\u00a77101), but they do not require a nonconformity and corrective action process (react, correct, root cause, prevent recurrence, review effectiveness, update PIMS) with documented evidence. Agency enforcement and audits (\u00a77301\u2013\u00a77304) are external oversight, not an internal corrective action system requirement."
