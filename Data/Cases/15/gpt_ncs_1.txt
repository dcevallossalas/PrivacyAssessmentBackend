"Category 0: The regulations define roles like \u201cbusiness,\u201d \u201cservice provider,\u201d \u201ccontractor,\u201d and \u201cthird party\u201d and allocate obligations accordingly (4), but they do not require an organization to systematically determine and document its internal/external issues affecting a PIMS outcome (ISO 4.1), nor to determine and document controller/processor role per processing instance (including joint controllership) as a management-system context exercise. The text is compliance-oriented and activity-specific (e.g., (1), (4), (10)) rather than requiring a formal \u201ccontext of the organization\u201d determination.\n\nThey also do not address the ISO 27701:2025 explicit requirement to consider whether climate change is a relevant issue to the PIMS (Category 0), and do not require a structured inventory of applicable \u201cjudicial/administrative decisions\u201d and \u201ccontractual requirements\u201d as part of context determination (beyond contract clauses required for vendors in (4)).\n\nCategory 1: While consumers are heavily addressed (2), and some stakeholder involvement is required for risk assessments (10) (e.g., employees and optional external stakeholders in (10)(a)-(b)), the regulations do not require the organization to identify all \u201cinterested parties\u201d relevant to a PIMS (e.g., supervisory authorities, customers in processor relationships, subcontractors) and determine their requirements as a systematic management-system input (ISO 4.2). The stakeholder involvement in (10) is limited to risk assessments, not the whole PIMS.\n\nThere is also no requirement to decide which interested-party requirements will be addressed through the PIMS (a scoping/commitment decision), nor to maintain this as documented information as part of a management system.\n\nCategory 2: The regulations have a \u201cTitle and Scope\u201d for the regulations themselves (1)(a), but they do not require each organization to define and document the scope/boundaries of its own privacy information management system, including interfaces, exclusions, and applicability across business units, products, and processing contexts (ISO 4.3). Requirements like notices, request handling, audits, and risk assessments apply based on statutory thresholds and activities, not on an organization-defined PIMS scope.\n\nThey also do not require the scope statement to be maintained as documented information, nor to explicitly ensure that all PII processing is included within the PIMS scope as a management-system artifact.\n\nCategory 3: The regulations impose many operational obligations (2)-(11), but they do not require establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a formal PIMS with defined processes and interactions (ISO 4.4). There is no requirement for a process model, PIMS governance structure, or continual improvement cycle as a system.\n\nInstead, the framework is a set of compliance controls (notices, rights, contracts, audits, risk assessments) without mandating an integrated management system architecture.\n\nCategory 4: Some executive involvement is implied for certifications and approvals (9)(7124), (10)(7152)(a)(9), (10)(7157)(c), but the regulations do not require \u201ctop management\u201d to demonstrate leadership and commitment to a PIMS (ISO 5.1), integrate PIMS into business processes, ensure resources, and promote continual improvement. Executive actions are limited to specific attestations and approvals, not ongoing leadership of a management system.\n\nThere is also no explicit requirement for top management to ensure PIMS achieves intended results, assign leadership responsibilities for PIMS performance reporting, or to communicate the importance of privacy management internally as a leadership duty.\n\nCategory 5: The regulations require a privacy policy and specify extensive content and accessibility requirements (2)(7011), but ISO 27701\u2019s \u201cprivacy policy\u201d in a PIMS sense also functions as a management-system policy with commitments (meet applicable requirements, continual improvement) and a framework for objectives. The CCPA privacy policy is primarily consumer-facing disclosure of practices and rights (2)(7011)(a), not a top-management policy statement for the PIMS.\n\nThey also do not require the privacy policy to include a commitment to continual improvement of the PIMS, nor to be communicated internally as a management-system policy (beyond training for certain staff in (8)(7100)).\n\nCategory 6: The regulations assign responsibilities in narrow contexts (e.g., who certifies audits/risk submissions in (9)(7124)(c) and (10)(7157)(c)), but they do not require an organization-wide assignment and communication of roles, responsibilities, and authorities for the PIMS (ISO 5.3). There is no mandated PIMS owner, no requirement to define authorities for ensuring PIMS conformity, and no requirement for reporting PIMS performance to top management.\n\nVendor oversight rights exist (4)(7051)(a)(6)-(8), but that is contractual control, not internal role assignment and governance for a PIMS.\n\nCategory 7: The regulations require risk assessments for certain high-risk processing (10) and cybersecurity audits for certain businesses (9), but ISO 27701 Category 7 includes planning actions to address risks/opportunities for the PIMS overall, defining risk criteria, producing a statement of applicability, and maintaining a privacy risk treatment plan with risk owner approval. The CCPA risk assessment in (10) is a balancing test and documentation of safeguards, but it does not require risk acceptance criteria, risk owners, or a statement of applicability against a control set.\n\nAlso, the CCPA framework does not require integrating risk actions into PIMS processes and evaluating effectiveness as a management-system planning activity (beyond specific audit/risk assessment requirements).\n\nCategory 8: The regulations do not require organizations to set measurable privacy objectives at relevant functions/levels, monitor them, and plan how to achieve them (ISO 6.2). Some metrics are required for large businesses (8)(7102), but those are reporting metrics about request volumes and response times, not objectives with plans, resources, responsibilities, and evaluation methods.\n\nRisk assessments require identifying benefits and deciding whether to initiate processing (10)(7152)(a)(4),(7), but that is not the same as establishing privacy objectives and planning to achieve them.\n\nCategory 9: The regulations require updates in certain contexts (e.g., risk assessment updates upon material change in (10)(7155)(a)(3)), but they do not require planned management of changes to the PIMS itself (ISO 6.3). There is no general change-control requirement for privacy governance, documentation, or processes beyond specific triggers.\n\nChange management appears as a cybersecurity program component to be assessed in audits (9)(7123)(c)(5)(E), but that is security-focused and audit-scoped, not PIMS change planning.\n\nCategory 10: The regulations do not require determining and providing resources for establishing/maintaining/continually improving a PIMS (ISO 7.1). They impose obligations (e.g., provide request methods in (3), maintain records in (8)(7101), conduct audits in (9), conduct risk assessments in (10)), but do not require resource planning or allocation as a management-system requirement.\n\nEven where training is required (8)(7100), it is not framed as part of a broader resource determination for a PIMS.\n\nCategory 11: Training requirements exist for staff handling inquiries/requests (8)(7100), but ISO competence requirements are broader: determine competence for all persons doing work under control affecting privacy performance, ensure competence, take actions, and retain evidence (ISO 7.2). The CCPA training is role-limited and does not require competence determination, effectiveness evaluation, or documented evidence of competence.\n\nIt also does not address competence for technical roles (e.g., engineers, product managers) whose work affects privacy outcomes, except indirectly via risk assessment stakeholder involvement (10)(7151)(a).\n\nCategory 12: The regulations do not require organization-wide awareness of the privacy policy, contributions to PIMS effectiveness, and implications of nonconformity (ISO 7.3). They require training for certain individuals (8)(7100), but not general awareness across personnel under organizational control.\n\nCybersecurity awareness is mentioned as an audit component (9)(7123)(c)(12)-(13), but that is security awareness and only for businesses subject to cybersecurity audits, not privacy awareness for a PIMS.\n\nCategory 13: The regulations require consumer-facing disclosures and communications to be clear and accessible (1)(7003) and define methods for submitting requests/consent (1)(7004), but ISO 7.4 requires the organization to determine internal and external communications relevant to the PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a management-system process. The CCPA does not require a communications plan covering internal reporting, regulator communications (beyond submissions in (9)(7124) and (10)(7157)), or communications with processors/controllers.\n\nThus, communication is mandated in specific consumer-rights contexts, not as a comprehensive PIMS communication process.\n\nCategory 14: The regulations require record-keeping for consumer requests (8)(7101) and retention of audit documents (9)(7122)(g) and risk assessments (10)(7155)(c), but ISO 7.5 requires documented information for the PIMS generally, including creation/update controls (identification, review/approval) and control of documented information (distribution, access, version control, retention/disposition). The CCPA does not impose a general documented-information control system for privacy management documentation.\n\nIt also does not require controlling external-origin documents necessary for PIMS planning/operation, nor does it define documentation requirements for many privacy processes beyond the specific logs/reports.\n\nCategory 15: The regulations impose operational controls for notices, requests, vendor contracts, audits, and risk assessments (2)-(10), but ISO 8.1 requires operational planning and control of PIMS processes with criteria, controlled changes, mitigation of unintended changes, and control of externally provided processes relevant to the PIMS. The CCPA has vendor contract controls (4) and some change-related requirements (10)(7155), but not a unified operational control framework for all PIMS processes.\n\nIn particular, there is no requirement to define process criteria and maintain documented information to provide confidence that PIMS processes were carried out as planned (beyond specific record-keeping in (8)(7101)).\n\nCategory 16: The regulations require risk assessments before initiating certain processing (10)(7150) and periodic updates (10)(7155), but ISO 8.2 requires privacy risk assessments at planned intervals and when significant changes occur, based on criteria established in planning (ISO 6.1.2), and retention of results. CCPA risk assessments are mandated for defined \u201csignificant risk\u201d activities, not for all processing within a PIMS scope, and do not require defined risk criteria (acceptance criteria, assessment criteria) as ISO expects.\n\nAlso, the CCPA risk assessment is a \u201crisks vs benefits\u201d balancing test (10)(7152)(a) rather than an ISO-style risk assessment method tied to a control selection/SoA approach.\n\nCategory 17: The regulations require safeguards to be identified in risk assessments (10)(7152)(a)(6) and require a plan to address gaps in cybersecurity audits (9)(7123)(e)(4), but ISO 8.3 requires implementing a privacy risk treatment plan and retaining documented information of treatment results. CCPA does not require a formal risk treatment plan with risk owner approval, residual risk acceptance, or tracking treatment effectiveness as a PIMS control.\n\nThe cybersecurity audit requires documenting a plan to address gaps (9)(7123)(e)(4), but it does not compel implementation, governance, and verification of completion as a risk treatment process across privacy risks (and applies only to certain businesses).\n\nCategory 18: The regulations require certain metrics for large businesses (8)(7102) and require audits and risk assessments, but ISO 9.1 requires determining what to monitor/measure, methods, timing, and analysis/evaluation of privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness. CCPA metrics are limited to consumer request statistics and response times (8)(7102), not broader privacy performance indicators (e.g., incident trends, DPIA completion rates, vendor compliance, training effectiveness).\n\nThere is also no requirement to evaluate the effectiveness of a PIMS (since a PIMS is not required), only compliance with discrete obligations.\n\nCategory 19: The regulations require \u201ccybersecurity audits\u201d for certain businesses (9) and allow Agency audits (13)(7304), but ISO 9.2 is about internal audits of the PIMS at planned intervals, with an audit program, objectives, criteria, scope, auditor selection for impartiality, and documented evidence. CCPA cybersecurity audits are not internal PIMS audits; they are security-program audits with prescribed scope and independence requirements (9)(7122)-(7123), and only for businesses meeting thresholds (9)(7120).\n\nThere is no requirement for an organization-wide internal audit program covering privacy management processes, nor for auditing against ISO 27701 requirements or the organization\u2019s own PIMS requirements.\n\nCategory 20: The regulations require executive review/approval of risk assessments (10)(7152)(a)(9) and delivery of cybersecurity audit reports to an executive responsible for cybersecurity (9)(7122)(f), but ISO 9.3 requires top management to conduct periodic management reviews of the PIMS with specified inputs/outputs and documented evidence. CCPA does not require management review meetings, review agendas, or decisions on PIMS changes and continual improvement.\n\nRisk assessment approval is processing-specific, and cybersecurity audit report receipt is security-specific; neither constitutes a management review of a privacy information management system.\n\nCategory 21: The regulations include some iterative elements (e.g., update risk assessments (10)(7155), evaluate verification methods annually if none exist (5)(7062)(g)), but ISO 10.1 requires continual improvement of the PIMS\u2019 suitability, adequacy, and effectiveness. CCPA does not require a continual improvement program, improvement objectives, or systematic improvement cycles for privacy management.\n\nWhere improvements are implied (e.g., record-keeping may be used to \u201creview and modify processes\u201d in (8)(7101)(d)), it is permissive and narrow, not a mandated continual improvement requirement.\n\nCategory 22: The regulations provide enforcement mechanisms and penalties (13) and require explanations for denials of consumer requests (3)(7022)(f), (3)(7023)(f), (3)(7024)(e), but ISO 10.2 is about nonconformity and corrective action within a PIMS: reacting, root-cause analysis, implementing corrective actions, reviewing effectiveness, and retaining documented information. CCPA does not require organizations to run a corrective action process for privacy compliance failures, nor to document nonconformities and corrective actions as a management-system practice.\n\nAgency enforcement and audits (13) are external accountability mechanisms, not an internal corrective action system aligned to ISO 27701."
