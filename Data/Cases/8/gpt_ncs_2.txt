"Category 0: Law 1581 defines roles (3)(d)-(e) and scope of applicability (2), but it does not require an organization to systematically determine and document internal/external issues affecting a PIMS outcome (e.g., governance, strategy, contractual requirements, relevant judicial/administrative decisions) as a management-system context exercise. It also does not require determining role per processing instance (controller vs processor per activity) or handling \u201cboth roles\u201d with separate control sets; it only defines roles and assigns duties (17)-(18) without a context-determination process.\n\nCategory 1: While it recognizes the data subject (\u201cTitular\u201d) and rights (8) and establishes a supervisory authority (19)-(21), it does not require an organization to identify all \u201cinterested parties\u201d relevant to a PIMS (e.g., customers, joint controllers, processors/subprocessors, partners) and determine/decide which of their requirements will be addressed through the PIMS. The law is rights-and-obligations focused, but lacks a structured stakeholder/expectations analysis and documentation requirement.\n\nCategory 2: The law applies broadly to processing in Colombia and certain extraterritorial cases (2), but it does not require an organization to define and maintain a documented PIMS scope statement (boundaries, applicability, interfaces, exclusions) as \u201cdocumented information.\u201d It also does not require considering internal/external issues and interested-party requirements as inputs to scope determination (as ISO does).\n\nCategory 3: Law 1581 imposes substantive privacy obligations (4), rights (8), and duties (17)-(18), but it does not require establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a formal privacy information management system with defined processes and interactions. There is no explicit PDCA-style management system requirement, nor requirements to integrate privacy processes into an overarching system.\n\nCategory 4: The law does not impose \u201ctop management\u201d leadership and commitment requirements (e.g., ensuring policy/objectives compatibility with strategic direction, integration into business processes, resource provision, promoting continual improvement). It assigns duties to \u201cResponsables/Encargados\u201d (17)-(18) but does not require executive accountability structures typical of ISO management system leadership.\n\nCategory 5: Although it requires internal policies/manuals (17)(k), (18)(f) and \u201cpolicies of information processing\u201d for database registration (25), it does not require a top-management-approved privacy policy with specific ISO elements (framework for objectives, commitment to continual improvement, availability as documented information, communication and availability to interested parties). The law\u2019s policy obligations are more compliance/manual oriented and tied to registration, not a management-system policy construct.\n\nCategory 6: Law 1581 defines controller/processor roles (3)(d)-(e) and assigns duties (17)-(18), but it does not require an organization to assign and communicate responsibilities/authorities for PIMS roles (including responsibility for PIMS conformance and reporting to top management). There is no requirement for a designated management-system role (e.g., privacy manager) or formal authority matrix beyond legal duties.\n\nCategory 7: The law includes principles like security and confidentiality (4)(g)-(h) and requires reporting security-code violations to the authority (17)(n), (18)(k), but it does not require a privacy risk assessment process with defined criteria, repeatability, statement of applicability, risk owners, risk treatment plan, and documented risk treatment process. It also does not require planning actions to address risks/opportunities for PIMS outcomes\u2014its approach is obligation-based rather than risk-management-system based.\n\nCategory 8: There is no requirement to set measurable privacy objectives, monitor them, communicate them, and plan resources/responsibilities/timelines to achieve them. The law mandates compliance duties (17)-(18) and rights handling (14)-(15), but does not require objective-setting and planning as a management practice.\n\nCategory 9: The law does not require planned management of changes to the PIMS (or to processing operations) in a structured way. While it requires keeping data updated/rectified (17)(f)-(g), (18)(c)-(d), that is about data accuracy, not planning and controlling changes to the privacy management system.\n\nCategory 10: The law does not require determining and providing resources for establishing/maintaining/improving a PIMS. It creates obligations and a regulator (19)-(21), but does not compel organizations to allocate resources, budget, tooling, or staffing for a management system.\n\nCategory 11: The law does not require competence management (identify needed competence, ensure training/experience, keep evidence). It mentions state/educational duties to educate about children\u2019s data risks (7), but that is not an organizational competence requirement for personnel processing PII.\n\nCategory 12: The law does not require awareness programs for personnel (awareness of policy, contribution to PIMS effectiveness, implications of nonconformity). It imposes confidentiality obligations (4)(h) and security principle (4)(g), but not structured awareness requirements.\n\nCategory 13: The law requires specific external communications to data subjects at collection/authorization (12) and responses to consultations/reclamos (14)-(15), and it contemplates reporting incidents to the authority (17)(n), (18)(k). However, it does not require an organization to determine and plan internal/external communications for the PIMS broadly (what/when/with whom/how), nor to manage communications as a system process beyond the enumerated notices and procedures.\n\nCategory 14: The law requires keeping proof of authorization (9), (12) and adopting internal manuals/policies (17)(k), (18)(f), and providing policies for registration (25), but it does not establish comprehensive documented-information controls (creation/update identification, review/approval, version control, retention/disposition, control of external documents) as required by ISO. It also does not require maintaining documented information for the effectiveness of a PIMS beyond specific evidentiary items.\n\nCategory 15: The law mandates operational duties (17)-(18) and procedures for consultations/reclamos (14)-(15), but it does not require operational planning and control of PIMS processes with defined criteria, controlled changes, and control of externally provided processes/services relevant to the PIMS. It also lacks explicit requirements for supplier/subcontractor control as a management-system operational control (beyond role definitions and general duty to \u201cexigir\u201d security/privacy from processors (17)(i)).\n\nCategory 16: There is no requirement to perform privacy risk assessments at planned intervals or upon significant changes, nor to retain documented results of such assessments. The law focuses on lawful bases/authorization (9)-(10), principles (4), and rights procedures (14)-(15), not periodic risk assessment.\n\nCategory 17: The law does not require implementing a documented privacy risk treatment plan or retaining results of risk treatment. It requires security measures (4)(g), safeguarding (17)(d), (18)(b), and incident reporting (17)(n), (18)(k), but not structured risk treatment planning and documentation.\n\nCategory 18: The law does not require monitoring/measurement/analysis/evaluation of privacy performance or PIMS effectiveness, nor documented evidence of such evaluation. It provides enforcement and sanctions (19)-(24) and requires compliance with instructions (17)(o), (18)(l), but that is external oversight rather than internal performance evaluation.\n\nCategory 19: The law does not require internal audits of a PIMS, audit programs, auditor objectivity, reporting, or retention of audit evidence. Oversight is performed by the authority (19)-(22), but ISO requires internal audit as an organizational control, which is not compelled by (17)-(18) or elsewhere.\n\nCategory 20: The law does not require top management to conduct periodic management reviews of the PIMS with specified inputs/outputs and documented evidence. Regulatory investigations and corrective orders (21)(b), (23)(b)-(c) are external mechanisms, not internal management review.\n\nCategory 21: The law does not require continual improvement of a PIMS; it provides a transition period to comply (28) and allows the authority to suggest adjustments to regulation (21)(i), but that is not an organizational continual improvement obligation. Organizations must comply, but are not required to continually improve system suitability/adequacy/effectiveness.\n\nCategory 22: The law provides complaint handling and enforcement (15)-(16), authority measures and sanctions (21)-(24), and requires adopting internal procedures/manuals (17)(k), (18)(f), but it does not require an internal nonconformity and corrective action process aligned to ISO (root-cause analysis, preventing recurrence, effectiveness review, and documented evidence of corrective actions). Correctives may be imposed by the authority (23)(b)-(c), yet the law does not compel a systematic internal CAPA process for the PIMS."
