"Category 0: Law 1581 defines roles (3)(d)-(e) and territorial scope (2), but it does not require an organization to systematically determine and document internal/external context issues affecting a PIMS (e.g., governance model, business strategy, contractual landscape) as a management-system activity. It also does not require determining role per processing instance (controller vs processor per activity) or handling joint controllership as a distinct governance determination (beyond definitional level in (3)(e)).\n\nCategory 1: The law recognizes the data subject (Titular) and the authority (19)-(21), and it regulates disclosures to certain recipients (13), but it does not require an organization to identify all \u201cinterested parties\u201d and their requirements (e.g., customers, suppliers/subprocessors, partners) and decide which requirements are addressed through a PIMS. There is no management-system requirement to capture expectations (including contractual expectations) and keep them under review.\n\nCategory 2: While the law states material scope (2) and defines \u201cTratamiento\u201d (3)(g), it does not require an organization to define and maintain a documented scope statement for a privacy information management system, including boundaries, interfaces, and applicability across sites, products, services, and outsourced processing. It also does not require considering context/party requirements to set that scope as ISO 27701 expects.\n\nCategory 3: Law 1581 is a rights-and-obligations statute, not a management system standard; it does not compel establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a PIMS with defined processes and interactions. Even where it requires internal manuals/policies (17)(k), (18)(f), it does not amount to a full PDCA management system requirement.\n\nCategory 4: The law imposes duties on controllers/processors (17), (18) but does not require \u201ctop management\u201d leadership, accountability, and integration of PIMS into business processes, nor ensuring resources and promoting continual improvement as explicit leadership obligations. There is no governance requirement comparable to ISO\u2019s leadership and commitment clause.\n\nCategory 5: The law requires informing the Titular at collection/authorization (12) and requires internal manuals/policies (17)(k), (18)(f) and policies for registration (25), but it does not require a top-management-approved privacy policy with a framework for objectives and a commitment to continual improvement. It also does not require making a policy available to interested parties in the ISO sense (beyond specific notices to Titulares in (12)).\n\nCategory 6: Law 1581 does not require defining and communicating organizational roles, responsibilities, and authorities for a PIMS (e.g., privacy officer/DPO-type responsibilities, reporting lines, independence). It defines \u201cResponsable\u201d and \u201cEncargado\u201d (3)(d)-(e) and assigns legal duties (17)-(18), but it does not require internal assignment of accountable roles for system conformance and performance reporting.\n\nCategory 7: The law includes a general security principle (4)(g) and breach notification to the authority (17)(n), (18)(k), but it does not require a formal privacy risk management framework: risk criteria, consistent assessment methodology, risk owners, statement of applicability, treatment plan, and residual risk acceptance. It also does not require integrating risk actions into PIMS processes or evaluating effectiveness as a planned activity.\n\nCategory 8: There is no requirement to set measurable privacy objectives, monitor them, communicate them internally, and plan resources/timelines to achieve them. The law focuses on compliance duties (17)-(18) and data subject rights (8), but does not impose an objectives-and-planning discipline.\n\nCategory 9: Law 1581 does not require planned management of changes to the PIMS or to processing that could affect privacy (e.g., change control, assessment before significant changes). It provides transition time to comply (28) but not an ongoing change-planning requirement.\n\nCategory 10: The law does not require determining and providing resources for a PIMS (budget, tools, staffing). It creates state resources for the authority (20) but not organizational resource planning for privacy management.\n\nCategory 11: There is no explicit requirement to determine competence needs, train personnel, and keep evidence of competence for privacy-related work. The only training-related element is directed to the State/educational entities regarding minors\u2019 risks (7), not organizational competence management for controllers/processors.\n\nCategory 12: The law does not require awareness programs ensuring personnel understand the privacy policy, their contribution, and consequences of nonconformity. Confidentiality obligations exist (4)(h), but that is not an awareness-management requirement.\n\nCategory 13: While the law mandates specific communications to Titulares (12), responses to consultations/reclamos (14)-(15), and reporting breaches to the authority (17)(n), (18)(k), it does not require an organization to determine and manage a comprehensive internal/external communications plan (what/when/with whom/how) for the PIMS. It also does not address internal communications governance beyond these specific legal interactions.\n\nCategory 14: The law requires keeping proof of authorization and proof of notice (9), (12) and having internal manuals/policies (17)(k), (18)(f), and submitting policies for database registration (25), but it does not establish a full documented-information control regime (versioning, approval, distribution, retention/disposition, protection) for all PIMS documents. It also does not require a documented \u201cscope,\u201d \u201cobjectives,\u201d \u201crisk assessment results,\u201d \u201caudit results,\u201d etc., as ISO 27701 does.\n\nCategory 15: The law imposes operational duties (17)-(18) and principles (4), but it does not require operational planning and control in the ISO sense (process criteria, controlled execution, documented confidence, control of planned changes, and control of externally provided processes as part of a PIMS). It also lacks explicit requirements for supplier/subcontractor operational controls beyond the controller/processor relationship implied by (3)(d)-(e) and duties like (17)(i).\n\nCategory 16: Law 1581 does not compel periodic privacy risk assessments or assessments triggered by significant changes, nor retention of documented results. Its approach is principle-based (4) and duty-based (17)-(18) without a mandated risk assessment cycle.\n\nCategory 17: The law does not require a documented privacy risk treatment plan, implementation tracking, or retention of treatment results. It requires security measures (4)(g), (17)(d), (18)(b) and authority notification (17)(n), (18)(k), but not structured risk treatment governance.\n\nCategory 18: There is no requirement to monitor, measure, analyze, and evaluate privacy performance or PIMS effectiveness with defined methods, timing, and documented evidence. The law provides enforcement and sanctions (21)-(24) but does not require internal performance evaluation.\n\nCategory 19: Law 1581 does not require internal audits of a PIMS, an audit program, auditor independence, reporting, or retention of audit evidence. Oversight is external via the authority (19)-(22), not internal audit.\n\nCategory 20: The law does not require top management to conduct periodic management reviews of the PIMS with specified inputs/outputs and documented results. External supervision exists (19)-(22), but management review is not mandated.\n\nCategory 21: While the law\u2019s structure implies ongoing compliance and includes sanctions and corrective orders (22)-(23), it does not require continual improvement of a PIMS as a management-system obligation. It lacks a PDCA improvement requirement; compliance is enforced through duties (17)-(18) and authority actions (21)-(23), not continuous improvement processes.\n\nCategory 22: The law provides for complaints, investigations, orders, and sanctions (15)-(16), (21)-(24), but it does not require an internal nonconformity and corrective action process (root cause analysis, preventing recurrence, effectiveness review, and documented evidence) within a PIMS. Correctives may be ordered by the authority (23)(b)-(c), yet the internal corrective-action lifecycle and documentation expected by ISO 27701 is not compelled."
