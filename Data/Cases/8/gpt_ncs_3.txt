"Category 0: Understanding the organization and its context (4.1)  \nLaw 1581 defines general applicability and roles (controller/processor equivalents) via \u201cResponsable\u201d and \u201cEncargado\u201d (3)(d)-(e) and territorial scope (2), but it does not require an organization to perform a structured \u201ccontext analysis\u201d of internal/external issues affecting a PIMS, nor to document and periodically review those issues. It also does not require separating roles per processing instance (joint controllership-like situations are not operationalized), beyond defining roles (3)(d)-(e).  \nAdditionally, ISO 27701:2025 explicitly asks to determine whether climate change is a relevant issue; Law 1581 contains no such requirement or any broader obligation to consider environmental/physical context as a management-system input (only a general security principle exists) (4)(g).\n\nCategory 1: Understanding the needs and expectations of interested parties (4.2)  \nLaw 1581 focuses mainly on the data subject (\u201cTitular\u201d) rights (8) and the supervisory authority (19)-(21), but it does not require the organization to identify all \u201cinterested parties\u201d (e.g., customers, partners, subcontractors, joint controllers, processors) and translate their requirements into PIMS requirements. The law is rights/obligations-based, not stakeholder-management-system-based.  \nIt also does not require determining which interested-party requirements will be addressed through the PIMS, nor does it mention expectations such as independent certification/audits to demonstrate conformity (a typical ISO expectation). Climate-change-related expectations of interested parties are also not contemplated (no equivalent provisions).\n\nCategory 2: Determining the scope of the PIMS (4.3)  \nLaw 1581 defines legal scope of application (2) but does not require each organization to define and document the boundaries/applicability of its own privacy management system, including interfaces, exclusions, and justification. ISO requires a documented scope statement; the law only sets when the law applies, not how an organization scopes its internal PIMS.  \nAlso, ISO expects scope determination to consider context (4.1) and interested parties (4.2); Law 1581 has no management-system mechanism linking those analyses to a defined PIMS scope.\n\nCategory 3: Privacy information management system (4.4)  \nLaw 1581 imposes substantive duties (17)-(18) and principles (4), but it does not require establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a formal PIMS with defined processes and interactions. The closest is the obligation to adopt an internal manual of policies and procedures (17)(k), (18)(f), but that is not equivalent to a full management system with process interaction, governance, and continual improvement cycles.  \nThere is no explicit requirement for a system approach (PDCA), performance evaluation, or integration with organizational processes as a coherent \u201cPIMS\u201d framework.\n\nCategory 4: Leadership and commitment (5.1)  \nLaw 1581 does not impose explicit top-management accountability for a PIMS, nor does it require leadership to align privacy policy/objectives with strategic direction, integrate PIMS into business processes, or ensure resources. Duties are assigned to \u201cResponsable/Encargado\u201d generally (17)-(18), but not to \u201ctop management\u201d with demonstrable commitment.  \nIt also lacks requirements for leadership-driven continual improvement and for directing/supporting personnel contributions to PIMS effectiveness (beyond general compliance duties).\n\nCategory 5: Privacy policy (5.2)  \nWhile the law requires \u201cpolicies of information processing\u201d to be provided for registration and to bind controllers/processors (25), it does not require top management to establish a privacy policy that includes a framework for objectives and a commitment to continual improvement. The law\u2019s policy concept is compliance-oriented (\u201ccannot be lower than duties\u201d) (25), not a management policy with measurable objectives and improvement commitments.  \nIt also does not require internal communication of the policy or making it available to interested parties \u201cas appropriate\u201d in the ISO sense (beyond what may be implied by transparency/notice duties (12) and registry-related publication (25)).\n\nCategory 6: Roles, responsibilities and authorities (5.3)  \nLaw 1581 defines roles of \u201cResponsable\u201d and \u201cEncargado\u201d (3)(d)-(e) and lists their duties (17)-(18), but it does not require an organization to assign and communicate internal responsibilities/authorities for PIMS conformance and reporting to top management. ISO expects explicit governance roles (e.g., PIMS manager, reporting lines).  \nThe law also does not require role assignment for specific PIMS processes (risk assessment, audits, management review), because those processes are not mandated as such.\n\nCategory 7: Actions to address risks and opportunities / privacy risk assessment process (6.1.1\u20136.1.2)  \nLaw 1581 includes a general security principle requiring technical/human/administrative measures (4)(g) and breach reporting to the authority (17)(n), (18)(k), but it does not require a formal risk-and-opportunity planning process for a PIMS. There is no obligation to define risk criteria, risk acceptance criteria, or to ensure repeatable, comparable risk assessments.  \nISO\u2019s requirements for a statement of applicability, control selection/justification, risk treatment plan approval by risk owners, and documented risk treatment processes are not present. The law is outcome/duty-based, not risk-management-system-based.\n\nCategory 8: Privacy objectives and planning to achieve them (6.2)  \nLaw 1581 does not require organizations to set measurable privacy objectives, monitor them, communicate them internally, update them, or document plans (what/resources/who/when/how evaluated). It mandates compliance duties (17)-(18) and principles (4), but not objective-setting and planning.  \nThe closest element\u2014internal manuals/policies (17)(k), (18)(f)\u2014does not equate to objectives with metrics and evaluation methods.\n\nCategory 9: Planning of changes (6.3)  \nLaw 1581 does not require planned management of changes to a PIMS (or to privacy controls) when changes are needed. It provides no change-control expectations (e.g., assessing impacts of changes in processing, technology, vendors).  \nAny change planning would be inferred from general compliance, but ISO requires explicit planned change management; the law is silent.\n\nCategory 10: Resources (7.1)  \nLaw 1581 does not require organizations to determine and provide resources for establishing/maintaining/improving a PIMS. It only sets duties to protect data and comply (17)-(18) without specifying resource planning.  \nResource requirements are only mentioned for the authority (20), not for regulated organizations.\n\nCategory 11: Competence (7.2)  \nLaw 1581 does not require determining competence needs, ensuring training/education/experience, or keeping documented evidence of competence for personnel affecting privacy performance.  \nThe law\u2019s approach is obligation-based (do X), not capability-based (ensure competent people and evidence).\n\nCategory 12: Awareness (7.3)  \nLaw 1581 does not require awareness programs so that personnel understand the privacy policy, their contribution to PIMS effectiveness, and implications of nonconformity.  \nIt imposes confidentiality and security duties (4)(g)-(h), but does not mandate internal awareness management as ISO does.\n\nCategory 13: Communication (7.4)  \nLaw 1581 mandates certain communications to data subjects (12) and enables complaints to the authority (8)(d), but it does not require an organization to plan and determine internal/external communications for the PIMS (what/when/with whom/how).  \nISO\u2019s structured communication planning (including internal communications and communications with processors/subprocessors/customers) is not required by the law.\n\nCategory 14: Documented information (7.5)  \nLaw 1581 requires keeping proof of consent (9; 17)(b) and proof of notice compliance (12 Par\u00e1grafo), and requires internal manuals/policies (17)(k), (18)(f), plus policies submitted to the registry (25). However, it does not require a comprehensive documented-information control system: document identification, versioning, approval, controlled distribution, retention/disposition rules for PIMS documentation, or control of external documents.  \nISO\u2019s document-control lifecycle (7.5.2\u20137.5.3) is largely absent; the law only requires certain records, not a controlled documentation framework.\n\nCategory 15: Operational planning and control (8.1)  \nLaw 1581 sets operational duties (17)-(18) but does not require process criteria, controlled execution against criteria, documented operational controls, or systematic control of planned/unintended changes.  \nIt also does not explicitly require controlling externally provided processes/products/services relevant to PIMS (e.g., vendor management, subcontractor controls), beyond general restrictions on access/circulation (4)(f) and the controller\u2019s duty to \u201cdemand\u201d security/privacy from processors (17)(i), which is not equivalent to ISO\u2019s operational control framework.\n\nCategory 16: Privacy risk assessment (8.2)  \nLaw 1581 does not require performing privacy risk assessments at planned intervals or upon significant changes, nor retaining documented results of such assessments.  \nSecurity obligations (4)(g) and breach reporting (17)(n), (18)(k) do not substitute for periodic, criteria-based risk assessments.\n\nCategory 17: Privacy risk treatment (8.3)  \nLaw 1581 does not require implementing a documented privacy risk treatment plan, nor retaining evidence of treatment results. It requires security measures (4)(g) and compliance with principles/duties, but not a structured risk treatment lifecycle.  \nThere is also no requirement for residual risk acceptance by risk owners or for tracking treatment effectiveness as a formal process.\n\nCategory 18: Monitoring, measurement, analysis and evaluation (9.1)  \nLaw 1581 does not require organizations to define what to monitor/measure, methods, frequency, analysis, and evaluation of privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness, nor to keep documented evidence of monitoring results.  \nThe law provides enforcement mechanisms via the authority (19)-(23), but that is external oversight, not internal performance evaluation.\n\nCategory 19: Internal audit (9.2)  \nLaw 1581 does not require internal audits of a PIMS, audit programs, audit criteria/scope, auditor independence, reporting, or audit records.  \nCompliance may be checked by the SIC through investigations (21)(b), but ISO requires proactive internal auditing; the law is silent.\n\nCategory 20: Management review (9.3)  \nLaw 1581 does not require top management to periodically review the PIMS for suitability/adequacy/effectiveness, nor to consider specified inputs (audit results, performance trends, changes in context/requirements) and produce documented outputs/decisions.  \nThe law\u2019s governance model relies on compliance duties and external supervision (19)-(21), not internal management review cycles.\n\nCategory 21: Continual improvement (10.1)  \nLaw 1581 does not impose a continual improvement obligation for a PIMS. It requires compliance with principles and duties (4), (17)-(18), but not systematic improvement of the management system over time.  \nEven though the SIC can recommend adjustments to norms (21)(i), that is regulatory evolution, not an organizational continual improvement requirement.\n\nCategory 22: Nonconformity and corrective action (10.2)  \nLaw 1581 provides sanctions and corrective measures imposed by the authority (21)(b), (23), but it does not require organizations to run an internal nonconformity/corrective action process: reacting, root-cause analysis, preventing recurrence, reviewing effectiveness, and keeping documented evidence.  \nIn other words, the law is enforcement-driven (external) rather than management-system-driven (internal CAPA), so ISO\u2019s corrective-action discipline is not covered."
