"## Category 0 \u2014 Understanding the organization and its context (4.1)\n**Items:** (1),(2),(3)(XXII),(3)(XXV),(3)(XXVII),(4),(8),(9),(11),(12),(16)(I),(57),(62),(74)  \n**Why:** These provisions define applicability and legal context ((1),(4)), require interpretation aligned with Constitution/treaties/jurisprudence ((8)) and supplementary law ((9)), and clarify roles akin to controller/processor via \u201cResponsable\u201d and \u201cPersona Encargada\u201d ((3)(XXV),(3)(XXII), plus (52)-(54)). They also anchor processing to legal powers and purposes ((11),(12)), identify when law itself authorizes processing without consent ((16)(I)), and address external context such as cloud services and cross-border processing constraints ((57),(62)) and special security/justice contexts ((74)).\n\n## Category 1 \u2014 Understanding the needs and expectations of interested parties (4.2)\n**Items:** (2)(I),(2)(III),(2)(VI),(6),(20),(21),(22),(23),(24)(VI),(34),(35),(37)-(51),(79),(80),(81)(I),(81)(IX),(83)(VI),(84),(85)  \n**Why:** The law centers the \u201cPersona Titular\u201d and their expectations through rights and accessible procedures ((2)(III),(37)-(51)), transparency via privacy notices ((20)-(22)), complaint/queries handling ((24)(VI)), breach notification to affected individuals and authorities ((34),(35)), and accessibility for priority groups ((80),(81)(IX),(83)(VI)). It also frames oversight bodies as interested parties (Secretar\u00eda/Autoridades garantes) with whom accountability and reporting are expected ((23),(81)(I)).\n\n## Category 2 \u2014 Determining the scope of the PIMS (4.3)\n**Items:** (1),(2),(3)(IX),(3)(XXVII),(4),(10),(31)-(36),(52)-(65)  \n**Why:** Scope is driven by the law\u2019s applicability to any processing in physical/electronic media ((4)) by obligated subjects ((3)(XXVII)) and by the definition of personal data ((3)(IX)). It implicitly requires including processing operations, security, incidents, and third-party processing/communications within scope ((10),(31)-(36),(52)-(65)).\n\n## Category 3 \u2014 Privacy information management system (4.4)\n**Items:** (23),(24),(28),(29),(30)  \n**Why:** These articles collectively require an organized, documented, and maintainable management approach: accountability mechanisms ((23),(24)), a \u201csistema de gesti\u00f3n\u201d for security and processing ((28)), a security document with required contents ((29)), and updating it upon changes/incidents/continuous improvement ((30)).\n\n## Category 4 \u2014 Leadership and commitment (5.1)\n**Items:** (24)(I),(24)(II),(24)(IV),(77),(78)(I),(78)(V),(84)  \n**Why:** Leadership commitment is reflected in mandated allocation of resources and internal policies ((24)(I)-(II)), periodic review of security programs ((24)(IV)), and governance structures where the Comit\u00e9 de Transparencia is the top authority for personal data protection ((77)) with coordination/supervision duties ((78)(I),(78)(V)). Ongoing institutional commitment is reinforced by mandatory collaboration for training ((84)).\n\n## Category 5 \u2014 Privacy policy (5.2)\n**Items:** (24)(II),(24)(VII),(24)(VIII),(58)(I)(a)  \n**Why:** The law requires internal \u201cpolicies and programs\u201d for data protection ((24)(II)) and privacy-by-design/default in public systems and technologies ((24)(VII)-(VIII)). For cloud services, it requires providers to have and apply privacy policies aligned with the law\u2019s principles and duties ((58)(I)(a)), reinforcing policy expectations.\n\n## Category 6 \u2014 Roles, responsibilities and authorities (5.3)\n**Items:** (3)(I),(3)(VI),(3)(XXII),(3)(XXV),(3)(XXXII),(24)(III),(27)(II),(52)-(56),(77)-(79),(81),(83)  \n**Why:** The framework defines organizational actors and assigns responsibilities: internal areas handling data ((3)(I)), controller/processor-like roles ((3)(XXV),(3)(XXII) and (52)-(56)), and governance bodies (Comit\u00e9/Unidad) with explicit functions ((77)-(79)). It also requires defining staff functions/obligations ((27)(II)) and training programs ((24)(III)).\n\n## Category 7 \u2014 Actions to address risks and opportunities (6.1)\n**Items:** (23),(24),(26),(27),(28),(29),(30),(31),(66)-(70)  \n**Why:** Risk-based planning is explicit in security measures considering risk, sensitivity, technology, consequences, transfers, scale, and prior incidents ((26)). It mandates risk analysis, gap analysis, work plans, monitoring, and role-based training ((27)), all documented within a management system and security document ((28),(29)), updated after changes/incidents and corrective actions ((30),(31)). \u201cBest practices\u201d schemes and criteria for intensive/relevant processing also support proactive improvement opportunities ((66)-(70)).\n\n## Category 8 \u2014 Privacy objectives and planning to achieve them (6.2)\n**Items:** (2)(VII),(24)(I)-(V),(66)  \n**Why:** While the law does not use \u201cobjectives\u201d language, it requires programs/policies, resources, training, periodic review, and audits to achieve compliance outcomes ((24)(I)-(V)). It also promotes a culture of data protection ((2)(VII)) and allows best-practice schemes to raise protection levels and demonstrate compliance ((66)).\n\n## Category 9 \u2014 Planning of changes (6.3)\n**Items:** (12) (second paragraph), (17) (retention/suppression), (30),(68),(71)-(73)  \n**Why:** Change planning is covered through requirements to update the security document when substantial processing changes alter risk ((30)(I)), and through DPIA obligations before deploying/modifying systems involving intensive/relevant processing ((68),(71)). Purpose changes require legal powers and (generally) consent ((12)), and lifecycle/retention changes are governed by suppression/blocking rules ((17)).\n\n## Category 10 \u2014 Resources (7.1)\n**Items:** (24)(I),(78)(I),(81)(XI)  \n**Why:** The law explicitly requires allocating authorized resources for data protection programs and policies ((24)(I)). Governance bodies must coordinate actions to guarantee the right ((78)(I)), and the Secretar\u00eda provides technical support to controllers to meet obligations ((81)(XI)), reflecting resourcing expectations.\n\n## Category 11 \u2014 Competence (7.2)\n**Items:** (24)(III),(27)(VIII),(29)(VII),(78)(VII),(84),(81)(XXIII),(83)(XIII)  \n**Why:** Competence is addressed through mandatory training and updating programs ((24)(III)), differentiated training by roles/responsibilities ((27)(VIII)), inclusion of training in the security document ((29)(VII)), and Comit\u00e9/authorities\u2019 roles in establishing and promoting training ((78)(VII),(84),(81)(XXIII),(83)(XIII)).\n\n## Category 12 \u2014 Awareness (7.3)\n**Items:** (2)(VII),(24)(III),(85)  \n**Why:** Awareness is promoted by requiring a culture of personal data protection ((2)(VII)), internal training/updates ((24)(III)), and broader educational and social promotion measures by authorities ((85)).\n\n## Category 13 \u2014 Communication (7.4)\n**Items:** (20)-(22),(34),(35),(63),(58)(II)(a),(86),(90),(106)  \n**Why:** Communication duties include providing privacy notices and updates ((20)-(22)), breach notifications to individuals and authorities with required content ((34),(35)), communicating the applicable privacy notice to data recipients in transfers ((63)), cloud-provider change notifications about privacy policies/terms ((58)(II)(a)), and formal communications/notifications in review procedures and publication of decisions ((86),(90),(106)).\n\n## Category 14 \u2014 Documented information (7.5)\n**Items:** (18),(23),(28),(29),(30),(33),(53),(60),(67),(68),(98),(99),(103),(106),(117)-(120)  \n**Why:** The law requires documented procedures for retention/suppression ((18)), accountability evidence ((23)), a documented management system and security document ((28),(29)) with update triggers ((30)), incident logs ((33)), contracts/instruments for processor and transfers ((53),(60)), notification/registration of best practices ((67)), DPIA documentation and submission ((68)), written conciliation agreements and resolutions/public versions ((98),(99),(103),(106)), and documented verification/audit processes and reports ((117)-(120)).\n\n## Category 15 \u2014 Operational planning and control (8.1)\n**Items:** (10)-(13),(17)-(19),(24)(VII)-(VIII),(25)-(27),(52)-(58),(59)-(65),(74)-(76)  \n**Why:** Operational control is established through processing principles and lawful purpose constraints ((10)-(13)), data minimization and lifecycle controls ((17)-(19)), privacy-by-design/default for systems ((24)(VII)-(VIII)), security controls and required activities ((25)-(27)), controls over processors/subprocessors and cloud services ((52)-(58)), and rules for transfers/remissions including cross-border conditions ((59)-(65)). Special operational constraints apply to security/justice databases ((74)-(76)).\n\n## Category 16 \u2014 Privacy risk assessment (8.2)\n**Items:** (26),(27)(IV),(27)(V),(29)(III),(29)(IV),(68)-(70)  \n**Why:** Risk assessment is explicitly required via considering inherent risk and consequences ((26)) and performing risk analysis and gap analysis ((27)(IV)-(V)), which must be reflected in the security document ((29)(III)-(IV)). DPIA requirements for intensive/relevant processing are a form of privacy risk assessment focused on impacts to rights and principles ((68)-(70)).\n\n## Category 17 \u2014 Privacy risk treatment (8.3)\n**Items:** (27)(VI),(29)(V),(31),(34),(35),(76),(118) (measures cautelares)  \n**Why:** Treatment is addressed through mandated work plans to implement missing controls and daily compliance measures ((27)(VI)) and inclusion of that plan in the security document ((29)(V)). After incidents, the controller must analyze causes and implement preventive/corrective actions ((31)), notify affected parties ((34),(35)), and apply higher security levels in sensitive security/justice contexts ((76)). Authorities may impose corrective precautionary measures during verification ((118)).\n\n## Category 18 \u2014 Monitoring, measurement, analysis and evaluation (9.1)\n**Items:** (24)(IV),(24)(V),(27)(VII),(29)(VI),(30)(II),(33),(79)(VI),(81)(XXII),(83)(XII),(120)  \n**Why:** The law requires periodic review of security policies/programs ((24)(IV)), supervision/audits ((24)(V)), monitoring and periodic review of controls and threats/incidents ((27)(VII)) documented in monitoring mechanisms ((29)(VI)), and continuous-improvement-driven updates ((30)(II)). Incident logs support measurement and analysis ((33)). Quality evaluation instruments for ARCO handling ((79)(VI)) and authority indicators/criteria for performance evaluation ((81)(XXII),(83)(XII)) further align with monitoring and evaluation; voluntary audits produce evaluative reports ((120)).\n\n## Category 19 \u2014 Internal audit (9.2)\n**Items:** (24)(V),(120)  \n**Why:** Internal audit is covered by the requirement to establish internal and/or external supervision systems including audits to verify compliance ((24)(V)). Additionally, the law provides for voluntary audits by authorities to assess adequacy and effectiveness of controls and recommend corrective actions ((120)).\n\n## Category 20 \u2014 Management review (9.3)\n**Items:** (24)(IV),(27)(VII),(30)(II),(78)(I),(78)(V)  \n**Why:** Management review is reflected in periodic review of security policies/programs ((24)(IV)) and ongoing monitoring/review of measures and threats ((27)(VII)), feeding improvement updates to the security document/system ((30)(II)). The Comit\u00e9 de Transparencia\u2019s coordinating/supervisory role and oversight of security document controls function as a management-level review mechanism ((78)(I),(78)(V)).\n\n## Category 21 \u2014 Continual improvement (10.1)\n**Items:** (23),(24)(IV),(27)(VII),(30)(II),(66)  \n**Why:** Continual improvement is embedded through accountability and use of standards/best practices ((23)), periodic review of programs ((24)(IV)), continuous monitoring and review ((27)(VII)), explicit updates due to \u201cmejora continua\u201d ((30)(II)), and optional best-practice schemes aimed at raising protection levels and demonstrating compliance ((66)).\n\n## Category 22 \u2014 Nonconformity and corrective action (10.2)\n**Items:** (30)(III),(30)(IV),(31),(33),(34),(35),(115),(118),(119),(120),(122)-(123),(132),(136)  \n**Why:** The law requires corrective/preventive actions after security breaches and to avoid recurrence ((31)), and mandates updating documentation due to breach mitigation and corrective/preventive actions ((30)(III)-(IV)). It requires logging incidents ((33)) and notifying affected parties and authorities ((34),(35)). Regulatory verification and resulting corrective measures ((115),(118),(119)) plus audits with corrective recommendations ((120)) align with corrective action processes, while enforcement mechanisms and sanctions for noncompliance ((122)-(123),(132),(136)) reinforce handling of nonconformities."
