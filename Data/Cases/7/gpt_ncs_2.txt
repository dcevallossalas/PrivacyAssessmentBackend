"Category 0: The law defines roles \u201cResponsable\u201d and \u201cPersona Encargada\u201d ((3)(XXV), (3)(XXII)) but it does not require an organization to formally determine, per processing activity, whether it acts as controller, processor, or joint controller, nor to separate roles where it acts in both capacities (ISO 27701/27001 context determination). It also does not require a structured determination of internal/external issues affecting the PIMS outcome (e.g., governance, contracts, administrative decisions, judicial decisions) as a management-system input; it mainly sets legal obligations and principles ((1), (2), (8)).\n\nIt also contains no requirement to consider climate change as a relevant issue for the management system context (explicitly present in ISO/IEC 27701:2025 clause 4.1), and no equivalent \u201ccontext analysis\u201d deliverable.\n\nCategory 1: The law is centered on data subjects (\u201cPersona Titular\u201d) and authorities (Secretar\u00eda, Autoridades garantes) and provides mechanisms for ARCO rights and complaints ((37)-(51), (81)-(83), (115)-(120)). However, it does not require the organization to identify and document all \u201cinterested parties\u201d relevant to a PIMS (e.g., customers, subcontractors, other controllers/processors) and their requirements, nor to decide which requirements will be addressed through the PIMS (ISO clause 4.2).\n\nWhile it references accountability and reporting to authorities and data subjects ((23)), it does not establish a systematic stakeholder-requirements process (including contractual expectations) as a management-system requirement.\n\nCategory 2: The law applies broadly to processing by \u201csujetos obligados\u201d ((1), (4), (3)(XXVII)) but it does not require each organization to define and maintain a documented scope statement for a privacy information management system (PIMS) including boundaries, interfaces, and applicability (ISO clause 4.3). There is no obligation to consider internal/external issues and interested-party requirements as inputs to scope definition.\n\nIn practice, the law sets the legal scope of applicability, but not the organization-specific PIMS scope documentation and maintenance expected by ISO/IEC 27701.\n\nCategory 3: The law requires a \u201csistema de gesti\u00f3n\u201d for security measures documentation and improvement ((28)), but it does not explicitly require establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a full PIMS with defined processes and interactions aligned to ISO clause 4.4. The \u201csistema de gesti\u00f3n\u201d is framed around security measures rather than the broader PIMS governance model (policy, objectives, performance evaluation, internal audit, management review, etc.).\n\nAlso, it does not require process interaction mapping, nor a PDCA-style management system across all privacy controls beyond security.\n\nCategory 4: The law creates governance bodies (Comit\u00e9 de Transparencia, Unidad de Transparencia) and assigns them functions ((77)-(79)), but it does not impose ISO-style \u201ctop management\u201d leadership and commitment requirements (integration into business processes, ensuring PIMS achieves intended results, promoting continual improvement) as explicit obligations (ISO clause 5.1). The governance is more compliance/oversight oriented than leadership-driven management system accountability.\n\nIt also does not require top management to ensure resources and communicate the importance of the PIMS in the ISO sense, beyond general accountability mechanisms ((23)-(24)).\n\nCategory 5: The law mandates an \u201caviso de privacidad\u201d content and communication ((20)-(22)), but that is not equivalent to an internal \u201cprivacy policy\u201d meeting ISO clause 5.2 (policy framework for objectives, commitment to meet requirements, commitment to continual improvement, availability as documented information, internal communication, availability to interested parties). The notice is an external transparency instrument to data subjects, not a management policy.\n\nAlthough it requires internal policies/programs for data protection ((24)(II), (27)(I)), it does not require a single overarching privacy policy with ISO-specified elements and lifecycle.\n\nCategory 6: The law defines functions for Comit\u00e9/Unidad and requires defining staff functions/obligations in security documentation ((27)(II), (29)(II), (78), (79)), but it does not require assignment of PIMS roles/responsibilities/authorities in the ISO clause 5.3 sense (including responsibility for ensuring PIMS conformity and reporting PIMS performance to top management). The governance bodies are mandated, yet the ISO \u201cmanagement representative\u201d style accountability and reporting lines are not explicitly required.\n\nIt also does not require communication of assigned authorities across the organization as a management-system control, beyond the functional descriptions.\n\nCategory 7: The law requires risk analysis for security measures ((27)(IV), (26)) and impact assessments for intensive/relevant processing ((68)-(72)), but it does not require a unified \u201crisks and opportunities\u201d planning process for the PIMS (ISO 6.1.1), including planning actions, integration into processes, and evaluation of effectiveness. The risk approach is focused on security and DPIA-like assessments, not on management-system risks/opportunities.\n\nIt also does not require establishing privacy risk criteria (risk acceptance criteria, assessment criteria), producing a statement of applicability, appointing risk owners, or formal risk treatment plan approval/acceptance of residual risk as ISO 6.1.2 expects.\n\nCategory 8: The law requires policies/programs and resources ((24)(I)-(II)) but does not require measurable privacy objectives at relevant functions/levels, monitoring, communication, updating, and documented planning to achieve them (ISO clause 6.2). There is no equivalent requirement to set objectives, define responsible persons, timelines, resources, and evaluation methods.\n\nThe framework is compliance-based (principles, duties, rights) rather than objective-driven performance management.\n\nCategory 9: The law requires updating the security document upon substantial changes and corrective actions ((30)), but it does not require planned management of changes to the PIMS (ISO clause 6.3) as a general requirement. The change planning is limited to the \u201cdocumento de seguridad\u201d and security measures, not the full privacy management system.\n\nIt also does not require assessing consequences of changes across PIMS processes and controls beyond security documentation updates.\n\nCategory 10: The law requires allocating resources for programs/policies ((24)(I)) but does not require determining and providing resources for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a PIMS (ISO clause 7.1) in a systematic way. Resource allocation is stated as a mechanism, not as a management-system requirement with ongoing adequacy evaluation.\n\nIt also does not cover broader resource categories (e.g., tools, monitoring, audit capability) as ISO expects.\n\nCategory 11: The law requires training/capacitation programs ((24)(III), (27)(VIII), (78)(VII), (84)) but it does not require determining competence requirements for roles affecting privacy performance, ensuring competence based on education/training/experience, taking actions to acquire competence, and retaining documented evidence of competence (ISO clause 7.2). The obligations are programmatic, not competence-management with evidence.\n\nIt also does not require evaluating effectiveness of competence actions in the ISO sense.\n\nCategory 12: The law promotes a culture of data protection and training ((2)(VII), (24)(III), (84)-(85)), but it does not require awareness of the privacy policy, individual contribution to PIMS effectiveness, and implications of nonconformity (ISO clause 7.3). Awareness is implied through training, yet not specified as an awareness control with defined content and coverage.\n\nThere is also no requirement to ensure awareness for all persons \u201cdoing work under the organization\u2019s control\u201d (including contractors) as ISO frames it.\n\nCategory 13: The law contains extensive communication duties to data subjects (privacy notice, breach notification) ((20)-(22), (34)-(35)) and to authorities (breach notification, verification cooperation) ((34), (115)). However, it does not require the organization to determine internal and external communications relevant to the PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a planned communication process (ISO clause 7.4). Communications are mandated in specific scenarios, not managed as a system.\n\nIt also does not require documenting or controlling the communication plan as part of PIMS operations.\n\nCategory 14: The law requires documentation of security measures in a \u201cdocumento de seguridad\u201d and documentation within a \u201csistema de gesti\u00f3n\u201d ((28)-(30)), plus breach logs ((33)). But it does not establish ISO-style documented information controls for the entire PIMS (creation/update controls, approval, versioning, distribution/access, retention/disposition, control of external documents) (ISO clause 7.5). The documentation requirements are narrower and do not cover all PIMS documented information.\n\nIt also does not require ensuring documented information is \u201cavailable and suitable\u201d where needed and \u201cadequately protected\u201d as a general PIMS documentation control, beyond security-document specifics.\n\nCategory 15: The law requires implementing security measures and internal policies, and controlling processors via contracts ((25)-(27), (53), (60)), but it does not require operational planning and control of PIMS processes with defined criteria, controlled changes, and documented information to ensure processes are carried out as planned (ISO clause 8.1). The law mandates outcomes and some mechanisms, not process criteria and operational control across all PIMS processes.\n\nWhile it addresses externally provided services (cloud) ((57)-(58)), it does not require a general control framework for all externally provided processes/products/services relevant to the PIMS.\n\nCategory 16: The law requires risk analysis for security measures ((27)(IV)) and DPIA-like evaluations for intensive/relevant processing ((68)-(72)), but it does not require performing privacy risk assessments at planned intervals or upon significant changes, based on defined criteria, and retaining documented results as an ongoing PIMS requirement (ISO clause 8.2). DPIAs are triggered by certain projects and can be waived in emergencies ((73)), which diverges from ISO\u2019s systematic periodicity.\n\nIt also does not require a consistent, repeatable privacy risk assessment methodology producing comparable results across time and scope.\n\nCategory 17: The law requires a plan of work to implement missing security measures ((27)(VI), (29)(V)) and corrective/preventive actions after incidents ((31)), but it does not require implementing a formal privacy risk treatment plan derived from privacy risk assessment, nor retaining documented information of treatment results (ISO clause 8.3). Treatment is framed as security gap remediation rather than privacy risk treatment across all privacy controls.\n\nIt also lacks ISO elements like residual risk acceptance and treatment effectiveness evaluation as part of the treatment process.\n\nCategory 18: The law requires monitoring and periodic review of security measures and threats ((27)(VII), (29)(VI)) and uses indicators by the authority to evaluate compliance ((81)(XXII), (83)(XII)). However, it does not require the organization to define what to monitor/measure, methods, timing, analysis/evaluation, and to evaluate privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness with documented evidence (ISO clause 9.1). Monitoring is security-centric and authority-driven rather than organization-managed performance evaluation.\n\nIt also does not require a structured privacy performance evaluation program covering all PIMS processes (e.g., rights handling performance, processor oversight performance) beyond specific legal timelines.\n\nCategory 19: The law mentions internal/external supervision and audits as an accountability mechanism ((24)(V)) and allows voluntary audits by authorities ((120)), but it does not require the organization to conduct internal audits at planned intervals to assess conformity with its own PIMS requirements and ISO requirements, nor to establish an audit program, define criteria/scope, ensure auditor impartiality, and retain documented audit evidence (ISO clause 9.2). Audits are optional/unspecified rather than mandatory internal audit governance.\n\nAlso, \u201cauditor independence\u201d and audit program management are not addressed.\n\nCategory 20: The law establishes oversight bodies (Comit\u00e9 de Transparencia) ((77)-(78)) but it does not require top management to perform management reviews at planned intervals with specified inputs/outputs and documented evidence (ISO clause 9.3). Committee functions include supervision and follow-up, but not a formal management review cycle evaluating suitability/adequacy/effectiveness of the PIMS.\n\nIt also does not require management review decisions on improvement opportunities and needed changes to the PIMS as a documented output.\n\nCategory 21: The law references \u201cmejora continua\u201d in updating the security document ((30)(II)) and requires periodic review of policies/programs ((24)(IV)) and monitoring/review of measures ((27)(VII)). However, it does not require continual improvement of the overall PIMS (ISO clause 10.1) as a system-wide obligation; improvement is mainly tied to security measures and incident response.\n\nThere is no explicit requirement to continually improve suitability/adequacy/effectiveness of a comprehensive PIMS beyond security governance.\n\nCategory 22: The law requires corrective and preventive actions after security incidents ((31)), updating the security document due to corrective/preventive actions ((30)(IV)), and keeping a breach log with corrective actions ((33)). But it does not establish a general nonconformity and corrective action process for the PIMS (react, correct, deal with consequences, root cause analysis, prevent recurrence, review effectiveness, make system changes, retain documented evidence) (ISO clause 10.2). The corrective-action logic is incident-focused, not nonconformity-focused across all PIMS requirements.\n\nIt also does not require capturing nonconformities unrelated to security incidents (e.g., failures in ARCO handling, notice deficiencies) into a unified corrective action system with documented evidence and effectiveness review."
