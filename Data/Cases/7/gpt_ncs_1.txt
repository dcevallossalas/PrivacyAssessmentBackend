"Category 0: The law defines roles (e.g., \u201cResponsable\u201d and \u201cPersona Encargada\u201d) (3)(XXII)(XXV) and sets general applicability (1)(4), but it does not require an organization to formally determine and document internal/external issues affecting a PIMS (e.g., governance context, contractual requirements as \u201ccontext,\u201d or systematic consideration of judicial/administrative decisions as inputs to the management system). It also does not require determining, per processing activity, whether the entity is acting as controller vs processor (or both) as a contextual analysis step; it mostly assumes \u201csujeto obligado\u201d as \u201cresponsable\u201d and regulates \u201cencargada\u201d via contracting (52)-(58).\n\nIt also does not include the ISO/IEC 27701:2025 explicit requirement to determine whether climate change is a relevant issue for the management system context (no equivalent in (1)-(9) or elsewhere).\n\nCategory 1: While the law is strongly rights-based and recognizes the \u201cPersona Titular\u201d and oversight bodies (3)(II)(XXIX), and provides mechanisms for complaints, review, and verification (86)-(120), it does not require the organization to identify all \u201cinterested parties\u201d and their requirements as an explicit management-system input (e.g., customers, subcontractors, other controllers/processors) beyond the legal relationships it regulates.\n\nIt also lacks a requirement to decide which interested-party requirements will be addressed through a PIMS (a planning/scoping decision). The law mandates compliance with legal duties, but not a structured stakeholder-requirements determination process.\n\nCategory 2: The law applies broadly to processing by subjects obligated (1)(4), but it does not require defining and maintaining a documented scope statement for a privacy information management system (PIMS) (i.e., boundaries, applicability, interfaces, exclusions) as \u201cdocumented information.\u201d It regulates processing generally rather than requiring a scoped management system artifact.\n\nAdditionally, it does not require considering internal/external issues and interested-party requirements (as in ISO clauses 4.1/4.2) when setting scope; those management-system linkages are not mandated.\n\nCategory 3: The law requires a \u201csistema de gesti\u00f3n\u201d for security actions to be documented (28) and a \u201cdocumento de seguridad\u201d (29), but it does not require establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a full PIMS with defined processes and interactions across all privacy controls (beyond security and certain accountability mechanisms). The \u201csistema de gesti\u00f3n\u201d in (28) is framed around treatment and security measures, not the full ISO 27701 management system model.\n\nThere is also no explicit requirement to define process interactions, sequence, and criteria for PIMS processes as a system-wide requirement (ISO 4.4).\n\nCategory 4: The law assigns institutional bodies (Comit\u00e9 de Transparencia as \u201cautoridad m\u00e1xima\u201d) (77)-(78) and requires resources and programs (24)(I)-(V), but it does not impose ISO-style \u201ctop management\u201d leadership duties such as ensuring PIMS integration into business processes, promoting continual improvement as a management obligation, and ensuring the PIMS achieves intended outcomes (explicitly as a management system).\n\nIn public-sector structures, responsibilities exist, but the law does not map to the ISO requirement for demonstrable leadership/commitment by top management to the PIMS as such (e.g., explicit accountability for PIMS effectiveness).\n\nCategory 5: The law mandates privacy notices (20)-(22) and internal policies/programs (24)(II), but it does not require a single, organization-wide \u201cprivacy policy\u201d meeting ISO 5.2 characteristics (appropriateness, framework for objectives, commitment to meet requirements, commitment to continual improvement) and to be made available as documented information and communicated internally/externally as appropriate.\n\nPrivacy notice obligations (20)-(22) are not equivalent to a management-system privacy policy; the law does not require publishing/maintaining a policy statement with ISO elements.\n\nCategory 6: The law defines functions for Comit\u00e9 de Transparencia and Unidad de Transparencia (77)-(79) and allows designating an \u201coficial de protecci\u00f3n de datos personales\u201d (79), but it does not require assigning and communicating responsibilities/authorities for all PIMS roles (including responsibility for ensuring PIMS conformity and reporting PIMS performance to top management) in the ISO sense.\n\nIt also does not require role assignment to cover all PIMS processes (risk, audits, management review, corrective action) as a coherent responsibility matrix; it focuses on statutory bodies and ARCO handling.\n\nCategory 7: The law requires risk analysis for security measures (27)(IV) and impact assessments for intensive/relevant processing (68)-(72), but it does not require a unified \u201crisks and opportunities\u201d planning process for the PIMS (ISO 6.1.1), including planning actions, integration into processes, and evaluation of effectiveness of those actions as a management-system planning requirement.\n\nIt also does not require ISO-style privacy risk criteria (risk acceptance criteria, consistent/comparable assessments) and a \u201cstatement of applicability\u201d against a control set (Annex A concept), nor formal risk owner approval and residual risk acceptance (elements in ISO 6.1.2 not mirrored by (27) or (68)).\n\nCategory 8: The law requires programs/policies and resources (24)(I)-(III) but does not require establishing measurable privacy objectives at relevant functions/levels, monitoring them, communicating them, updating them, and keeping them as documented information (ISO 6.2). It is compliance-driven rather than objective-driven.\n\nThere is also no requirement to plan how to achieve objectives (actions, resources, responsibilities, timelines, evaluation) as a structured objective planning process.\n\nCategory 9: The law requires updating the security document upon substantial changes (30)(I) and ties impact assessments to new/modified systems (68), but it does not require planned changes to the PIMS as a general management-system requirement (ISO 6.3). Change planning is addressed in specific contexts (security documentation, DPIA-like evaluation), not as a system-wide change management control.\n\nIt also does not require assessing consequences of changes to the PIMS and ensuring integrity of the management system during change (beyond security-document updates).\n\nCategory 10: The law requires allocating resources for programs/policies (24)(I), but it does not require determining and providing resources for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a PIMS (ISO 7.1) as a management-system requirement. Resource allocation is mentioned as a mechanism, not as a systematic requirement tied to PIMS lifecycle.\n\nIt also lacks requirements to ensure resource adequacy for monitoring, internal audit, management review, corrective actions, etc., as part of a PIMS.\n\nCategory 11: The law mandates training programs (24)(III) and general capacity-building (84), but it does not require determining competence needs for persons doing work under the organization\u2019s control that affects privacy performance, ensuring competence based on education/training/experience, and retaining documented evidence of competence (ISO 7.2).\n\nTraining obligations exist, yet competence management (including evaluation of training effectiveness and competence records) is not explicitly required.\n\nCategory 12: The law includes confidentiality duties (36) and training/culture promotion (2)(VII), (24)(III), but it does not require ensuring personnel awareness of the privacy policy, their contribution to PIMS effectiveness, and implications of nonconformity with PIMS requirements (ISO 7.3). The law focuses on legal duties and sanctions (132) rather than structured awareness outcomes.\n\nThere is no explicit requirement for ongoing awareness communications tied to a PIMS framework.\n\nCategory 13: The law requires notifying data subjects and authorities of significant breaches (34)-(35) and sets notice/communication via privacy notice (20)-(22), but it does not require determining internal and external communications relevant to the PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a management-system communication plan (ISO 7.4).\n\nCommunications are prescribed for specific events (privacy notice, breach notification, ARCO procedures), not as a comprehensive PIMS communications process.\n\nCategory 14: The law requires documenting security measures in a \u201cdocumento de seguridad\u201d (29) and documenting actions in a \u201csistema de gesti\u00f3n\u201d (28), but it does not require the broader ISO 7.5 documented information controls for all PIMS documentation (creation/update controls, approval, versioning, distribution/access, retention/disposition) across the management system.\n\nIt also does not require controlling \u201cdocumented information of external origin\u201d needed for PIMS planning/operation (ISO 7.5.3), beyond general legal recordkeeping expectations.\n\nCategory 15: The law mandates implementing and maintaining security measures and related activities (25)-(27) and requires procedures for retention/suppression (18), but it does not require operational planning and control for all PIMS processes with defined criteria, controlled changes, and documented evidence that processes were carried out as planned (ISO 8.1).\n\nIt also does not comprehensively require control of externally provided processes/products/services relevant to the PIMS (beyond processor/cloud provisions in (53)-(58) and transfer instruments (60)-(62)), leaving gaps for broader supplier governance as a PIMS operational control.\n\nCategory 16: The law requires risk analysis for security measures (27)(IV) and periodic monitoring/review of security measures (27)(VII), but it does not require performing privacy risk assessments at planned intervals or upon significant changes as a formal PIMS requirement, nor retaining documented results specifically as \u201cprivacy risk assessments\u201d (ISO 8.2). The risk analysis is embedded in security measures, not framed as a PIMS-wide privacy risk assessment regime.\n\nAlso missing are ISO elements like consistent/comparable assessment outputs and explicit linkage to risk criteria and acceptance (more detailed in ISO 6.1.2).\n\nCategory 17: The law requires a plan of work to implement missing security measures (27)(VI) and corrective/preventive actions after incidents (31), but it does not require implementing a privacy risk treatment plan as a PIMS requirement and retaining documented information of treatment results (ISO 8.3). Treatment is security-centric and incident-driven rather than a structured privacy risk treatment lifecycle.\n\nIt also does not require formal selection of controls and justification for inclusion/exclusion (statement of applicability concept), which is central to ISO-aligned risk treatment.\n\nCategory 18: The law requires monitoring and periodic review of security measures (27)(VII), maintaining a breach log (33), and authorities to apply indicators to evaluate compliance (81)(XXII), (83)(XII), but it does not require the organization to determine what to monitor/measure, methods, timing, analysis/evaluation, and to retain documented evidence of results as part of evaluating privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness (ISO 9.1).\n\nMuch of the \u201cmeasurement\u201d function is placed on authorities (81)(XXII) rather than requiring internal organizational performance evaluation of the PIMS.\n\nCategory 19: The law contemplates audits as part of accountability mechanisms (24)(V) and allows voluntary audits by authorities (120), but it does not require an internal audit program at planned intervals with defined objectives, criteria, scope, auditor impartiality, reporting, and documented evidence (ISO 9.2). \u201cAuditor\u00edas\u201d in (24)(V) are not specified to the ISO depth.\n\nBecause audits can be internal and/or external under (24)(V), the law still lacks mandatory program structure and requirements for objectivity and audit records.\n\nCategory 20: The law assigns oversight functions to the Comit\u00e9 de Transparencia (78) and requires periodic review of policies/programs (24)(IV), but it does not require top management to conduct management reviews at planned intervals with specified inputs (changes in issues, interested parties, performance trends, audit results, nonconformities) and outputs (decisions on improvements/changes) with documented evidence (ISO 9.3).\n\nThe periodic review in (24)(IV) is narrower (policies/programs of security) and not a full management review of a PIMS.\n\nCategory 21: The law references \u201cmejora continua\u201d in the context of updating the security document (30)(II) and requires monitoring/review (27)(VII), but it does not require continual improvement of the overall PIMS suitability, adequacy, and effectiveness (ISO 10.1). Continuous improvement is not established as a general management-system duty across all privacy processes.\n\nImprovements are mainly tied to security controls and incident response, not to a holistic PIMS improvement cycle.\n\nCategory 22: The law requires analyzing causes of security breaches and implementing corrective/preventive actions (31), updating the security document due to corrective actions (30)(IV), and keeping a breach log with corrective actions (33), but it does not require a general nonconformity and corrective action process for the PIMS (react, correct, deal with consequences, root cause, prevent recurrence, review effectiveness, update PIMS) with documented evidence (ISO 10.2) beyond security incidents.\n\nNoncompliance is largely handled through sanctions and enforcement (121)-(137) rather than an internal, systematic corrective-action process covering all PIMS requirements (e.g., ARCO process failures, notice deficiencies, transfer governance) with documented corrective action records."
