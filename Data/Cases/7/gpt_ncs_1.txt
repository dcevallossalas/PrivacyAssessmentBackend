"Category 0: Understanding the organization and its context (4.1)  \nThe Law defines scope and actors (e.g., \u201cresponsable\u201d, \u201cpersona encargada\u201d, \u201csujetos obligados\u201d) and sets general principles (1)\u2013(4), (10)\u2013(13), but it does not require each organization to perform a structured \u201ccontext analysis\u201d as a management-system input (internal/external issues, governance, policies, procedures, administrative/judicial decisions, contractual requirements). There is only a partial reference to \u201ccontext\u201d in security activities (27)(I), which is limited to security management and the data lifecycle, not the broader PIMS context determination required by ISO 27701.  \n\nAlso, ISO 27701 explicitly requires determining whether climate change is a relevant issue and determining/segregating controller vs processor roles per processing instance. The Law does not mention climate change at all, and while it defines \u201cresponsable\u201d and \u201cpersona encargada\u201d (3)(XXII), (3)(XXV) and regulates their relationship (52)\u2013(58), it does not require an organization to formally determine and document its role(s) per processing activity nor to separate controls/scopes when acting in both roles.\n\nCategory 1: Understanding the needs and expectations of interested parties (4.2)  \nThe Law focuses on rights of the data subject (\u201cpersona titular\u201d) and oversight bodies (e.g., ARCO rights (37)\u2013(51), authorities\u2019 powers (81)\u2013(83)), but it does not require an organization to identify all \u201cinterested parties\u201d relevant to a PIMS (e.g., other controllers, processors, subcontractors, customers, supervisory authorities) and to determine their requirements as an input to the system. It provides obligations toward some parties (e.g., contracts with processors (53), cloud providers (58), transfers (60)\u2013(63)), yet it does not impose a systematic stakeholder/requirements determination process.  \n\nISO 27701 also notes that interested parties can have requirements related to climate change and that some parties can expect independently audited conformity to standards. The Law does not address climate-related expectations, nor does it require considering expectations of certification/audited conformity (it only allows using standards/best practices optionally (23), and provides for voluntary audits by authorities (120), not stakeholder-driven conformity expectations).\n\nCategory 2: Determining the scope of the privacy information management system (4.3)  \nThe Law defines legal applicability broadly (1), (4) and regulates processing activities, but it does not require each organization to define and maintain a documented scope statement for a PIMS (boundaries, applicability, interfaces, included processing activities) as ISO 27701 requires. Legal scope \u2260 management-system scope; the Law does not mandate documenting organizational boundaries, exclusions, or interfaces with external providers as a \u201cPIMS scope\u201d artifact.  \n\nWhile the Law requires documenting certain security elements (28)\u2013(30) and procedures for retention/suppression (18), these are not equivalent to a formal PIMS scope determination considering issues (4.1) and interested parties (4.2) as ISO requires.\n\nCategory 3: Privacy information management system (4.4)  \nThe Law requires a \u201csistema de gesti\u00f3n\u201d for security measures documentation and improvement (28) and a \u201cdocumento de seguridad\u201d (29)\u2013(30), but this is narrower than establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a full PIMS covering all ISO 27701 clauses (governance, leadership, objectives, audits, management review, etc.). The Law\u2019s management-system concept is tied to \u201ctreatment and security\u201d measures (28), not a comprehensive privacy information management system.  \n\nAdditionally, ISO 27701 requires defining processes and their interactions for the PIMS. The Law mandates certain activities (27) and mechanisms (24), but it does not require mapping process interactions or operating a PDCA-style system across all privacy controls.\n\nCategory 4: Leadership and commitment (5.1)  \nThe Law assigns institutional structures (Comit\u00e9 de Transparencia as \u201cautoridad m\u00e1xima\u201d (77) and its functions (78)), but it does not require \u201ctop management\u201d to demonstrate leadership and commitment in the ISO sense (integration into business processes, ensuring resources, promoting continual improvement, communicating importance). Some resource and program obligations exist (24)(I)\u2013(IV), yet they are framed as mechanisms of accountability, not explicit top-management leadership duties.  \n\nAlso, ISO requires leadership to ensure PIMS achieves intended results and to support relevant roles. The Law defines roles and functions (78)\u2013(79) but does not impose explicit leadership behaviors, strategic alignment, or governance review responsibilities comparable to ISO 27701.\n\nCategory 5: Privacy policy (5.2)  \nThe Law requires privacy notices (20)\u2013(22) and internal policies/programs (24)(II), but it does not require a single, top-management-approved \u201cprivacy policy\u201d that provides a framework for objectives and includes commitments to meet requirements and continually improve the PIMS, nor does it require making such a policy available to interested parties as documented information.  \n\nA privacy notice is not equivalent to an ISO privacy policy: the notice is outward-facing information to data subjects (20)\u2013(22), while ISO requires an internal policy statement with commitments and a management-system orientation. The Law does not explicitly require that.\n\nCategory 6: Roles, responsibilities and authorities (5.3)  \nThe Law defines responsibilities for Comit\u00e9 de Transparencia and Unidad de Transparencia (78)\u2013(79) and requires defining staff functions/obligations in the security document (27)(II), (29)(II). However, ISO 27701 requires assigning responsibility/authority for ensuring PIMS conformity and reporting PIMS performance to top management. The Law does not require a designated role accountable for PIMS conformity to ISO requirements, nor a formal reporting line to \u201ctop management\u201d on PIMS performance.  \n\nThe optional \u201coficial de protecci\u00f3n de datos personales\u201d (79, second paragraph) is not mandatory and is not framed with ISO-style authority to oversee PIMS conformity and performance reporting.\n\nCategory 7: Actions to address risks and opportunities (6.1)  \nThe Law mandates risk analysis and gap analysis for security measures (27)(IV)\u2013(V) and a work plan (27)(VI), plus updates after changes/incidents (30)\u2013(31). But ISO 27701 requires a broader planning process addressing risks and opportunities to achieve intended PIMS outcomes, including risk criteria, risk acceptance criteria, consistent repeatable assessments, statement of applicability, risk owners, and documented risk treatment plan approval (6.1.2). The Law does not require risk acceptance criteria, risk owner approval, or a statement of applicability against Annex A controls.  \n\nAlso, ISO explicitly includes physical and environmental security and encourages integrating privacy and information security risk assessments. The Law addresses physical/technical/admin measures (25) and definitions (3)(XIX)\u2013(XXI), but it does not require the ISO-style integrated risk governance artifacts (criteria, SoA, residual risk acceptance).\n\nCategory 8: Privacy objectives and planning to achieve them (6.2)  \nThe Law requires programs/policies and resources (24)(I)\u2013(IV) and training (24)(III), but it does not require establishing measurable privacy objectives at relevant functions/levels, monitoring them, communicating them, updating them, and keeping them as documented information, nor planning actions/resources/responsibilities/timelines/evaluation methods to achieve them as ISO 27701 requires.  \n\nThe Law\u2019s approach is compliance-driven (principles, duties, procedures) rather than objective-driven management. There is no explicit requirement to define KPIs/objectives for privacy performance.\n\nCategory 9: Planning of changes (6.3)  \nThe Law requires updating the security document when substantial processing changes occur (30)(I) and performing DPIAs for intensive/relevant processing changes (68)\u2013(72), but it does not require that changes to the overall PIMS be carried out in a planned manner (change management for the management system itself). The Law focuses on specific artifacts (security document, DPIA) rather than systematic PIMS change planning.  \n\nAlso, ISO expects planned change control and review of unintended changes. The Law mentions monitoring/review (27)(VII) and corrective actions after incidents (31), but not a general change-management discipline for the PIMS.\n\nCategory 10: Resources (7.1)  \nThe Law requires allocating resources for privacy programs/policies (24)(I), but ISO 27701 requires determining and providing resources for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving the PIMS. The Law does not explicitly connect resourcing to a PIMS lifecycle, nor does it require resource adequacy assessment for PIMS effectiveness.  \n\nIn addition, the Law does not require documenting resource planning or ensuring resources for all PIMS processes (communications, audits, management review, etc.), beyond the limited mechanisms listed in (24).\n\nCategory 11: Competence (7.2)  \nThe Law requires training and updating personnel (24)(III), (27)(VIII), and Comit\u00e9 de Transparencia programs (78)(VII), but it does not require determining competence requirements for roles affecting privacy performance, ensuring competence based on education/training/experience, taking actions to acquire competence, evaluating effectiveness, and retaining documented evidence of competence as ISO 27701 requires.  \n\nTraining obligations exist, yet competence management (role-based competence criteria + evidence) is not explicitly mandated.\n\nCategory 12: Awareness (7.3)  \nThe Law mandates confidentiality controls (36) and training programs (24)(III), but it does not require ensuring that persons under the organization\u2019s control are aware of the privacy policy, their contribution to PIMS effectiveness, and implications of nonconformity (ISO awareness requirements). The Law is silent on awareness outcomes and internal communication of policy/benefits.  \n\nAwareness is implied through training, but ISO requires explicit awareness topics and organizational assurance that awareness exists.\n\nCategory 13: Communication (7.4)  \nThe Law regulates external communication to data subjects via privacy notices (20)\u2013(22) and breach notification (34)\u2013(35), and it provides mechanisms for complaints/questions (24)(VI). However, it does not require the organization to determine and plan internal/external communications for the PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a structured communication process.  \n\nISO communication includes internal PIMS communications and communications with interested parties beyond data subjects; the Law does not require a communication plan or governance for PIMS communications.\n\nCategory 14: Documented information (7.5)  \nThe Law requires documenting specific items: retention/suppression procedures (18), security management system documentation (28), security document contents (29), breach log (33), and contracts/instruments with processors and transfers (53), (60). But ISO 27701 requires controlling documented information (creation/update controls, versioning, access, retention/disposition, external documents control) for all PIMS documentation. The Law does not establish a general document control regime for PIMS documentation.  \n\nWhile (30) requires updating the security document under certain events, it does not cover ISO-style document control requirements (approval, distribution, retrieval, change control) across all PIMS documents.\n\nCategory 15: Operational planning and control (8.1)  \nThe Law mandates operational security activities (27) and requires controls for processors/cloud providers (53)\u2013(58) and transfers (60)\u2013(63), but it does not require establishing process criteria, controlling processes against criteria, and maintaining documented information to ensure processes are carried out as planned across the PIMS. The Law is prescriptive on certain operations but not on operational control methodology.  \n\nAlso, ISO requires controlling planned changes and reviewing unintended changes, and controlling externally provided processes relevant to the PIMS. The Law addresses external providers in specific contexts (cloud (57)\u2013(58), processors (53)\u2013(56)), but not a general supplier control framework for all externally provided PIMS-relevant processes.\n\nCategory 16: Privacy risk assessment (8.2)  \nThe Law requires risk analysis for security measures (27)(IV) and DPIAs for intensive/relevant processing (68)\u2013(72), but it does not require performing privacy risk assessments at planned intervals or upon significant changes using criteria established under an ISO-style risk framework, nor retaining documented results specifically as \u201cprivacy risk assessments\u201d distinct from security risk analysis or DPIA.  \n\nDPIA (68) is not the same as periodic privacy risk assessment of the PIMS scope; it is triggered by certain projects and is subject to possible exemption (73), which conflicts with ISO\u2019s expectation of planned intervals and change-triggered assessments.\n\nCategory 17: Privacy risk treatment (8.3)  \nThe Law requires a work plan to implement missing security measures (27)(VI) and corrective/preventive actions after incidents (31), but it does not require implementing a formal privacy risk treatment plan derived from a privacy risk assessment process, nor retaining documented information of treatment results as ISO 27701 requires.  \n\nThe Law\u2019s \u201cplan de trabajo\u201d is security-measure oriented and does not include ISO elements like selecting controls based on risk treatment decisions, documenting residual risk acceptance, or tracking treatment effectiveness for privacy risks broadly.\n\nCategory 18: Monitoring, measurement, analysis and evaluation (9.1)  \nThe Law requires monitoring and periodic review of security measures and threats (27)(VII) and maintaining a breach log (33), and authorities may design indicators to evaluate compliance (81)(XXII), (83)(XII). However, it does not require each organization to define what to monitor/measure, methods, timing, analysis/evaluation, and to keep documented evidence of results to evaluate privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness.  \n\nIn other words, the Law supports oversight and some monitoring, but it does not impose an internal performance measurement framework comparable to ISO 27701.\n\nCategory 19: Internal Audit (9.2)  \nThe Law mentions establishing supervision/audits as a mechanism (24)(V) and allows voluntary audits by authorities (120), but it does not require an internal audit program at planned intervals with defined objectives, criteria, scope, auditor impartiality, reporting, and documented evidence of audit program/results as ISO 27701 requires.  \n\n\u201cAuditor\u00edas\u201d in (24)(V) are not specified with ISO rigor (program management, independence, audit criteria tied to PIMS requirements), and (120) is external/voluntary rather than mandatory internal auditing.\n\nCategory 20: Management review (9.3)  \nThe Law assigns oversight functions to the Comit\u00e9 de Transparencia (78) and requires periodic review of policies/programs (24)(IV) and security measures (27)(VII), but it does not require top management to conduct planned management reviews of the PIMS with defined inputs (changes in issues, interested parties, performance trends, audit results, nonconformities) and outputs (decisions/actions) with documented evidence.  \n\nThe Law lacks an explicit \u201cmanagement review\u201d cycle and does not require documenting review results as a management-system control.\n\nCategory 21: Continual improvement (10.1)  \nThe Law references improvement indirectly (e.g., updating the security document due to \u201cmejora continua\u201d (30)(II), and corrective actions after incidents (31)), but it does not require continual improvement of the overall PIMS\u2019s suitability, adequacy, and effectiveness. Improvement duties are mainly tied to security controls and incident response, not the full privacy management system.  \n\nISO\u2019s continual improvement is broader (policy, objectives, processes, audits, management review). The Law does not mandate that comprehensive improvement loop.\n\nCategory 22: Nonconformity and corrective action (10.2)  \nThe Law requires corrective/preventive actions after security breaches (31) and updates to the security document after corrective/preventive actions (30)(IV), and it provides sanctions for noncompliance (132). However, it does not require an internal nonconformity management process covering all PIMS requirements: reacting, root-cause analysis, determining recurrence/elsewhere, reviewing effectiveness, and retaining documented evidence for nonconformities and corrective actions.  \n\nThe Law\u2019s enforcement model is largely external (verification (115)\u2013(119), sanctions (132)), and its internal corrective-action requirement is incident-centric (security vulnerabilities), not a general PIMS nonconformity/corrective action system."
