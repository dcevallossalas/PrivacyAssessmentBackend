"Category 0: Decree 1377 is a regulatory instrument focused on consent, notices/policies, data subject rights, transfers/transmissions, and \u201caccountability\u201d in the sense of being able to *demonstrate* compliance to the SIC (e.g., (26)). It does **not** require an organization to systematically determine and document its internal/external context, including governance, applicable judicial/administrative decisions, contractual requirements, or whether climate change is a relevant issue (ISO requirement). Nor does it require an explicit determination of the organization\u2019s role as controller vs processor (or joint controller) per processing activity; it defines \u201cResponsable/Encargado\u201d and regulates their obligations (e.g., (25)), but does not impose a context/role-scoping exercise.\n\nCategory 1: The Decree recognizes \u201cTitulares\u201d and their rights and channels to exercise them (e.g., (20)-(23)), and it contemplates the SIC as an oversight authority (e.g., (4), (26)-(27)). However, it does **not** require an organization to identify and manage the needs/expectations of the full set of \u201cinterested parties\u201d (customers, processors/subprocessors, partners, etc.) as an input to a management system, nor to decide which requirements will be addressed through a PIMS. It also does not address climate-change-related expectations of interested parties (ISO note), and it frames communications mainly toward data subjects (policies/notice) rather than a broader stakeholder requirement management approach ((13)-(17)).\n\nCategory 2: The Decree does not require defining the **scope** and boundaries of a privacy information management system as documented information. While it regulates specific processing topics (authorization, policies, rights, international transfers) ((4)-(27)), it does not mandate an organization to determine which business units, processes, systems, locations, and processing activities are included/excluded, nor to document that scope (ISO 4.3). There is also no requirement to ensure the scope explicitly \u201cincludes the processing of PII\u201d as a management-system boundary statement; the Decree assumes processing exists and regulates it, but does not require a formal scoping artifact.\n\nCategory 3: Decree 1377 does not establish a requirement to create, implement, maintain, and **continually improve** a privacy information management system with defined processes and interactions (ISO 4.4). It requires certain operational/privacy governance elements (policies, notices, procedures, contracts) ((13), (18), (25), (27)), but these are compliance obligations, not a management system framework with process interaction, PDCA cycle, and system-level continual improvement.\n\nCategory 4: The Decree does not impose explicit **top management leadership and commitment** duties comparable to ISO 5.1 (strategic alignment, integration into business processes, resource provision, promoting continual improvement). It requires \u201cresponsables\u201d to develop policies and demonstrate measures to the SIC ((13), (26)-(27)), but it does not assign these obligations to \u201ctop management,\u201d nor require evidence of leadership behaviors, resource allocation, or strategic integration.\n\nCategory 5: Although the Decree requires privacy policies and minimum content ((13)) and requires communicating changes ((5), (13)), it does not require a **privacy policy** to include commitments to meet applicable requirements and to **continual improvement** of a PIMS (ISO 5.2(c)-(d)). The policy content mandated is largely informational (identity, purposes, rights, contact point, procedures, effective date) ((13)(1)-(6)), not a management commitment statement or framework for objectives.\n\nCategory 6: The Decree requires designating a person/area to handle data protection requests ((23)) and requires policies to identify the area responsible for petitions/complaints ((13)(4)). However, it does not require a full role/responsibility/authority model for a PIMS, including assignment of responsibility for ensuring PIMS conformity to ISO 27701 and reporting PIMS performance to top management (ISO 5.3). In short, it creates an operational \u201ccontact point\u201d but not a governance structure with defined authorities and reporting lines.\n\nCategory 7: The Decree references \u201cpotential risks\u201d as a proportionality factor for accountability ((26)(4)) and mentions security measures to be instructed by the SIC ((19)). But it does not require a formal **risk and opportunity** planning process, risk criteria, repeatable risk assessments, risk owners, statement of applicability, risk treatment plan approval, or documented risk treatment process (ISO 6.1.1\u20136.1.2). The Decree\u2019s \u201cdemonstration\u201d approach ((26)-(27)) is not equivalent to ISO\u2019s structured privacy risk management lifecycle.\n\nCategory 8: The Decree does not require establishing **privacy objectives** that are measurable (where practicable), monitored, communicated internally, updated, and documented, nor planning actions/resources/responsibilities/timelines to achieve them (ISO 6.2). It mandates compliance actions (e.g., policies, notices, procedures) ((13)-(18)) but does not require objective-setting or performance planning beyond compliance.\n\nCategory 9: The Decree requires communicating \u201csubstantial changes\u201d to policies and, in some cases, obtaining new consent when purposes change ((5), (13)). However, it does not require **planned change management** for the PIMS itself (ISO 6.3): assessing impacts of changes, planning implementation, and maintaining system integrity. Its change provisions are narrow (policy/purpose/identity changes affecting authorization), not a general management-system change control requirement.\n\nCategory 10: The Decree does not require determining and providing **resources** for establishing/implementing/maintaining/continually improving a PIMS (ISO 7.1). It implies some organizational capability (e.g., \u201cestructura administrativa\u201d proportional to size) ((27)(1)), but it does not mandate resource planning, budgeting, or ensuring availability of people/tools/time for privacy management system operation.\n\nCategory 11: The Decree mentions training/education mechanisms as part of internal policies ((27)(2)), but it does not require a competence management process: determining required competence, ensuring competence via education/training/experience, evaluating effectiveness, and retaining documented evidence (ISO 7.2). The obligation is high-level and not framed as a controlled competence program with records.\n\nCategory 12: The Decree does not require an **awareness** program ensuring personnel understand the privacy policy, their contribution to PIMS effectiveness, and implications of nonconformity (ISO 7.3). While it requires policies and procedures to exist and be applied ((13), (27)), it does not impose organization-wide awareness obligations or internal communication of policy and consequences.\n\nCategory 13: The Decree requires communicating privacy policies/notice to data subjects and communicating substantial policy changes ((5), (13)-(17)). But it does not require determining and planning **internal and external communications** for the PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a systematic communication plan (ISO 7.4). Its communication duties are mostly outward-facing to Titulares and event-driven, not a comprehensive PIMS communications framework.\n\nCategory 14: The Decree requires certain documents to exist (policies in physical/electronic form) ((13)) and to retain models of privacy notices ((16)), and it requires documenting procedures for retention/suppression ((11)). However, it does not establish ISO-style **documented information control**: identification, versioning, review/approval, controlled distribution/access, retention/disposition rules for all PIMS documents, and control of external-origin documents (ISO 7.5.1\u20137.5.3). The Decree\u2019s documentation requirements are partial and not a full document control system.\n\nCategory 15: The Decree mandates certain operational controls (consent collection, notices, rights procedures, contracts with processors) ((4)-(7), (18), (25)). But it does not require \u201coperational planning and control\u201d in the ISO sense: defining process criteria, controlling processes against criteria, ensuring documented information to evidence processes were carried out as planned, controlling planned changes, and controlling externally provided processes/services relevant to the PIMS (ISO 8.1). Processor control exists mainly via contract minimums ((25)), not a broader operational control framework.\n\nCategory 16: The Decree does not require performing **privacy risk assessments** at planned intervals or upon significant change, nor retaining documented results (ISO 8.2). It only requires being able to demonstrate measures and consider potential risks as a proportionality factor ((26)(4)), which is not equivalent to periodic, criteria-based risk assessments with documented outputs.\n\nCategory 17: The Decree does not require implementing a documented **privacy risk treatment plan** and retaining results (ISO 8.3). It requires implementing \u201cappropriate and effective measures\u201d and being able to evidence them to the SIC ((26)), but it does not require a formal treatment plan, residual risk acceptance, or tracking treatment outcomes as ISO expects.\n\nCategory 18: The Decree does not require defining what to **monitor and measure**, methods, timing, analysis/evaluation, and documented evidence to evaluate privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness (ISO 9.1). It provides compliance obligations and allows SIC oversight and requests for evidence ((4), (26)), but it does not impose internal performance measurement, KPIs, trend analysis, or effectiveness evaluation.\n\nCategory 19: The Decree does not require an **internal audit** program (planned intervals, objectives/criteria/scope, auditor impartiality, reporting, documented evidence) (ISO 9.2). Oversight is externalized to the SIC (e.g., (26)-(27)), and while organizations must respond to SIC requests, there is no obligation to self-audit the privacy management system.\n\nCategory 20: The Decree does not require **management review** by top management at planned intervals with specified inputs/outputs and documented evidence (ISO 9.3). It requires policies and accountability demonstration to the SIC ((13), (26)-(27)), but it does not mandate periodic executive review of PIMS suitability/adequacy/effectiveness, nor decisions on improvement opportunities.\n\nCategory 21: The Decree does not require **continual improvement** of a PIMS (ISO 10.1). It includes some dynamic elements (e.g., updating policies and re-consenting when purposes change) ((5), (13)), but these are compliance triggers, not a requirement to continually improve the management system\u2019s effectiveness.\n\nCategory 22: The Decree does not establish a **nonconformity and corrective action** process (react, correct, root cause, prevent recurrence, review effectiveness, keep records) (ISO 10.2). It provides for data subject complaints and escalation to the SIC if deletion/revocation is not honored ((9)), but that is a rights-enforcement mechanism, not an internal corrective action system for PIMS nonconformities with documented root-cause analysis and systemic remediation."
