"Category 0: GDPR defines controller/processor roles and territorial/material scope ((2)-(4)), but it does not require an organization to perform a structured \u201ccontext\u201d determination as a management-system activity (e.g., identifying internal/external issues, governance context, applicable judicial/administrative decisions, contractual requirements) as a maintained input to a PIMS. It also does not include the ISO 27701/27701:2025 explicit requirement to determine whether climate change is a relevant issue.\n\nCategory 1: GDPR recognizes multiple stakeholders (data subjects, supervisory authorities, recipients) and transparency duties ((12)-(15), (51)-(58), (77)), but it does not require an organization to systematically identify \u201cinterested parties\u201d and their expectations as a management-system input, nor to decide which expectations will be addressed through a PIMS. It also does not address the ISO note that interested parties can have requirements related to climate change.\n\nCategory 2: GDPR defines scope of application ((2)-(3)) and requires records of processing ((30)), but it does not require an organization to define and maintain a documented \u201cscope of the PIMS\u201d (boundaries/applicability) as a management-system artifact, nor to explicitly consider internal/external issues and interested-party requirements when setting that scope.\n\nCategory 3: GDPR imposes substantive compliance obligations (e.g., principles (5), accountability (5)(2), (24)-(25)), but it does not require establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a formal privacy information management system with defined processes and interactions (i.e., a PIMS as a management system per ISO clause 4.4).\n\nCategory 4: GDPR assigns accountability to the controller ((5)(2), (24)) and requires DPO involvement in some cases ((37)-(39)), but it does not require \u201ctop management\u201d leadership/commitment actions typical of ISO management systems (integration into business processes, ensuring resources, promoting continual improvement, etc.). The DPO framework ((38)(3)) is not equivalent to top management leadership requirements.\n\nCategory 5: GDPR requires transparency information to data subjects ((12)-(14)) and may imply internal policies ((24)(2)), but it does not compel a documented, organization-wide \u201cprivacy policy\u201d meeting ISO elements (framework for objectives, commitment to continual improvement, availability to interested parties) as a management-system policy statement.\n\nCategory 6: GDPR defines roles (controller/processor/joint controllers) ((4)(7)-(8), (26), (28)) and mandates a DPO in certain cases ((37)), but it does not require top management to assign and communicate responsibilities/authorities for all relevant PIMS roles (including responsibility for PIMS conformance and reporting to top management) as ISO 5.3 requires.\n\nCategory 7: GDPR requires risk-based measures ((24)(1), (25), (32), (35)) and DPIAs ((35)), but it does not require a formal \u201crisks and opportunities\u201d planning process for the PIMS, nor ISO-specific artifacts like privacy risk criteria, risk acceptance criteria, risk owners, a statement of applicability, and a documented privacy risk treatment plan approval/acceptance workflow (ISO 6.1.1\u20136.1.2). GDPR\u2019s risk approach is substantive but not structured as an ISO management-system planning requirement.\n\nCategory 8: GDPR does not require setting measurable privacy objectives, monitoring them, communicating them internally, and maintaining documented objectives and plans to achieve them (ISO 6.2). While GDPR requires compliance and accountability ((5)(2), (24)), it does not translate into an objectives-and-planning framework.\n\nCategory 9: GDPR includes \u201cdata protection by design and by default\u201d ((25)) and requires updating measures as needed ((24)(1)), but it does not require planned management-system change control for changes to the PIMS itself (ISO 6.3), including planned implementation and evaluation of changes as a system requirement.\n\nCategory 10: GDPR requires appropriate measures and implies resourcing (e.g., security measures (32), DPO resources (38)(2)), but it does not require an organization to determine and provide resources for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a PIMS as a management-system requirement (ISO 7.1). GDPR\u2019s resource expectations are partial and role-specific, not system-wide.\n\nCategory 11: GDPR requires DPO expertise ((37)(5)) and implies competence for certain functions, but it does not require an organization-wide competence management process (determine competence needs, ensure competence, take actions, evaluate effectiveness, retain evidence) for all personnel affecting privacy performance (ISO 7.2).\n\nCategory 12: GDPR contains transparency and some training/awareness-related expectations via DPO tasks ((39)(1)(b)) and general accountability ((24)), but it does not require a structured awareness program ensuring all persons under the organization\u2019s control are aware of the privacy policy, their contribution, and implications of nonconformity (ISO 7.3). GDPR does not mandate internal awareness controls as a management-system element.\n\nCategory 13: GDPR mandates external communications to data subjects and authorities (e.g., (12)-(15), breach notification (33)-(34)), but it does not require the organization to determine and manage internal/external communications relevant to a PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a systematic communication process (ISO 7.4).\n\nCategory 14: GDPR requires certain documentation (records (30), breach documentation (33)(5), DPIA documentation (35), processor contracts (28)(3), etc.), but it does not establish ISO-style documented information controls (creation/update controls, versioning, distribution/access/retrieval, retention/disposition, control of external documents) for the PIMS documentation set (ISO 7.5.1\u20137.5.3).\n\nCategory 15: GDPR requires implementing controls and managing processors ((24)-(32), (28)), but it does not require operational planning and control in the ISO sense (process criteria, controlled execution, documented confidence that processes were carried out as planned, control of planned changes and unintended changes, and control of externally provided processes as part of the PIMS) (ISO 8.1). GDPR\u2019s obligations are outcome/control-focused, not process-control framework requirements.\n\nCategory 16: GDPR requires DPIAs for high-risk processing ((35)) and risk consideration for security ((32)), but it does not require periodic privacy risk assessments at planned intervals or when significant changes occur, nor retention of risk assessment results as part of a defined ISO 8.2 process. DPIA triggers are specific and not equivalent to a recurring risk assessment program.\n\nCategory 17: GDPR requires implementing measures to mitigate risks ((24)(1), (32), (35)(7)(d)), but it does not require an ISO-style privacy risk treatment plan, implementation tracking, and documented results of risk treatment (ISO 8.3). GDPR lacks the explicit \u201crisk treatment plan\u201d construct and associated evidence requirements.\n\nCategory 18: GDPR requires some monitoring-like activities (e.g., security testing/evaluation (32)(1)(d)), and supervisory authorities monitor compliance ((57)), but it does not require the organization to define what to monitor/measure, methods, timing, analysis/evaluation, and to evaluate PIMS effectiveness and privacy performance with documented evidence (ISO 9.1). GDPR is not a management-system performance evaluation framework.\n\nCategory 19: GDPR allows/mentions audits in contexts like DPO tasks ((39)(1)(b)) and supervisory authority audits ((58)(1)(b)), and requires processors to enable audits by controllers ((28)(3)(h)), but it does not require the organization to run an internal audit program of its PIMS (objectives/criteria/scope, auditor impartiality, reporting, documented evidence) (ISO 9.2). External/regulatory audits are not the same as internal PIMS audits.\n\nCategory 20: GDPR does not require top management to conduct periodic management reviews of a PIMS with specified inputs/outputs and documented evidence (ISO 9.3). While GDPR requires accountability and updating measures ((24)(1)), it does not prescribe management review governance.\n\nCategory 21: GDPR requires ongoing compliance and updating measures ((24)(1)) and includes review mechanisms at EU level ((97)), but it does not require an organization to continually improve the suitability, adequacy, and effectiveness of a PIMS as a management-system requirement (ISO 10.1). GDPR\u2019s \u201cupdate when necessary\u201d is narrower than continual improvement of a management system.\n\nCategory 22: GDPR provides enforcement, sanctions, and corrective powers for supervisory authorities ((58), (83)-(84)), and requires breach remediation documentation ((33)(5)), but it does not require an internal nonconformity and corrective action process for the organization\u2019s PIMS (react, root-cause analysis, prevent recurrence, effectiveness review, documented evidence) (ISO 10.2). GDPR focuses on legal compliance and regulator actions rather than internal corrective-action system requirements."
