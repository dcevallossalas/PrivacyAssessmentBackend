"Category 0: GDPR defines controller/processor roles ((4)(7), (4)(8)) and territorial/material scope ((2), (3)), but it does not require an organization to systematically determine and document \u201cexternal and internal issues\u201d affecting a PIMS (e.g., governance, strategy, internal context) as a management-system activity. It also does not require identifying applicable \u201cjudicial/administrative decisions\u201d and \u201ccontractual requirements\u201d as part of a context analysis (beyond specific processing-lawfulness bases in (6) and processor contracts in (28)).\n\nIt also does not include the ISO 27701/27001-style requirement to consider whether climate change is a relevant issue (no equivalent in (1)\u2013(99)), nor to separate roles per processing instance as a formal context determination step (even though roles can vary by processing in practice).\n\nCategory 1: GDPR strongly recognizes data subjects and supervisory authorities (e.g., (12)\u2013(23), (51)\u2013(58)), but it does not require an organization to identify all \u201cinterested parties\u201d relevant to a PIMS and their requirements as a structured management-system input. For example, it does not compel mapping expectations of customers, subcontractors, joint controllers, internal stakeholders, etc., as \u201cinterested parties\u201d for the system (even though it regulates some relationships like joint controllers (26) and processors (28)).\n\nIt also does not require determining which interested-party requirements will be addressed through the PIMS (a planning/scoping decision), nor does it include the ISO note about climate-change-related interested-party requirements.\n\nCategory 2: GDPR defines scope of application ((2), (3)) and requires records of processing ((30)), but it does not require an organization to define and maintain a documented \u201cscope of the PIMS\u201d as a management-system boundary statement (documented information about boundaries/applicability). GDPR\u2019s scope is legal applicability, not an internal management-system scope statement covering processes, sites, products/services, interfaces, and exclusions.\n\nIt also does not require explicitly considering internal/external issues ((4.1) concept) and interested-party requirements ((4.2) concept) when setting that scope; those are ISO management-system constructs not mandated by GDPR.\n\nCategory 3: GDPR imposes many operational compliance obligations (e.g., (24)\u2013(36)), but it does not require establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a \u201cprivacy information management system\u201d with defined processes and interactions (a formal management system per ISO). There is no GDPR equivalent to a PIMS architecture requirement (process map, system governance, PDCA cycle) as such.\n\nWhile accountability ((5)(2), (24)(1)) implies organizational measures, GDPR does not compel the management-system meta-requirement that the system itself be established and continually improved.\n\nCategory 4: GDPR requires controllers to implement appropriate measures and be able to demonstrate compliance ((24)(1), (5)(2)), and it requires DPO independence and support ((38)), but it does not explicitly require \u201ctop management\u201d leadership and commitment, integration into business processes, or explicit leadership actions as described in ISO clause 5.1. GDPR does not mandate executive-level governance behaviors (e.g., ensuring resources for the PIMS as a leadership duty) beyond general accountability.\n\nIt also does not require top management to promote continual improvement of a PIMS (as a system), only compliance with legal obligations and appropriate measures.\n\nCategory 5: GDPR mentions \u201cpolicies\u201d as one possible measure ((24)(2)) and requires transparency information to data subjects ((12)\u2013(14)), but it does not require top management to establish a documented \u201cprivacy policy\u201d with a framework for objectives and a commitment to continual improvement (ISO 5.2). GDPR does not compel a single overarching privacy policy document, its required contents, internal communication, and availability to interested parties as a management-system artifact.\n\nAny privacy notices under (13)\u2013(14) are not equivalent to an internal privacy policy with objectives and continual improvement commitments.\n\nCategory 6: GDPR assigns responsibilities in functional terms (controller/processor duties in (24)\u2013(36); DPO tasks in (39)), but it does not require top management to assign and communicate responsibilities/authorities for PIMS conformance and reporting to top management (ISO 5.3). The DPO is not universally required ((37)(1)) and is not framed as the management-system \u201cconformance\u201d role.\n\nThere is also no GDPR requirement to define a management-system governance structure with explicit authorities for system performance reporting.\n\nCategory 7: GDPR uses a risk-based approach (e.g., (24)(1), (32), (35)), but it does not require a formal \u201crisks and opportunities\u201d planning process for the PIMS, including planning actions, integration into processes, and evaluation of effectiveness (ISO 6.1.1). GDPR focuses on risks to rights and freedoms, not broader \u201copportunities\u201d or system-level planning.\n\nAlso, GDPR does not require producing a \u201cstatement of applicability\u201d or a privacy risk treatment plan with risk owners\u2019 approval and residual risk acceptance (ISO 6.1.2 elements). DPIA requirements ((35)) are not the same as an ISO-style risk assessment framework with acceptance criteria and SoA.\n\nCategory 8: GDPR does not require establishing measurable privacy objectives at relevant functions/levels, monitoring them, communicating them, updating them, and keeping them as documented information (ISO 6.2). While GDPR requires compliance and certain outcomes (e.g., security (32), transparency (12)), it does not compel an objectives-and-plans framework.\n\nIt also does not require planning \u201chow to achieve objectives\u201d with resources, responsibilities, timelines, and evaluation methods as a management-system planning requirement.\n\nCategory 9: GDPR does not require \u201cplanning of changes\u201d to the PIMS (ISO 6.3). It requires reassessment in some contexts (e.g., review/update measures in (24)(1); DPIA review when risk changes in (35)(11)), but it does not impose a general requirement that changes to the privacy management system be carried out in a planned manner with controlled change management.\n\nThus, GDPR only partially touches change triggers, not a comprehensive planned-change process for the system.\n\nCategory 10: GDPR requires providing resources for supervisory authorities ((52)(4)) and requires controllers/processors to implement appropriate measures ((24)(1), (32)), but it does not require an organization to determine and provide resources needed for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a PIMS (ISO 7.1). GDPR\u2019s resource expectations are implicit and tied to \u201cappropriate measures,\u201d not a system resource planning requirement.\n\nThere is also no explicit requirement to resource continual improvement of a management system.\n\nCategory 11: GDPR requires DPO expertise ((37)(5)) and implies competence through obligations (e.g., security measures in (32)), but it does not require an organization to determine competence needs for all roles affecting privacy performance, ensure competence via training/experience, take actions to acquire competence, and retain documented evidence (ISO 7.2). GDPR does not mandate competence management as a systematic HR/training control.\n\nEven where DPO is required, GDPR does not require competence records for all relevant personnel, nor evaluation of training effectiveness.\n\nCategory 12: GDPR does not require a general \u201cawareness\u201d program ensuring persons under the organization\u2019s control are aware of the privacy policy, their contribution to PIMS effectiveness, and implications of nonconformity (ISO 7.3). Some awareness is implied (e.g., processor confidentiality commitments in (28)(3)(b)), but GDPR does not compel organization-wide awareness controls.\n\nIt also does not require awareness of management-system requirements, only compliance with legal duties.\n\nCategory 13: GDPR mandates specific external communications (data subject information (12)\u2013(14), breach communications (33)\u2013(34), DPO contact publication (37)(7)), but it does not require determining and planning internal/external communications for the PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a management-system process (ISO 7.4). GDPR is prescriptive about certain communications, not a comprehensive communications plan.\n\nInternal communication planning (e.g., escalation paths, routine reporting) is not required as such.\n\nCategory 14: GDPR requires certain documentation (records (30), breach documentation (33)(5), processor contracts (28), DPIA documentation (35)), but it does not establish a general \u201cdocumented information\u201d control framework (creation/update controls, versioning, distribution, retention/disposition) as in ISO 7.5. GDPR does not require document control procedures for all PIMS documents.\n\nIt also does not require controlling external-origin documents needed for PIMS planning/operation as a formal requirement.\n\nCategory 15: GDPR requires implementing measures and controlling processors ((24), (28), (32)), but it does not require \u201coperational planning and control\u201d in the ISO sense: establishing criteria for processes, controlling processes against criteria, ensuring documented information to have confidence processes were carried out as planned, and controlling planned/unintended changes (ISO 8.1). GDPR is outcome/obligation-based rather than process-control-based.\n\nWhile it requires controller oversight of processors ((28)(3)(h)), it does not mandate a general operational control framework for all PIMS processes and externally provided processes/services.\n\nCategory 16: GDPR requires DPIAs in high-risk cases ((35)) and risk-based security evaluation ((32)), but it does not require performing privacy risk assessments at planned intervals (or when significant changes occur) as a general requirement across the PIMS (ISO 8.2). DPIAs are conditional and scoped to \u201clikely high risk,\u201d not a periodic enterprise-wide privacy risk assessment regime.\n\nIt also does not require retaining documented information of \u201cprivacy risk assessment results\u201d for all processing, only DPIA documentation where applicable and certain records (30).\n\nCategory 17: GDPR requires implementing measures to address risks (e.g., (24)(1), (32), DPIA mitigation in (35)(7)(d), prior consultation (36)), but it does not require an ISO-style \u201cprivacy risk treatment plan\u201d and evidence of its implementation/results (ISO 8.3). GDPR does not compel a formal treatment plan artifact, risk owners, residual risk acceptance, or tracking treatment completion.\n\nMitigation is required, but the management-system structure for treatment planning and documented results is not.\n\nCategory 18: GDPR does not require defining what to monitor/measure, methods, timing, and analysis/evaluation of privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness (ISO 9.1). It requires certain reviews (e.g., regular testing/evaluation of security measures in (32)(1)(d)) and supervisory authority reporting, but not a comprehensive monitoring and measurement program for privacy performance.\n\nThere is also no GDPR requirement to evaluate the effectiveness of a PIMS as a system, only compliance with specific obligations.\n\nCategory 19: GDPR provides for supervisory authority audits ((58)(1)(b)) and requires controllers to allow audits by controllers over processors ((28)(3)(h)), but it does not require the organization to conduct internal audits of its PIMS at planned intervals with an audit program, objectives, criteria, scope, auditor impartiality, and documented results (ISO 9.2). External regulatory audits are not equivalent to internal audit requirements.\n\nThus, GDPR lacks a mandatory internal audit function for privacy management.\n\nCategory 20: GDPR does not require \u201cmanagement review\u201d by top management at planned intervals with specified inputs/outputs and documented evidence (ISO 9.3). While GDPR requires review/update of measures when necessary ((24)(1)) and DPIA review when risk changes ((35)(11)), it does not mandate executive management reviews of the PIMS, including audit results, nonconformities, performance trends, and improvement opportunities.\n\nTherefore, the structured management review cycle is a gap.\n\nCategory 21: GDPR does not require continual improvement of a PIMS (ISO 10.1). It requires compliance and appropriate measures, and encourages updating measures ((24)(1)), but it does not impose a continual improvement obligation for the management system\u2019s suitability/adequacy/effectiveness.\n\nAccountability ((5)(2)) is not the same as a continual improvement requirement.\n\nCategory 22: GDPR provides enforcement, corrective powers, and sanctions (e.g., supervisory authority corrective powers in (58), fines in (83), other sanctions in (84)), but it does not require an organization to run an internal nonconformity and corrective action process (react, root cause analysis, prevent recurrence, review effectiveness, update PIMS) with documented evidence (ISO 10.2). GDPR focuses on legal compliance and regulator actions, not internal CAPA governance.\n\nBreach documentation ((33)(5)) and processor audit support ((28)(3)(h)) do not establish a general corrective action system for all privacy nonconformities."
