"Category 0: GDPR is a legal framework defining *what* must be achieved (lawful processing, rights, accountability), but it does not require an organization to run a formal \u201ccontext analysis\u201d process as a management-system activity. ISO/IEC 27701 requires determining internal/external issues affecting the PIMS and explicitly assessing whether climate change is relevant; GDPR contains no equivalent requirement to evaluate climate change as a contextual issue (no article). GDPR also does not require a structured identification of \u201capplicable judicial decisions/administrative decisions/contractual requirements\u201d as PIMS inputs; it only sets legal obligations and allows Member State law to specify details (e.g., (6)(2)\u2013(3), (23)).\n\nAdditionally, while GDPR defines controller/processor roles ((4)(7)\u2013(8)) and joint controllership ((26)), it does not require the organization to systematically determine and document role-by-processing-instance and to separate controls per role as a management-system scoping/control design step (ISO requirement). GDPR imposes obligations depending on role, but it does not mandate a \u201crole determination methodology\u201d or \u201cseparate set of controls\u201d per role.\n\nCategory 1: GDPR recognizes data subjects (\u201cinteresados\u201d) and supervisory authorities and regulates communications to them (e.g., (12)\u2013(15), (31), (57)), but it does not require an organization to identify *all* \u201cinterested parties\u201d relevant to a PIMS (customers, subcontractors, other controllers/processors, etc.) and to decide which of their requirements will be addressed through the PIMS. ISO/IEC 27701 is broader: it expects a stakeholder mapping and requirement selection process; GDPR is not a management-system stakeholder requirement.\n\nAlso, ISO/IEC 27701 notes that interested parties can have requirements related to climate change; GDPR has no concept of climate-related stakeholder expectations in privacy governance (no article). GDPR focuses on legal rights/obligations, not on capturing and managing broader stakeholder expectations as PIMS inputs.\n\nCategory 2: GDPR defines material and territorial scope ((2), (3)) and applies to processing of personal data, but it does not require an organization to define and maintain a documented \u201cscope of the PIMS\u201d as a management-system artifact, including boundaries, interfaces, and applicability decisions. ISO/IEC 27701 requires a documented scope statement and consideration of context and interested parties (categories 0\u20131); GDPR does not impose that documentation structure.\n\nMoreover, GDPR scope is legal applicability, not an internal management-system boundary decision. For example, GDPR does not require an organization to decide and document which business units, products, locations, or outsourced processes are included in the PIMS, beyond ensuring compliance wherever GDPR applies.\n\nCategory 3: GDPR requires compliance and accountability ((5)(2), (24)(1)), but it does not require establishing, implementing, maintaining, and *continually improving* a formal Privacy Information Management System with defined processes and interactions (PDCA-style). ISO/IEC 27701 is a certifiable management system; GDPR is outcome/obligation-based and does not mandate a system architecture.\n\nWhile GDPR encourages measures and policies ((24)(2), (25), (32)), it does not require the organization to define process interactions, system governance cycles, or management-system documentation typical of ISO standards.\n\nCategory 4: GDPR assigns responsibilities to controllers and requires accountability ((24)(1)), and it requires DPO independence in certain cases ((38)(3)), but it does not require \u201ctop management\u201d leadership and commitment actions such as integrating PIMS into business processes, ensuring resources, promoting continual improvement, and communicating the importance of the PIMS (ISO 5.1). GDPR obligations can be met without explicit top-management leadership behaviors being mandated.\n\nIn short, GDPR is enforceable regardless of governance style; ISO/IEC 27701 requires demonstrable leadership practices. GDPR does not contain a requirement that privacy objectives/policy be aligned with \u201cstrategic direction\u201d or that top management actively direct and support PIMS roles (no article).\n\nCategory 5: GDPR requires transparency information to data subjects ((12)\u2013(14)) and may imply internal policies ((24)(2)), but it does not require top management to establish a documented \u201cprivacy policy\u201d with a framework for objectives and a commitment to continual improvement, nor to make it available to interested parties as appropriate (ISO 5.2). GDPR focuses on privacy notices and compliance measures, not a management-system policy statement.\n\nAlso, GDPR does not require the policy to be communicated internally as a management-system control; it requires that processing information be provided to data subjects and that measures exist, but not a formal policy lifecycle.\n\nCategory 6: GDPR defines roles (controller/processor) ((4)(7)\u2013(8)) and requires DPO tasks/position in some cases ((37)\u2013(39)), but it does not require a general assignment and communication of responsibilities and authorities for all PIMS-relevant roles, including a role responsible for ensuring PIMS conformity and reporting PIMS performance to top management (ISO 5.3). GDPR\u2019s governance requirements are narrower (e.g., DPO where applicable) and do not cover a full RACI-style role framework.\n\nTherefore, an organization could comply with GDPR without formally documenting and communicating internal authorities for PIMS conformance and performance reporting beyond what is needed for compliance.\n\nCategory 7: GDPR requires risk-based measures (e.g., security (32), DPIA (35), accountability (24)), but ISO/IEC 27701 requires a *privacy risk management framework* with explicit risk criteria, risk acceptance criteria, consistent repeatable assessments, statement of applicability, risk treatment plan, risk owner approval, and documented residual risk acceptance. GDPR does not require risk acceptance criteria, a statement of applicability, or formal risk owner sign-off (no article).\n\nGDPR also does not require integrating privacy risk assessment with \u201cphysical and environmental security\u201d as a defined element of privacy risk assessment (ISO 6.1.2(c)(2)); it requires appropriate security measures (32) but not a structured combined privacy+physical/environmental risk methodology.\n\nCategory 8: GDPR requires compliance and accountability but does not require the organization to set measurable privacy objectives, monitor them, communicate them internally, update them, and document plans to achieve them (ISO 6.2). GDPR has no equivalent \u201cprivacy objectives\u201d requirement; it mandates outcomes (lawfulness, rights, security) rather than objective-setting and KPI management (no article).\n\nThus, GDPR can be satisfied through compliance controls without a formal objectives-and-planning framework.\n\nCategory 9: GDPR addresses change implicitly (e.g., DPIA when processing is likely high risk (35), security measures appropriate to risk (32)), but it does not require \u201cplanning of changes\u201d to the PIMS as a management-system process (ISO 6.3). There is no GDPR obligation to manage PIMS changes in a planned manner, with documented change control, impact review, and governance (no article).\n\nGDPR triggers (like DPIA) are specific to certain processing changes, not to management-system changes broadly.\n\nCategory 10: GDPR requires implementing appropriate measures ((24)(1), (32)(1)) and ensuring resources for supervisory authorities ((52)(4)) but does not require an organization to determine and provide resources for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a PIMS (ISO 7.1). GDPR is silent on resourcing a management system; it only requires that measures be effective.\n\nTherefore, GDPR does not align with ISO\u2019s explicit resource planning for the PIMS lifecycle.\n\nCategory 11: GDPR requires DPO expertise where applicable ((37)(5)) and implies competence for secure processing ((32)), but it does not require an organization-wide competence management process: determining competence needs for all persons affecting privacy performance, training plans, effectiveness evaluation, and documented evidence (ISO 7.2). GDPR\u2019s competence requirements are role-specific (DPO) and not a general HR competence system.\n\nHence, GDPR does not fulfill ISO\u2019s systematic competence management expectations.\n\nCategory 12: GDPR requires transparency to data subjects and certain internal governance (e.g., DPO involvement (38)(1)), but it does not require a formal awareness program ensuring all persons under the organization\u2019s control are aware of the privacy policy, their contribution to PIMS effectiveness, and implications of nonconformity (ISO 7.3). GDPR can be complied with without a structured awareness program (no article).\n\nISO\u2019s awareness requirement is broader than GDPR\u2019s compliance obligations.\n\nCategory 13: GDPR mandates specific external communications (privacy notices (13)\u2013(14), breach communications (34), authority notifications (33)) and some internal coordination (e.g., processor to controller breach notice (33)(2)), but it does not require the organization to determine and manage *all* internal/external communications relevant to the PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a planned communication process (ISO 7.4). GDPR is prescriptive on certain communications, not a communication management system.\n\nAlso, GDPR does not require communication planning for non-legal interested parties (e.g., customers as \u201cinterested parties\u201d in ISO sense) unless they are data subjects or otherwise covered by contractual arrangements.\n\nCategory 14: GDPR requires certain documentation (records of processing (30), DPIA (35), breach documentation (33)(5), processor contracts (28)(3)), but ISO/IEC 27701 requires a broader documented information control system: creation/update controls, versioning, distribution, retention/disposition, and control of external documents (ISO 7.5). GDPR does not prescribe document control mechanics (e.g., version control, retention rules for PIMS documents) beyond specific record-keeping obligations.\n\nThus, GDPR does not align with ISO\u2019s management-system documentation governance, even though it requires some documented evidence.\n\nCategory 15: GDPR requires implementing measures and controlling processors (28) and security (32), but it does not require \u201coperational planning and control\u201d as a management-system clause: establishing criteria for processes, controlling processes against criteria, controlling planned changes, reviewing unintended changes, and ensuring externally provided processes are controlled as part of PIMS operations (ISO 8.1). GDPR addresses some operational controls (e.g., processor contracts) but not the full process-control framework.\n\nIn particular, GDPR does not require documented operational criteria for PIMS processes or systematic review of unintended changes (no article).\n\nCategory 16: GDPR requires DPIAs in high-risk cases (35) and implies reassessment when risk changes (35)(11), but ISO 8.2 requires privacy risk assessments at planned intervals and when significant changes occur, with documented results, based on defined criteria (linked to ISO 6.1.2). GDPR does not require periodic risk assessments on a schedule, nor a general privacy risk assessment program beyond DPIA triggers.\n\nTherefore, GDPR is not fully aligned with ISO\u2019s recurring, planned privacy risk assessment regime.\n\nCategory 17: GDPR requires implementing measures to address risks (e.g., (24)(1), (32)(1), (35)(7)(d)), but it does not require a formal \u201cprivacy risk treatment plan\u201d with documented results of treatment and linkage to a statement of applicability and risk acceptance (ISO 8.3). GDPR requires appropriate measures, not a structured risk treatment lifecycle with documented treatment outcomes.\n\nAlso, GDPR does not require explicit retention of \u201cresults of risk treatment\u201d as a distinct documented output (no article).\n\nCategory 18: GDPR requires accountability and may require demonstrating compliance ((5)(2), (24)(1)), but it does not require an organization to define what to monitor/measure, methods, timing, analysis/evaluation, and to evaluate PIMS effectiveness as a management-system performance process (ISO 9.1). GDPR enforcement is external (supervisory authorities) and evidence-based, but it does not mandate internal performance measurement KPIs or monitoring programs.\n\nHence, GDPR does not fulfill ISO\u2019s monitoring/measurement/analysis/evaluation structure.\n\nCategory 19: GDPR allows/anticipates audits in some contexts (e.g., processor must allow audits by controller (28)(3)(h)), but it does not require the organization to run an internal audit program for the PIMS at planned intervals, with audit criteria/scope, auditor impartiality, reporting, and documented evidence (ISO 9.2). GDPR\u2019s audit concept is contractual/controller-driven, not a management-system internal audit requirement.\n\nTherefore, GDPR is not aligned with ISO\u2019s internal audit governance.\n\nCategory 20: GDPR does not require \u201cmanagement review\u201d by top management of the privacy management system at planned intervals, with specified inputs (audit results, performance trends, changes in context) and documented outputs (ISO 9.3). GDPR requires compliance and accountability but not periodic executive review of a PIMS (no article).\n\nEven where GDPR requires updating measures ((24)(1)), it does not prescribe a formal management review meeting/process.\n\nCategory 21: GDPR does not require continual improvement of a PIMS as a management-system requirement. While GDPR expects measures to be reviewed/updated ((24)(1)) and security to be appropriate over time ((32)(1)), it does not mandate a continual improvement cycle for the management system itself (ISO 10.1).\n\nThus, GDPR is partially compatible in spirit but not aligned in explicit continual improvement governance.\n\nCategory 22: GDPR provides enforcement, remedies, and sanctions (e.g., complaints (77), judicial remedies (78)\u2013(79), fines (83), other sanctions (84)), but it does not require an internal \u201cnonconformity and corrective action\u201d process: reacting to nonconformities, root-cause analysis, preventing recurrence, reviewing effectiveness, and maintaining documented evidence (ISO 10.2). GDPR focuses on legal noncompliance consequences, not internal corrective action management.\n\nEven breach handling documentation ((33)(5)) is incident-focused and does not equate to a general corrective action system for any PIMS nonconformity."
