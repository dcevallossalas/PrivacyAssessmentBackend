"Category 0: The framework defines roles such as \u201ctitular del banco de datos personales\u201d and \u201cencargado de tratamiento\u201d (2)(7), (2)(17), and also \u201cresponsable del tratamiento\u201d in the Regulation (Regl. 2)(14), but it does not require an organization to formally determine and document its context, internal/external issues, and applicable requirements as inputs to a management system (ISO 27701/27001 clause-4 style). There is no obligation to identify whether the organization acts as controller/processor per processing instance, nor to separate roles per processing activity (even if the definitions allow it).\n\nCategory 1: While the law recognizes the data subject (\u201ctitular de datos personales\u201d) and establishes rights and remedies (18)-(25), it does not require an organization to systematically identify \u201cinterested parties\u201d beyond data subjects (e.g., customers, partners, subcontractors, supervisory authorities) and determine their requirements as a management-system input. The Authority\u2019s functions (33) exist, but there is no organizational duty to map stakeholder expectations and decide which will be addressed through a PIMS.\n\nCategory 2: The law/regulation define scope of application (3) and territorial scope (Regl. 5), but they do not require each organization to define and maintain a documented scope of a privacy information management system (PIMS) including boundaries, interfaces, and applicability (e.g., business units, locations, products/services, outsourced processes). There is also no requirement to keep that scope as \u201cdocumented information\u201d as a management-system artifact.\n\nCategory 3: The framework imposes substantive privacy obligations (e.g., principles (4)-(11), security (16), confidentiality (17), processor restrictions (30), third-party/outsourcing controls (Regl. 33)-(38)), but it does not compel establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a PIMS with defined processes and interactions (ISO management system clause 4.4). In other words, compliance duties exist, but not a mandated management system structure.\n\nCategory 4: There is no requirement for \u201ctop management\u201d leadership/commitment, integration into business processes, or ensuring resources for a PIMS. The law assigns obligations to the controller/processor roles (28) and creates a supervisory authority (32)-(33), but it does not impose governance duties on senior leadership comparable to ISO clause 5.1.\n\nCategory 5: The framework requires informing data subjects and allows privacy policies as a means to satisfy the duty of information online (18) and (Regl. 13), but it does not require top management to establish a privacy policy with commitments (compliance + continual improvement), to maintain it as documented information, and to make it available to interested parties as a management-system policy (ISO clause 5.2). \u201cPol\u00edticas de privacidad\u201d here are treated as notice, not as an organizational policy framework.\n\nCategory 6: The law defines roles (controller/processor equivalents) (2)(7), (2)(17) and (Regl. 2)(14), but it does not require assignment and communication of responsibilities/authorities for PIMS roles, nor appointment of a person responsible for ensuring PIMS conformity and reporting performance to top management (ISO clause 5.3). Authority functions (33) are external oversight, not internal role assignment.\n\nCategory 7: The framework does not require a structured risk-and-opportunity planning process for a PIMS, nor a defined privacy risk assessment methodology with criteria, repeatability, statement of applicability, risk treatment plan, risk owners, and documented results (ISO clause 6.1). Although it mandates \u201cmedidas t\u00e9cnicas, organizativas y legales\u201d (9), (16) and detailed security controls (Regl. 39)-(46), these are not tied to a formal risk assessment/treatment lifecycle.\n\nCategory 8: There is no requirement to set measurable privacy objectives, monitor them, communicate them, update them, and plan actions/resources/responsibilities/timelines to achieve them (ISO clause 6.2). The law focuses on compliance with principles and rights (4)-(11), (18)-(23), but not on objective-setting and planning.\n\nCategory 9: The framework does not require planned management of changes to the PIMS (ISO clause 6.3). Some change-related notice duties exist for new processors or changes after consent (18, last paragraphs) and for service providers changing privacy policies requiring consent (Regl. 35)(1), but these are not a general \u201cplanning of changes\u201d requirement for the management system.\n\nCategory 10: There is no obligation for organizations to determine and provide resources for establishing/maintaining/improving a PIMS (ISO clause 7.1). The law does address funding for the Authority (36) and public budgeting (D\u00e9cima), but not organizational resourcing for privacy management.\n\nCategory 11: The framework does not require determining competence needs, ensuring competence via training/experience, and retaining evidence of competence for personnel affecting privacy performance (ISO clause 7.2). It promotes education/culture at a societal level via the Authority (33)(5)-(7), but does not impose competence management requirements on controllers/processors.\n\nCategory 12: There is no requirement that persons under the organization\u2019s control be aware of the privacy policy, their contribution to PIMS effectiveness, and implications of nonconformity (ISO clause 7.3). The law imposes confidentiality (17) and security duties (9), (16), but not awareness programs.\n\nCategory 13: The framework contains extensive external communication/notice requirements to data subjects (18), consent information elements (Regl. 12)(4), and response procedures (Regl. 50)-(59), but it does not require an organization to determine and manage internal/external communications for a PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a systematic communication plan (ISO clause 7.4). It is largely prescriptive notice rather than a communications management process.\n\nCategory 14: While there are documentation-related obligations (e.g., proof of consent burden (Regl. 15), proof of transfer compliance (Regl. 20), logging/traceability (Regl. 39)(2), registration of databases (29), (Regl. 78)-(85)), the framework does not establish a general \u201cdocumented information\u201d control system for a PIMS (creation/update control, versioning, retention/disposition, protection, control of external documents) as required by ISO clause 7.5. Requirements are fragmented and not framed as a unified documentation control regime.\n\nCategory 15: The law requires operational controls for processing (purpose limitation (6), proportionality (7), quality/retention (8), security (9), (16), processor limitations (30), outsourcing controls (Regl. 33)-(38), transfer conditions (19)-(25)), but it does not require an organization to plan, implement, and control PIMS processes with defined criteria, documented confidence, and control of planned/unintended changes, including control of externally provided processes as part of a management system (ISO clause 8.1). Many controls exist, yet not as an integrated operational planning/control framework.\n\nCategory 16: There is no explicit requirement to perform privacy risk assessments at planned intervals or upon significant changes, nor to retain documented results of such assessments (ISO clause 8.2). Security measures are mandated (16), (Regl. 39)-(46), but they are not linked to periodic risk assessment cycles.\n\nCategory 17: The framework does not require implementing a privacy risk treatment plan and retaining documented results of treatment (ISO clause 8.3). It mandates security and confidentiality measures (9), (16), (17) and allows corrective measures by the Authority (38; Regl. 118), but that is enforcement-driven rather than an internal risk treatment planning requirement.\n\nCategory 18: There is no requirement for organizations to define what to monitor/measure, methods, timing, analysis/evaluation, and to evaluate privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness (ISO clause 9.1). The Authority supervises and can audit/fiscalize (33)(19)-(20), (Regl. 98)-(114), but that is external oversight, not internal monitoring and measurement.\n\nCategory 19: The framework does not require internal audits of a PIMS, an audit program, audit criteria/scope, auditor impartiality, reporting, and retention of audit evidence (ISO clause 9.2). The regulation provides for governmental \u201cfiscalizaci\u00f3n\u201d visits (Regl. 108)-(113), which are not internal audits conducted by the organization.\n\nCategory 20: There is no requirement for top management to conduct management reviews of a PIMS at planned intervals with specified inputs/outputs and documented evidence (ISO clause 9.3). The only periodic reporting is by the Authority to the Minister of Justice (32), not by organizations reviewing their own privacy management system.\n\nCategory 21: The framework does not impose a continual improvement requirement for a PIMS (ISO clause 10.1). It provides sanctions, corrective measures, and incentives/attenuants (38), (Regl. 118), (Regl. 126), but these are compliance/enforcement mechanisms rather than a mandated continual improvement cycle.\n\nCategory 22: The framework provides for corrective measures ordered by the Authority (38; Regl. 118) and sanctions for not complying with corrective measures (Regl. 132)(3)(e), but it does not require organizations to run an internal nonconformity and corrective action process (react, root-cause analysis, prevent recurrence, review effectiveness, update PIMS, retain documented information) as per ISO clause 10.2. The approach is primarily external enforcement rather than internal CAPA governance."
