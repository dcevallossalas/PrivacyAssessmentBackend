"Category 0: **Understanding the organization and its context (4.1)**  \nLaw 29733 and its Regulation define roles and scope concepts (e.g., \u201ctitular del banco\u201d and \u201cencargado\u201d in (2)(17) and (2)(7); \u201cresponsable del tratamiento\u201d in Reglamento (2)(14)), but they do **not** require an organization to perform a structured determination of *internal/external issues* affecting a Privacy Information Management System (PIMS), nor to maintain this as a management-system input. The framework is compliance-oriented (principles, rights, obligations) rather than requiring a context analysis that includes governance, strategy, and operational environment as ISO 27701 expects.\n\nAdditionally, ISO 27701:2025 explicitly asks to determine whether **climate change** is a relevant issue; nothing in the Peruvian framework addresses climate change as a contextual factor for privacy management. While the law addresses applicable legal requirements and cross-border protection levels ((11), (15)), it does not require mapping \u201capplicable judicial/administrative decisions\u201d or \u201ccontractual requirements\u201d as part of a formal context determination process.\n\nCategory 1: **Understanding the needs and expectations of interested parties (4.2)**  \nThe Peruvian framework focuses mainly on the *data subject* (\u201ctitular de datos personales\u201d) and the supervisory authority (e.g., rights in (18)\u2013(27), authority functions in (33)), but it does not require an organization to systematically identify *all* \u201cinterested parties\u201d relevant to a PIMS (e.g., customers, partners, subcontractors, joint controllers, etc.) and determine which of their requirements will be addressed through the PIMS. It regulates some third-party relationships (e.g., outsourcing/subcontracting in Reglamento (36)\u2013(38)), but not as a stakeholder-requirements management process.\n\nAlso, ISO 27701 expects interested-party requirements to include not only legal obligations but also contractual and self-imposed objectives. The Peruvian framework mentions contracts mainly as a legal basis/exception ((14)(5), (15)(4)) and as a restriction for processors ((30)), but it does not require capturing and managing contractual privacy requirements as \u201cinterested party expectations\u201d within a PIMS.\n\nCategory 2: **Determining the scope of the PIMS (4.3)**  \nThe law defines the *legal scope of applicability* ((3) and Reglamento (3)\u2013(5)), but it does not require each organization to define and document the *boundaries and applicability* of its own PIMS (processes, locations, products/services, interfaces, and processing activities) as ISO 27701 requires. Legal applicability is not the same as an organization-specific PIMS scope statement.\n\nFurther, ISO 27701 requires the scope to be available as documented information and to explicitly include PII processing. While the framework requires registration of databases and certain information about them (e.g., (29), (34), Reglamento (79)), that is a regulatory registry obligation, not a PIMS scope definition covering all processing contexts and interactions.\n\nCategory 3: **Privacy information management system (4.4)**  \nLaw 29733 imposes privacy principles and obligations ((4)\u2013(12), (28), (16)\u2013(17)), but it does not require organizations to **establish, implement, maintain, and continually improve** a management system with defined processes and interactions. The framework is not structured as a PDCA management system requirement; it is a set of compliance duties and enforcement mechanisms.\n\nAlthough there are elements that resemble system components (e.g., security measures in (9), (16) and Reglamento (39)\u2013(46); registration in (34), Reglamento (76)\u2013(85)), there is no explicit requirement to integrate them into a coherent PIMS with continual improvement cycles.\n\nCategory 4: **Leadership and commitment (5.1)**  \nThe Peruvian framework does not impose explicit duties on **top management** to demonstrate leadership, align privacy with strategic direction, integrate PIMS requirements into business processes, or ensure resources\u2014core ISO 27701 leadership expectations. Obligations are assigned to \u201ctitular del banco\u201d and \u201cencargado\u201d ((9), (16), (28)), but without governance requirements at executive level.\n\nAlso missing are requirements for leadership-driven continual improvement and internal promotion of privacy importance. The law provides enforcement and sanctions ((37)\u2013(40), Reglamento (115)\u2013(133)), but enforcement is not equivalent to leadership commitment mechanisms.\n\nCategory 5: **Privacy policy (5.2)**  \nThe framework requires informing data subjects and allows privacy policies as a means to satisfy the duty of information ((18) and Reglamento (13)), but it does not require an organization-wide **privacy policy** that provides a framework for objectives, includes commitment to meet requirements, and commitment to continual improvement, nor that it be communicated internally and made available as documented information in the ISO sense.\n\nIn short, \u201cpol\u00edticas de privacidad\u201d in (18) / Reglamento (13) are treated mainly as *notice/ transparency tools* for data subjects, not as a management policy instrument mandated for governance and improvement.\n\nCategory 6: **Roles, responsibilities and authorities (5.3)**  \nThe law defines roles such as \u201ctitular del banco,\u201d \u201cencargado,\u201d and \u201cresponsable del tratamiento\u201d ((2)(17), (2)(7), Reglamento (2)(14)), and assigns certain obligations ((28), Reglamento (36)\u2013(38)). However, it does not require top management to assign and communicate internal roles/responsibilities for ensuring PIMS conformity and reporting PIMS performance to top management.\n\nThere is also no requirement for a formal privacy governance structure (e.g., accountable owner, reporting lines, internal authorities) beyond the legal responsibility concepts and contractual restrictions for processors ((30), Reglamento (37)).\n\nCategory 7: **Actions to address risks and opportunities (6.1) + privacy risk assessment process (6.1.2)**  \nPeruvian law requires \u201cmedidas t\u00e9cnicas, organizativas y legales\u201d for security ((9), (16)) and provides detailed security controls in the Regulation (e.g., access control and logs in Reglamento (39), backups in (40)). But it does not require a **privacy risk management framework**: defining risk criteria, risk acceptance criteria, consistent repeatable assessments, identification of privacy risks (including physical/environmental security as a risk domain), and producing a \u201cstatement of applicability\u201d and risk treatment plan as ISO 27701 requires.\n\nIn other words, the framework mandates safeguards and allows the authority to set security requirements ((16)), but it does not require organizations to perform documented, periodic, methodical privacy risk assessments and to plan actions for risks/opportunities and evaluate their effectiveness.\n\nCategory 8: **Privacy objectives and planning to achieve them (6.2)**  \nThe Peruvian framework does not require organizations to set **measurable privacy objectives**, monitor them, communicate them internally, and plan resources/responsibilities/timelines to achieve them. It focuses on compliance with principles ((4)\u2013(12)) and obligations ((28)), not on objective-setting and performance planning.\n\nEven where the authority promotes education and culture ((33)(5)\u2013(7)), that is a public function, not an organizational requirement to define privacy objectives and plans.\n\nCategory 9: **Planning of changes (6.3)**  \nThere is no explicit requirement to plan changes to a PIMS or to privacy controls in a structured way. The framework addresses changes mainly through legal events like new processors or transfers after consent, requiring notice mechanisms ((18) paragraphs 3\u20134), and database modification/cancellation registration (Reglamento (84)). These are not equivalent to ISO\u2019s requirement to manage changes to the management system itself in a planned manner.\n\nThus, change management for privacy governance, processes, and controls (e.g., assessing impacts of organizational/technical changes) is not mandated.\n\nCategory 10: **Resources (7.1)**  \nThe law does not require organizations to determine and provide resources for establishing and improving a PIMS. It imposes obligations (e.g., security measures in (16), Reglamento (39)\u2013(46)) but does not require resource planning or allocation processes.\n\nThe only explicit resource/financing provisions relate to the authority\u2019s funding ((36), D\u00e9cima Disposici\u00f3n Complementaria Final), not to regulated organizations\u2019 internal resourcing for privacy management.\n\nCategory 11: **Competence (7.2)**  \nThe framework does not require organizations to determine competence needs, ensure training/education/experience, or keep documented evidence of competence for personnel affecting privacy performance. It imposes confidentiality duties ((17)) and security measures ((16), Reglamento (39)\u2013(46)), but without competence management requirements.\n\nEven though the authority promotes education and training in society ((33)(5)\u2013(7)), that does not translate into an organizational competence program requirement.\n\nCategory 12: **Awareness (7.3)**  \nThere is no explicit requirement for internal awareness so that persons under the organization\u2019s control understand the privacy policy, their contribution to PIMS effectiveness, and implications of nonconformity. The law focuses on legal compliance and sanctions ((37)\u2013(40), Reglamento (132)), not on internal awareness programs.\n\nConfidentiality obligations ((17)) imply a need for awareness, but the framework does not mandate awareness activities or evidence thereof.\n\nCategory 13: **Communication (7.4)**  \nThe framework strongly regulates **external communication to data subjects** (information duties in (18), consent conditions in (13)(5), Reglamento (12)(4), and mechanisms for new processors/transfers in (18)). However, ISO 27701 requires determining *internal and external communications relevant to the PIMS* (what/when/with whom/how) as a planned communication process. The Peruvian framework does not require an organization-wide communication plan covering all PIMS communications (e.g., with customers, processors, regulators, internal stakeholders).\n\nAlso, it does not require defining communication responsibilities and channels for privacy incidents, performance reporting, or management review outputs (those are ISO management system expectations, not present here).\n\nCategory 14: **Documented information (7.5)**  \nThe Peruvian framework requires certain documentation/records (e.g., proof of consent burden on controller in Reglamento (15); database registration details in Reglamento (79); access logs and procedures in Reglamento (39)(1)\u2013(2); documentation of suppression communications in Reglamento (68)). But it does not establish a general requirement to control documented information for the PIMS (version control, retention/disposition, protection, identification/approval) as a management system discipline.\n\nIn short, documentation exists as compliance artifacts, but ISO\u2019s broader \u201cdocumented information control\u201d system (creation, updating, control, external documents) is not comprehensively required.\n\nCategory 15: **Operational planning and control (8.1)**  \nThe framework mandates operational controls in specific areas (security controls in Reglamento (39)\u2013(46); processor restrictions in (30) and Reglamento (36)\u2013(38); transfer formalization in Reglamento (23), (25)). However, it does not require organizations to plan and control PIMS processes by establishing process criteria, ensuring processes are carried out as planned, controlling planned changes, and reviewing unintended changes as a unified operational control requirement.\n\nAlso, while it requires controlling outsourced processing (e.g., Reglamento (34)\u2013(38)), it does not require a broader operational control framework covering all PII processing operations and their interactions.\n\nCategory 16: **Privacy risk assessment (8.2)**  \nThe Peruvian framework does not require performing privacy risk assessments at planned intervals or upon significant changes, nor retaining documented results of such assessments. Security measures are required ((16), Reglamento (39)\u2013(46)), but they are not tied to a periodic risk assessment cycle.\n\nTherefore, ISO\u2019s explicit operational requirement for scheduled/triggered privacy risk assessments is not aligned with the legal framework.\n\nCategory 17: **Privacy risk treatment (8.3)**  \nSimilarly, there is no requirement to implement a **privacy risk treatment plan** and retain documented information of treatment results. The law requires adopting security measures ((9), (16)) and allows the authority to impose corrective measures (Reglamento (118); (38) second paragraph), but that is enforcement-driven, not an organization\u2019s planned risk treatment process.\n\nISO\u2019s concept of risk owners approving treatment and accepting residual risk is also absent.\n\nCategory 18: **Monitoring, measurement, analysis and evaluation (9.1)**  \nThe framework does not require organizations to define what to monitor/measure regarding privacy performance, methods, timing, analysis, and evaluation of PIMS effectiveness. It provides regulatory supervision and sanctions (e.g., authority functions in (33), fiscalization in Reglamento (98)\u2013(114)), but external enforcement is not equivalent to internal monitoring and measurement.\n\nThere is also no requirement to keep documented evidence of monitoring/measurement results as part of a PIMS performance evaluation cycle.\n\nCategory 19: **Internal audit (9.2)**  \nNo requirement exists for organizations to conduct **internal audits** of a PIMS, define audit programs, select impartial auditors, report results, or retain audit evidence. The framework relies on the authority\u2019s inspections and procedures (Reglamento (98)\u2013(114)), which are external audits/inspections, not internal audit mechanisms.\n\nThus, ISO\u2019s internal audit requirement is not covered.\n\nCategory 20: **Management review (9.3)**  \nThe Peruvian framework does not require top management to periodically review the PIMS for suitability, adequacy, and effectiveness, nor to consider inputs like audit results, nonconformities, performance trends, and changes in context/interested parties. There are procedures for complaints/tutela ((24), Reglamento (73)\u2013(75)) and sanctions (Reglamento (115)\u2013(133)), but no management review cycle.\n\nTherefore, ISO\u2019s governance requirement for management review and documented outputs is not aligned.\n\nCategory 21: **Continual improvement (10.1)**  \nWhile the law\u2019s principles are \u201cenunciative\u201d and can guide interpretation ((12)), and codes of conduct aim to \u201casegurar y mejorar\u201d practices ((31), Reglamento (90)), the framework does not impose a general obligation on organizations to **continually improve** the suitability/adequacy/effectiveness of a PIMS. Improvement is optional (codes of conduct are voluntary: Reglamento (89)(1)) and enforcement-driven rather than system-driven.\n\nISO\u2019s continual improvement requirement is therefore not fulfilled as a mandatory organizational process.\n\nCategory 22: **Nonconformity and corrective action (10.2)**  \nThe framework provides corrective measures mainly through the authority\u2019s enforcement powers (e.g., measures correctivas/cautelares in Reglamento (118); sanctions and corrective measures in (38) second paragraph; escalation for repeated infringements in Reglamento (133)). But it does not require organizations themselves to run an internal nonconformity process: reacting, root-cause analysis, preventing recurrence elsewhere, reviewing effectiveness, and updating the PIMS, with documented evidence.\n\nIn other words, corrective action exists as an external regulatory consequence, not as an internal management-system requirement aligned with ISO 27701\u2019s corrective action cycle."
