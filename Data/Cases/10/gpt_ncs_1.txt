"Category 0: The law defines roles such as \u201ctitular del banco de datos personales\u201d and \u201cencargado\u201d (2)(17), (2)(7) and the regulation adds \u201cresponsable del tratamiento\u201d (Regl. 2)(14), but it does not require an organization to formally determine and document its context, internal/external issues, or systematically identify applicable obligations as management-system inputs (ISO 4.1). There is also no requirement to determine role per processing instance or to separate controller/processor roles in a structured way beyond definitions.\n\nCategory 1: While the framework recognizes the data subject (\u201ctitular\u201d) and their rights (18\u201325) and establishes an authority (32\u201336) plus complaint channels (24), it does not require an organization to identify \u201cinterested parties\u201d broadly (e.g., customers, partners, processors, subcontractors) and determine their requirements as an input to a PIMS (ISO 4.2). The law is rights/obligations-based, not stakeholder-expectations-based, and does not require a systematic mapping of parties and requirements.\n\nCategory 2: The law sets a legal scope of applicability (3) and territorial scope in the regulation (Regl. 5), but it does not require each organization to define and maintain a documented scope for a privacy management system, including boundaries, interfaces, and applicability decisions (ISO 4.3). There is no obligation to publish/maintain \u201cscope\u201d as documented information for a PIMS.\n\nCategory 3: The framework mandates lawful processing principles and obligations (4\u201312), (28), security (16), and enforcement, but it does not require establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a formal Privacy Information Management System with defined processes and interactions (ISO 4.4). Compliance can be achieved ad hoc; no management-system architecture is compelled.\n\nCategory 4: There is no explicit requirement for \u201ctop management\u201d leadership, accountability, integration into business processes, or ensuring resources for a PIMS (ISO 5.1). The law assigns duties to the \u201ctitular\u201d and \u201cencargado\u201d (9), (16), (28) but does not impose governance/leadership behaviors typical of ISO management systems.\n\nCategory 5: The framework requires informing data subjects and may use privacy policies as a mechanism to satisfy the duty to inform (18) and (Regl. 13), but it does not require top management to establish a privacy policy with commitments, framework for objectives, and continual improvement (ISO 5.2). \u201cPolicies of privacy\u201d are treated as notice, not as a management policy.\n\nCategory 6: The law defines roles (2)(7), (2)(17) and responsibilities in general terms (28), and the regulation assigns burdens of proof (Regl. 15), but it does not require an organization to assign and communicate internal roles, responsibilities, and authorities for a PIMS (ISO 5.3), nor to appoint accountable persons for system conformance and reporting to top management.\n\nCategory 7: The framework includes principles like proportionality and security (7), (9), and requires security measures (16) and some controls for outsourced processing (Regl. 34\u201338), but it does not require a structured \u201crisks and opportunities\u201d planning process, risk criteria, risk owners, treatment plans, or a statement of applicability (ISO 6.1). It is largely prescriptive and rights-based rather than risk-management-system-based.\n\nCategory 8: There is no requirement to set measurable privacy objectives, monitor them, communicate them internally, and plan actions/resources/owners/timelines to achieve them (ISO 6.2). The law focuses on compliance duties (28) and authority requirements, not organizational objective-setting.\n\nCategory 9: The framework does not require planned management of changes to the privacy management system (ISO 6.3). While it regulates modification/cancellation of databases in the registry (29) and (Regl. 84), that is about registration updates, not planned PIMS change control.\n\nCategory 10: The law does not require determining and providing resources for establishing/maintaining/improving a PIMS (ISO 7.1). It addresses authority funding (36) and state financing (D\u00e9cima DCF Ley), but not organizational resource planning for privacy management.\n\nCategory 11: There is no obligation to determine competence needs, ensure training/experience, and retain evidence of competence for personnel affecting privacy performance (ISO 7.2). Some promotion of education/culture is assigned to the authority (33)(5)\u2013(7), but that is not an organizational competence management requirement.\n\nCategory 12: The framework does not require awareness programs so personnel understand the privacy policy, their contribution, and implications of nonconformity (ISO 7.3). Confidentiality duties exist (17) and sector duties for telecom operators (Regl. 31\u201332), but not structured awareness requirements.\n\nCategory 13: The law requires communications to data subjects (18) and mechanisms for revocation (Regl. 16) and provides registry publicity (29), (34), but it does not require an organization to determine internal/external communications for a PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) (ISO 7.4). Communications are mandated in specific cases, not managed as a system.\n\nCategory 14: Some documentation is implied/required (e.g., proof of consent burden (Regl. 15), registry information (Regl. 79), security procedures documented for access privileges (Regl. 39)(1), logs and disposal procedures (Regl. 39)(2)), but the framework does not establish a comprehensive documented-information control regime (creation/update/approval/versioning/retention/disposition) for a PIMS (ISO 7.5). Requirements are fragmented and do not cover the full lifecycle of PIMS documentation.\n\nCategory 15: The framework imposes operational controls for processing (principles 4\u201311; obligations (28); processor restrictions (30); outsourcing controls (Regl. 34\u201338); security controls (Regl. 39\u201346)), but it does not require \u201coperational planning and control\u201d as a management-system process with defined criteria, controlled changes, and control of externally provided processes in the ISO sense (ISO 8.1). Controls exist, yet not framed as a systematic process with documented criteria and change review.\n\nCategory 16: The law does not require periodic privacy risk assessments or assessments triggered by significant changes, nor retention of documented results as \u201crisk assessment outputs\u201d (ISO 8.2). Security measures are mandated (9), (16), and detailed controls exist (Regl. 39\u201346), but they are not tied to a formal risk assessment cycle.\n\nCategory 17: The framework does not require implementing a privacy risk treatment plan, documenting treatment results, or obtaining risk owner approval/acceptance of residual risk (ISO 8.3). It provides corrective measures by the authority (38, 118) and security obligations (16), but not an internal risk treatment governance process.\n\nCategory 18: There is no requirement for organizations to monitor, measure, analyze, and evaluate privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness with defined methods and evidence (ISO 9.1). The authority conducts supervision/fiscalization (33)(19)\u2013(20), (Regl. 98\u2013114), but that is external enforcement, not internal performance evaluation.\n\nCategory 19: The framework does not require internal audits of a PIMS, audit programs, auditor independence, reporting, and retention of audit evidence (ISO 9.2). It provides for authority inspections and fiscalization (Regl. 108\u2013113), which are not internal audits.\n\nCategory 20: There is no requirement for top management management reviews of a PIMS at planned intervals with specified inputs/outputs and documented evidence (ISO 9.3). The only \u201creview\u201d mechanisms are regulatory/authority reporting (32: authority reports to minister) and enforcement processes, not organizational management review.\n\nCategory 21: Although the law allows for corrective measures and sanctions (38\u201340) and encourages codes of conduct (31) and authority campaigns (33)(5), it does not require continual improvement of a PIMS (ISO 10.1). Compliance is enforced, but continuous improvement as a management-system obligation is not mandated for organizations.\n\nCategory 22: The framework provides administrative sanctions and allows corrective measures by the authority (38), (118), and defines infringements (Regl. 132), but it does not require an organization\u2019s internal nonconformity handling and corrective action process (react, root cause, prevent recurrence, review effectiveness, update system, retain documented evidence) (ISO 10.2). The model is regulator-driven remediation rather than an internal corrective-action management process."
