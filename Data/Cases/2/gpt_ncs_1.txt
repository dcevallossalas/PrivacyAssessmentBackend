"Category 0: The law defines controller/processor roles ((3)(9), (3)(12)) and even joint controllers (10), but it does not require an organization to *systematically determine and document* its internal/external context as a management-system input (e.g., governance, strategy, internal issues) as ISO 27701 clause 4.1 expects. It also does not require determining the role \u201cper processing instance\u201d or maintaining separate role-specific control sets; it only sets legal definitions and obligations.\n\nIt also does not address the ISO 27701:2025 explicit prompt to consider whether climate change is a relevant issue to the PIMS (no equivalent anywhere).\n\nCategory 1: While the law recognizes data subjects (\u201ctitular\u201d) and the supervisory authority (34\u201335), and provides extensive data subject rights (26\u201333), it does not require an organization to identify *all* relevant interested parties (e.g., customers, suppliers/subprocessors, partners) and their requirements as an input to a PIMS (ISO 4.2). The framework is rights/obligations-based, not a stakeholder-requirements determination process.\n\nThere is also no requirement to decide which interested-party requirements will be addressed through a PIMS, nor to keep this as documented information.\n\nCategory 2: The law sets territorial/material scope of applicability (2) but does not require each organization to define and maintain a *documented scope of its PIMS* (boundaries, applicability, interfaces, exclusions) as ISO 4.3 requires. It also does not require considering internal/external issues and interested-party requirements when setting that scope (no management-system scoping process).\n\nCategory 3: The law compels compliance with privacy principles and operational obligations (4, 9, 16, 26\u201333), but it does not require establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a *privacy information management system* with defined processes and interactions (ISO 4.4). \u201cImprovement\u201d appears in relation to security measures (16) and general diligence (4)(h), but not as a full PDCA management system requirement.\n\nCategory 4: There is no requirement for \u201ctop management\u201d to demonstrate leadership/commitment to a PIMS (ISO 5.1): integrating PIMS into business processes, ensuring resources, promoting continual improvement, etc. The law imposes duties on \u201cresponsable/encargado\u201d (9, 11) and requires a DPO in some cases (18), but does not create explicit top-management accountability and leadership behaviors.\n\nCategory 5: The law references keeping \u201cpolicies\u201d available as an enforceable expectation (44)(8), but it does not require top management to establish a privacy policy meeting ISO 5.2 elements (framework for objectives, commitment to continual improvement, documented/communicated/available to interested parties). The law\u2019s transparency duties (4)(f), (27) are not equivalent to a management-system privacy policy.\n\nCategory 6: The law requires designation of an \u201coficial de protecci\u00f3n de datos\u201d in cases to be set by regulation (18) and defines controller/processor responsibilities (9\u201311), but it does not require assigning and communicating PIMS roles/responsibilities/authorities in the ISO 5.3 sense (including responsibility for ensuring PIMS conformity and reporting PIMS performance to top management). DPO duties/authority, reporting lines, and independence safeguards are largely deferred to regulation (18).\n\nCategory 7: The law is risk-aware (9, 14\u201315) and mandates DPIAs for high-risk processing (14) plus prior consultation (15), but it does not require an organization-wide process to identify \u201crisks and opportunities\u201d for the PIMS and plan actions, integration, and effectiveness evaluation (ISO 6.1.1). It also does not require defining privacy risk criteria, risk acceptance criteria, consistent repeatable assessments, a statement of applicability, or a formal risk treatment plan approval/acceptance workflow (ISO 6.1.2).\n\nDPIA obligations (14) are narrower than ISO\u2019s broader privacy risk assessment program and do not cover the ISO requirement to compare selected controls against Annex A and justify inclusions/exclusions.\n\nCategory 8: The law does not require establishing measurable privacy objectives at relevant functions/levels, monitoring them, communicating them, updating them, and keeping them documented (ISO 6.2). It imposes compliance duties and rights (4\u201333) but does not require objective-setting and planning (what/resources/who/when/how evaluated).\n\nCategory 9: There is no requirement to plan changes to the PIMS (ISO 6.3). The law requires reviewing/updating measures \u201cwhen necessary\u201d (9) and periodic security actions (16), but not a structured change-management process for the privacy management system.\n\nCategory 10: The law does not require determining and providing resources for establishing/maintaining/improving a PIMS (ISO 7.1). It requires the *Agency* to have resources (34), but that is about the regulator, not regulated organizations\u2019 PIMS resourcing.\n\nCategory 11: The law requires the DPO be appointed considering professional qualities/capacity (18) but does not require organizations to determine competence needs for all personnel affecting privacy performance, ensure competence via training/experience, evaluate effectiveness, and retain evidence (ISO 7.2). No general training/competence management obligation is stated.\n\nCategory 12: The law does not require awareness programs ensuring personnel understand the privacy policy, their contribution to PIMS effectiveness, and implications of nonconformity (ISO 7.3). Confidentiality duty exists (4)(j), but that is not an awareness-management requirement.\n\nCategory 13: The law mandates certain external communications (e.g., transparency information (27), breach notification (17), data subject request handling (26\u201333)), but it does not require an organization to determine and plan internal/external communications for the PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a systematic process (ISO 7.4). Communication duties are fragmented and event-based rather than a communications plan.\n\nCategory 14: The law requires some records (e.g., burden to prove consent (6), record of denied requests (27), contracts with processors (11)), but it does not establish a comprehensive documented-information control regime (creation/update controls, versioning, retention/disposition, control of external documents) as ISO 7.5 requires. It also does not require maintaining the full set of documented information needed for PIMS effectiveness (beyond specific legal proofs).\n\nCategory 15: The law requires implementing appropriate technical/organizational measures (9, 16) and controlling processors via contracts (11), but it does not require operational planning and control in the ISO 8.1 sense: defining process criteria, controlling processes against criteria, ensuring documented information to evidence processes carried out as planned, controlling planned changes, and reviewing unintended changes. Supplier/external process control is addressed only partially through processor contracting (11) and international transfer safeguards (19), not as a general operational control framework.\n\nCategory 16: DPIA obligations (14) and risk-based measures (9) exist, but ISO 8.2 requires privacy risk assessments at planned intervals and upon significant changes, with documented results. The law does not mandate periodic risk assessments across the organization; it mandates DPIAs only for certain high-risk processing types (14) and prior consultation in a subset of cases (15). It also leaves DPIA content to regulation (14), so alignment with ISO\u2019s assessment documentation requirements is not ensured by the statute itself.\n\nCategory 17: The law requires implementing measures and allows corrective measures by the authority (42), but it does not require implementing a documented privacy risk treatment plan and retaining documented results of risk treatment (ISO 8.3). The statute lacks the ISO concept of risk owners approving treatment plans and accepting residual risks (part of ISO 6.1.2/8.3 lifecycle).\n\nCategory 18: The law requires periodic security actions including monitoring/review/maintenance/improvement of security measures (16) and provides enforcement/audits by the authority (35)(5), but it does not require the organization to define what to monitor/measure, methods, timing, analysis/evaluation, and to evaluate PIMS effectiveness with documented evidence (ISO 9.1). Regulatory oversight is not equivalent to internal performance evaluation.\n\nCategory 19: The law empowers the Agency to conduct audits (35)(5) and inspections (38)(7), but it does not require organizations to run *internal* audits at planned intervals, define audit programs, ensure auditor objectivity, report results, and retain audit evidence (ISO 9.2). External regulatory audits do not satisfy ISO\u2019s internal audit requirement.\n\nCategory 20: There is no requirement for top management to conduct management reviews of the PIMS at planned intervals with specified inputs/outputs and documented evidence (ISO 9.3). The law focuses on compliance, enforcement, and data subject rights, not internal governance review cycles.\n\nCategory 21: The law references \u201cmejora continua\u201d for security measures (16) and \u201cdiligencia debida\u201d including privacy by design/default and DPIA (4)(h), but it does not require continual improvement of the *PIMS* suitability/adequacy/effectiveness (ISO 10.1) as a management-system obligation. Improvement is not framed as a system-wide PDCA requirement.\n\nCategory 22: The law provides regulatory corrective measures and sanctions (42\u201347) and administrative procedures (53\u201356), but it does not require an organization\u2019s internal nonconformity and corrective action process (react/correct, root cause analysis, prevent recurrence, review effectiveness, update PIMS, retain documented evidence) as ISO 10.2 requires. The statute\u2019s \u201cmedidas correctivas\u201d are imposed by the authority (42) rather than being an internal corrective-action system requirement."
