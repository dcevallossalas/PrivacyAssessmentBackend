"Category 0: Understanding the organization and its context (4.1)  \nLaw 7593/25 defines roles of controller/processor and joint controllers ((3)(9), (3)(12), (10), (11)), and sets applicability criteria ((2)), but it does not require an organization to perform a structured \u201ccontext analysis\u201d of internal/external issues affecting a PIMS (e.g., governance, strategy, business model, risk appetite, supply chain, technology landscape). The law is compliance-oriented (principles and obligations) ((4), (9)) rather than a management-system requirement to identify and maintain a living set of contextual issues and their impacts.  \nAlso, ISO 27701:2025 explicitly requires determining whether climate change is a relevant issue; Law 7593/25 contains no equivalent obligation or consideration (no article addresses climate change or environmental context).\n\nCategory 1: Understanding the needs and expectations of interested parties (4.2)  \nThe law focuses mainly on the data subject (\u201ctitular\u201d) rights and information duties ((26)\u2013(33), (27)) and on the supervisory authority\u2019s powers ((34)\u2013(35)), but it does not require the organization to identify all \u201cinterested parties\u201d relevant to a PIMS (e.g., customers, partners, subcontractors, employees, regulators in other jurisdictions, certification bodies) and determine their requirements as inputs to the system.  \nWhile it mentions processors/subprocessors and contracts ((11)) and promotes codes/certifications ((13), (35)(11)), it does not impose a systematic stakeholder-requirements determination process, nor does it require deciding which requirements will be addressed through the PIMS (a planning/management-system step).\n\nCategory 2: Determining the scope of the PIMS (4.3)  \nLaw 7593/25 defines the legal scope of applicability ((2)) but does not require each organization to define and document the scope/boundaries of its own privacy information management system (processes, locations, products/services, interfaces, exclusions) as \u201cdocumented information.\u201d  \nIn other words, the law sets *where the law applies*, not *how the organization scopes its PIMS* across business units and processing activities, including interfaces with externally provided processes (ISO scope discipline is not mandated).\n\nCategory 3: Privacy information management system (4.4)  \nThe law requires compliance measures and accountability-like capability to demonstrate compliance ((4)(b), (9)), and periodic security actions ((16)), but it does not require establishing, implementing, maintaining, and *continually improving* a formal PIMS with defined processes and interactions (management system architecture).  \nObligations such as DPIA ((14)) and DPO ((18)) are control-like, but they do not amount to a full ISO-style system requirement (process mapping, system governance, PDCA cycle).\n\nCategory 4: Leadership and commitment (5.1)  \nLaw 7593/25 imposes duties on controllers/processors ((9), (11)) and requires policies to be available ((44)(8)), but it does not require \u201ctop management\u201d to demonstrate leadership/commitment, integrate PIMS into business processes, ensure resources, or promote continual improvement as explicit governance duties.  \nThe law is largely silent on executive accountability mechanisms (e.g., management sponsorship, strategic alignment, internal communication of importance), beyond general compliance expectations.\n\nCategory 5: Privacy policy (5.2)  \nThe law implies the existence of privacy policies by sanctioning failure to keep them available ((44)(8)), and it requires transparency information to data subjects ((4)(f), (27)). However, it does not require a top-management-approved privacy policy that includes a framework for objectives and a commitment to continual improvement, nor does it specify internal communication requirements for the policy (ISO requires documented, communicated, and available to interested parties).  \nThus, \u201cpolicy existence\u201d is indirectly referenced, but ISO\u2019s policy content and governance expectations are not fully covered.\n\nCategory 6: Roles, responsibilities and authorities (5.3)  \nThe law defines controller/processor roles ((3)(9), (3)(12), (11)) and introduces the DPO role ((18)), but it does not require an organization-wide assignment and communication of responsibilities/authorities for all PIMS-relevant roles, including responsibility for ensuring PIMS conformity and reporting PIMS performance to top management.  \nDPO requirements are left largely to future regulation ((18)), and there is no ISO-like internal accountability structure mandated across functions (IT, HR, procurement, etc.).\n\nCategory 7: Actions to address risks and opportunities / privacy risk assessment process (6.1)  \nLaw 7593/25 includes risk-based concepts (e.g., \u201cnature, scope, context and purposes\u201d and \u201crisks\u201d in (9); DPIA in (14); prior consultation in (15)), but it does not require a comprehensive privacy risk management framework with defined risk criteria, risk acceptance criteria, repeatable assessment methodology, statement of applicability, and documented risk treatment plan approval by risk owners (all explicit in ISO 27701:2025 6.1.2).  \nAdditionally, ISO requires comparing selected controls against Annex A and justifying inclusions/exclusions; the law does not require a control-catalog mapping or a \u201cstatement of applicability\u201d artifact. Security is addressed generally ((16)) but not as an integrated privacy+information security risk assessment discipline.\n\nCategory 8: Privacy objectives and planning to achieve them (6.2)  \nThe law does not require organizations to set measurable privacy objectives, monitor them, communicate them internally, update them, and keep them as documented information. It focuses on compliance duties and rights ((4), (9), (26)\u2013(33)) rather than objective-setting and planning.  \nEven though it promotes accountability measures ((4)(h), (9)), it does not require planning \u201cwhat will be done, resources, responsible persons, timelines, evaluation\u201d to achieve privacy objectives (ISO planning discipline is missing).\n\nCategory 9: Planning of changes (6.3)  \nLaw 7593/25 does not require planned management of changes to the PIMS (e.g., when introducing new systems, vendors, or processing purposes) beyond specific triggers like DPIA for high-risk processing ((14)) and prior consultation ((15)).  \nISO\u2019s requirement is broader: any changes to the PIMS should be planned and controlled; the law does not impose a general change-management requirement for privacy governance.\n\nCategory 10: Resources (7.1)  \nThe law requires controllers/processors to implement appropriate measures ((9), (16)) and creates a supervisory authority with resources ((34), (36)), but it does not require each organization to determine and provide resources for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and improving a PIMS.  \nResource allocation is implicit in compliance, yet ISO requires an explicit management-system resource determination; the law does not mandate that internal budgeting/staffing be assessed and ensured.\n\nCategory 11: Competence (7.2)  \nApart from requiring that a DPO be appointed based on professional qualities ((18)) and that processors provide \u201csufficient guarantees\u201d ((11)), the law does not require organizations to determine competence needs for personnel affecting privacy performance, provide training/education, and retain documented evidence of competence.  \nISO\u2019s competence management (training plans, evaluation of effectiveness, records) is not established by the law.\n\nCategory 12: Awareness (7.3)  \nLaw 7593/25 does not require awareness programs ensuring that persons under the organization\u2019s control know the privacy policy, their contribution to PIMS effectiveness, and implications of nonconformity.  \nWhile confidentiality duties exist ((4)(j)) and sanctions exist ((44)\u2013(46)), the law does not mandate internal awareness/behavioral controls typical of ISO management systems.\n\nCategory 13: Communication (7.4)  \nThe law mandates external transparency to data subjects ((4)(f), (27)) and breach notification to the authority and, where applicable, the data subject ((17)), but it does not require an organization to define a comprehensive internal/external communication plan for the PIMS (what/when/with whom/how).  \nISO\u2019s communication requirement includes internal governance communications and structured external communications beyond notices and breach reporting; the law is narrower and event/rights driven.\n\nCategory 14: Documented information (7.5)  \nLaw 7593/25 requires certain records in specific contexts (e.g., burden to prove consent (6); record of denied requests (27); contracts with processors (11); DPIA (14) implied; security actions (16) periodic), but it does not establish a general documented-information control framework (creation, review/approval, version control, retention/disposition, protection, control of external documents).  \nISO\u2019s document control requirements (7.5.2\u20137.5.3) are management-system mechanics not covered by the law, which focuses on substantive obligations rather than documentation lifecycle governance.\n\nCategory 15: Operational planning and control (8.1)  \nThe law requires implementing measures and periodic security actions ((9), (16)) and controlling processors via contracts ((11)), but it does not require operational planning with defined process criteria, controlled execution, documented evidence that processes were carried out as planned, and systematic control of planned/unintended changes across PIMS processes.  \nVendor/outsourcing control exists in part ((11), (19)), yet ISO\u2019s broader operational control framework (process KPIs, criteria, change consequence review) is not mandated.\n\nCategory 16: Privacy risk assessment (8.2)  \nThe law requires DPIAs for certain high-risk processing ((14)) and allows/mandates prior consultation when residual risk remains ((15)), but it does not require privacy risk assessments at planned intervals or upon significant changes for *all* processing within scope, nor does it require retaining documented results of periodic risk assessments as a general rule.  \nDPIA is narrower than ISO 8.2: it is triggered by \u201csignificant risk\u201d categories, not a systematic periodic risk assessment program.\n\nCategory 17: Privacy risk treatment (8.3)  \nLaw 7593/25 requires adopting measures to mitigate risks (general accountability in (9); security measures in (16); DPIA leading to mitigation and consultation in (15)), but it does not require a formal privacy risk treatment plan, implementation tracking, and retention of documented results of risk treatment as ISO 8.3 requires.  \nThe law also lacks ISO\u2019s explicit requirement for risk owner approval and residual risk acceptance documentation (linked to 6.1.2(g)).\n\nCategory 18: Monitoring, measurement, analysis and evaluation (9.1)  \nThe law provides for supervisory audits by the authority ((35)(5)) and requires periodic security actions ((16)), but it does not require organizations to define what to monitor/measure, methods, timing, analysis/evaluation, and to keep documented evidence of monitoring results to evaluate PIMS effectiveness.  \nCompliance can be inspected externally, yet ISO requires internal performance evaluation of the PIMS (metrics, trend analysis, effectiveness evaluation), which is not imposed by the law.\n\nCategory 19: Internal audit (9.2)  \nLaw 7593/25 empowers the authority to audit ((35)(5), (38)(7)) but does not require organizations to conduct internal audits at planned intervals, define audit programs, ensure auditor objectivity, report results to management, and retain audit records.  \nExternal regulatory audit capability is not equivalent to ISO\u2019s internal audit requirement; the law does not mandate an internal audit function for privacy.\n\nCategory 20: Management review (9.3)  \nThe law does not require top management to review the PIMS at planned intervals, consider specified inputs (audit results, performance trends, changes in context, stakeholder needs), and document outputs/decisions.  \nAlthough the Agency must produce annual management reports about its own activities ((35)(16)) and the Director reports to Congress ((38)(9)), that is about the regulator, not about each organization\u2019s management review of its PIMS.\n\nCategory 21: Continual improvement (10.1)  \nThe law includes improvement-like language for security measures (\u201cmejora continua\u201d) ((16)) and requires measures to be reviewed/updated ((9)), but it does not require continual improvement of the *overall PIMS* suitability, adequacy, and effectiveness (ISO 10.1).  \nImprovement is framed mainly around security controls and compliance updates, not a systematic continual improvement cycle across governance, processes, documentation, audits, objectives, and performance evaluation.\n\nCategory 22: Nonconformity and corrective action (10.2)  \nThe law provides an enforcement/corrective framework via the authority\u2019s corrective measures ((42)) and sanctions regime ((43)\u2013(47)), but it does not require organizations to run an internal nonconformity and corrective action process (root cause analysis, preventing recurrence, effectiveness review, and documented evidence) as part of a PIMS.  \nCorrective action is regulator-driven (post-violation) rather than an internal management-system mechanism for handling nonconformities against the organization\u2019s own PIMS requirements and ISO requirements."
