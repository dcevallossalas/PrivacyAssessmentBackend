"Category 0: While the law defines controller/processor roles ((3)(9), (3)(12)) and joint controllers (10), it does not require an organization to perform a structured \u201ccontext\u201d determination for a PIMS (internal/external issues, governance, contractual requirements, judicial/administrative decisions) as a management-system input. It also does not require separating scope/controls per role \u201cper processing instance\u201d as ISO expects (role can vary by processing), beyond the definitional and contractual framing in (11).\n\nCategory 1: The law recognizes data subjects and the supervisory authority (e.g., (26), (34)-(35)), but it does not require an organization to systematically identify \u201cinterested parties\u201d and their requirements as an input to a PIMS (e.g., customers, subcontractors, partners) nor to decide which requirements will be addressed through the PIMS. It focuses on legal rights/obligations rather than a stakeholder-requirements process.\n\nCategory 2: The law sets territorial/material applicability ((2)) but does not require each organization to define and document the boundaries/applicability (\u201cscope\u201d) of a privacy information management system, including interfaces, locations, and processing activities included/excluded. There is no requirement that such scope be maintained as documented information as a management-system artifact.\n\nCategory 3: The law compels compliance with privacy principles and obligations (e.g., (4), (9), (16)) but does not require establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a formal PIMS with defined processes and interactions (management system architecture). \u201cImprovement\u201d appears for security measures (16) but not as a system-wide PIMS requirement.\n\nCategory 4: There is no explicit requirement for \u201ctop management\u201d leadership/commitment to a PIMS (policy/objectives integration, resource assurance, promoting continual improvement) comparable to ISO clause 5.1. Obligations are imposed on the \u201cresponsible/processor\u201d ((9), (16), (18)) without governance requirements that map to executive accountability and integration into business processes.\n\nCategory 5: The law references having \u201cpolicies\u201d available as an enforceable expectation ((44)(8)), but it does not define the required content of a privacy policy (commitment to meet requirements, framework for objectives, continual improvement) nor require top management approval, internal communication, and availability to interested parties as ISO 5.2 requires. The policy obligation is also framed as an infringement if absent, not as a structured policy lifecycle requirement.\n\nCategory 6: The law requires designation of a DPO in cases to be set by regulation (18) and mentions representatives (12), but it does not require an organization-wide assignment and communication of responsibilities/authorities for all relevant PIMS roles, including responsibility for PIMS conformity and reporting to top management. DPO duties/authority and reporting lines are largely deferred to regulation (18).\n\nCategory 7: The law is risk-aware (e.g., (9) \u201cnature, scope, context\u2026 risks\u201d; DPIA in (14); prior consultation in (15)), but it does not require a management-system \u201crisks and opportunities\u201d planning process, risk criteria, risk owners, risk acceptance criteria, statement of applicability, or a documented risk treatment plan as ISO 6.1.2 expects. DPIA is limited to \u201csignificant risks\u201d processing types (14) and its content is deferred to regulation, leaving gaps versus ISO\u2019s systematic risk framework.\n\nCategory 8: The law does not require setting measurable privacy objectives, monitoring them, communicating them, updating them, and keeping them as documented information, nor planning actions/resources/responsibilities/timelines to achieve them. It imposes compliance duties (4), (9), (16) but not an objectives-and-planning regime.\n\nCategory 9: The law does not require planned management of changes to the PIMS. While (9) requires measures to be \u201creviewed and updated when necessary\u201d and (16) requires periodic actions and improvement of security measures, it does not establish a controlled change process (planning, assessing consequences, maintaining integrity of the PIMS).\n\nCategory 10: The law does not require determining and providing resources for establishing/maintaining/improving a PIMS. It does require the supervisory authority to have resources ((34)), but that is about the regulator, not organizations implementing a PIMS.\n\nCategory 11: The law does not require determining competence needs, ensuring competence via training/experience, evaluating effectiveness, and retaining evidence of competence for personnel affecting privacy performance. It mentions DPO qualifications generally (18) but not broader competence management.\n\nCategory 12: The law does not require awareness programs ensuring personnel understand the privacy policy, their contribution to PIMS effectiveness, and implications of nonconformity. Confidentiality duties exist ((4)(j)), but that is not an awareness/behavioral control program.\n\nCategory 13: The law mandates certain external communications to data subjects (27) and breach notification (17), and provides channels for complaints (51), but it does not require an organization to determine and manage internal/external communications relevant to a PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a systematic communication plan. It is obligation-driven rather than a communications management process.\n\nCategory 14: The law requires some records (e.g., burden to prove consent (6); record of denied requests (27); contracts with processors (11)), but it does not establish a documented-information control framework (creation/update controls, versioning, retention/disposition, protection, control of external documents) for the PIMS. Requirements are fragmented and do not amount to ISO 7.5 governance.\n\nCategory 15: The law requires implementing measures and controlling processing via contracts and safeguards ((9), (11), (16)), but it does not require operational planning and control of PIMS processes with defined criteria, documented evidence that processes were carried out as planned, and control of planned/unintended changes as a management-system operational control. Supplier/external process control is partially addressed via processor guarantees and contracts (11), but not as a comprehensive \u201cexternally provided processes\u201d control regime.\n\nCategory 16: DPIA obligations (14) and risk-based measures (9) exist, but the law does not require periodic privacy risk assessments at planned intervals or upon significant changes, nor retention of documented results of \u201cprivacy risk assessments\u201d as such. DPIA is triggered by certain high-risk processing types (14) and prior consultation (15), leaving a gap for routine, organization-wide risk assessment cadence.\n\nCategory 17: The law requires mitigation before proceeding in some cases (15) and periodic security improvement (16), but it does not require implementing a formal privacy risk treatment plan with documented results and residual risk acceptance by risk owners. The enforcement model (42) focuses on corrective measures imposed by the authority, not an internal ISO-style risk treatment lifecycle.\n\nCategory 18: The law does not require organizations to define what to monitor/measure, methods, frequency, analysis/evaluation, and to retain evidence of results to evaluate privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness. It provides for regulator audits ((35)(5)) and sanctions (46), but that is external oversight, not internal monitoring/measurement of a PIMS.\n\nCategory 19: The law provides for regulator audits ((35)(5), (38)(7)) but does not require organizations to conduct internal audits at planned intervals, define audit programs, ensure auditor objectivity, report results, and retain audit evidence. ISO\u2019s internal audit function is not compelled.\n\nCategory 20: The law does not require top management to perform periodic management reviews of the PIMS with specified inputs/outputs and documented evidence. Governance review is not addressed for organizations; only the Agency\u2019s reporting/accountability is mentioned ((35)(16), (38)(9)).\n\nCategory 21: The law mentions \u201cimprovement\u201d for security measures (16) and encourages mechanisms like codes/certifications (13), but it does not require continual improvement of the overall PIMS (suitability, adequacy, effectiveness) as a management-system obligation. Improvements are compliance-driven and regulator-driven rather than a structured continual improvement cycle.\n\nCategory 22: The law provides for corrective measures by the authority (42) and sanctions (46), but it does not require an organization\u2019s internal nonconformity and corrective action process (root cause analysis, preventing recurrence, effectiveness review, documented evidence) for PIMS nonconformities. The framework is enforcement-centric (administrative procedure in (53)) rather than an internal corrective-action system."
