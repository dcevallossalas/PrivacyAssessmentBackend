"Category 0: While the law defines controller/processor roles ((4) \u201cResponsable\u201d and \u201cEncargado\u201d; (5)), it does not require organizations to perform a formal \u201ccontext of the organization\u201d determination as a management-system input (e.g., systematic identification of internal/external issues, governance, contractual requirements, and applicable judicial/administrative decisions) as ISO 27701 expects. References to \u201cjurisprudencia aplicable\u201d in the principles ((10)(a)) are general and do not compel a structured context analysis process.\n\nIt also does not address ISO 27701\u2019s explicit requirement to determine whether climate change is a relevant issue (no equivalent in the law). Nor does it require separating roles per processing instance when an organization acts as both controller and processor (the law defines both roles but does not mandate role-scoping per processing activity).\n\nCategory 1: The law recognizes multiple actors in the \u201csystem\u201d ((5)) and provides extensive data subject information rights ((12)\u2013(24)), but it does not require an organization to identify and document \u201cinterested parties\u201d and their requirements as an input to a PIMS (e.g., customers, subcontractors, supervisory authorities, joint controllers) in the ISO 27701 management-system sense.\n\nAlthough it establishes an Authority and complaint channels ((62)\u2013(64), (75)\u2013(76)), it does not compel a systematic process to determine which interested-party requirements will be addressed through the PIMS, nor does it require periodic review of those expectations as a management-system requirement.\n\nCategory 2: The law sets material/territorial scope of applicability ((2)\u2013(3)) but does not require each organization to define and maintain a documented scope statement for its own privacy information management system (boundaries, applicability, interfaces, exclusions) as ISO 27701 clause 4.3 requires.\n\nIt also does not require organizations to ensure the scope explicitly includes all PII processing activities and supporting processes in a management-system scope document; instead, it regulates processing generally.\n\nCategory 3: The law imposes substantive obligations (e.g., security (37), accountability (10)(k), controller obligations (47)) but does not require organizations to establish, implement, maintain, and continually improve a formal PIMS with defined processes and interactions (ISO 27701 clause 4.4). \u201cResponsabilidad proactiva\u201d ((10)(k), (52)) is not framed as a management system requirement.\n\nThere is no explicit requirement for a PDCA-style system, process interaction mapping, or system-level continual improvement mechanisms beyond general accountability language.\n\nCategory 4: The law does not impose explicit \u201ctop management leadership and commitment\u201d duties comparable to ISO 27701 clause 5.1 (e.g., integrating PIMS into business processes, ensuring resources, promoting continual improvement, assigning responsibilities for PIMS performance reporting). It requires designation of a DPO in certain cases ((48)) and gives the DPO direct access to highest executive level ((50)(5)), but that is not the same as mandated top-management leadership behaviors.\n\nAdditionally, the law does not require top management to ensure privacy objectives are compatible with strategic direction or to communicate the importance of conforming to PIMS requirements.\n\nCategory 5: The law requires implementing \u201cpolicies of personal data protection\u201d ((47)(4)) and empowers the Authority to issue guidelines for policy content ((76)(12)), but it does not explicitly require top management to establish a privacy policy meeting ISO 27701\u2019s specific elements (framework for objectives, commitment to meet applicable requirements, commitment to continual improvement) and to make it available as documented information to interested parties.\n\nIt also does not clearly mandate internal communication and external availability of the policy \u201cas appropriate\u201d in the management-system sense; transparency duties to data subjects ((12)) are not equivalent to a PIMS privacy policy requirement.\n\nCategory 6: The law defines roles (controller/processor/DPO) ((4), (5)) and mandates DPO designation in certain cases ((48)) with functions ((49)) and safeguards ((50)). However, it does not require top management to assign and communicate responsibilities and authorities for all relevant PIMS roles, including responsibility for ensuring PIMS conformity and reporting PIMS performance to top management (ISO 27701 clause 5.3).\n\nThe DPO\u2019s role is compliance-focused, but the law does not establish an organization-wide responsibility matrix for PIMS processes, nor does it require formal internal communication of authorities beyond DPO-related provisions.\n\nCategory 7: The law requires risk analysis and methodologies ((40), (47)(5)) and security measures based on risk ((37), (41)), and it mandates DPIAs for high-risk processing ((42)). However, it does not require a comprehensive \u201crisks and opportunities\u201d planning process for the PIMS (ISO 27701 6.1.1), including planning actions, integration into processes, and evaluation of effectiveness as a management-system planning requirement.\n\nIt also does not require a \u201cstatement of applicability\u201d or formal risk acceptance criteria, risk owners, residual risk acceptance, and documented risk treatment plan approval as described in ISO 27701\u2019s management-system risk planning (even if some elements are implied by (37) and (47), they are not compelled in the ISO structure).\n\nCategory 8: The law does not require organizations to set measurable privacy objectives at relevant functions/levels, monitor them, communicate them, update them, and keep them as documented information (ISO 27701 clause 6.2). It focuses on legal compliance obligations and rights ((10)\u2013(24), (47)) rather than objective-setting.\n\nThere is also no requirement to plan how to achieve privacy objectives (what will be done, resources, responsibilities, timelines, evaluation) as a formal planning activity.\n\nCategory 9: The law does not require planned management of changes to the PIMS (ISO 27701 clause 6.3). While it requires privacy by design/default ((39)) and DPIAs prior to high-risk processing ((42)), it does not establish a general requirement to plan and control changes to the privacy management system itself (e.g., organizational changes, process changes, technology changes) in a structured way.\n\nChange management is only indirectly addressed through \u201cdesign\u201d and \u201cprior assessment\u201d concepts, not as a system-level planned change requirement.\n\nCategory 10: The law does not require organizations to determine and provide resources for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a PIMS (ISO 27701 clause 7.1). It requires enabling the DPO with resources ((50)(2)) and training ((50)(3)), but that is narrower than resourcing the entire PIMS.\n\nThere is no explicit obligation to allocate resources for monitoring, internal audits, management review, or continual improvement activities as a management system.\n\nCategory 11: The law includes training obligations for the DPO ((50)(3)) and broader digital education duties for the State/educational entities ((23)\u2013(24)), but it does not require organizations to determine competence needs for all persons doing work under their control that affects privacy performance, ensure competence, and retain documented evidence (ISO 27701 clause 7.2).\n\nIt also does not mandate competence evaluation or effectiveness assessment of training for staff involved in processing, beyond general obligations to implement measures and confidentiality agreements ((47)(10)).\n\nCategory 12: The law does not require awareness programs ensuring personnel are aware of the privacy policy, their contribution to PIMS effectiveness, and implications of nonconformity (ISO 27701 clause 7.3). It mandates confidentiality duties in some contexts ((30)) and confidentiality contracts ((47)(10)), but not systematic awareness.\n\nDigital education rights ((23)) are societal/educational and not an organizational internal awareness requirement tied to a PIMS.\n\nCategory 13: The law mandates transparency to data subjects ((12)) and breach notifications to authorities and data subjects ((43), (46)), but it does not require organizations to determine and manage internal/external communications relevant to the PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a management-system communication plan (ISO 27701 clause 7.4).\n\nIt also does not require internal communication structures for PIMS performance, audit results, or management review outcomes.\n\nCategory 14: The law requires registration of processing information in the National Registry ((47)(12), (51)) and implies documentation for accountability ((10)(k), (59)), but it does not establish a general documented information control framework (creation, updating, approval, version control, retention/disposition, protection) for PIMS documentation as ISO 27701 clause 7.5 requires.\n\nRegistry reporting is not equivalent to internal documented information governance; the law does not compel organizations to control PIMS documents\u2019 lifecycle, access, and integrity in the ISO management-system manner.\n\nCategory 15: The law imposes operational obligations (contracts with processors/third parties (34)\u2013(35), security measures (37), privacy by design/default (39), DPIA (42)), but it does not require \u201coperational planning and control\u201d of PIMS processes with defined criteria, controlled changes, and documented information sufficient to ensure processes are carried out as planned (ISO 27701 clause 8.1).\n\nIt also does not explicitly require control of externally provided processes/products/services relevant to the PIMS beyond contractual clauses for processors/third parties; broader supplier governance for PIMS processes is not mandated.\n\nCategory 16: The law requires risk analysis methodology ((40)) and continuous evaluation of security measures ((37)) and mandates DPIAs for high-risk processing ((42)). However, it does not require periodic privacy risk assessments at planned intervals or upon significant changes, nor does it require retaining documented results of \u201cprivacy risk assessments\u201d as a distinct recurring activity (ISO 27701 clause 8.2).\n\nDPIA obligations ((42)) are conditional and focused on high-risk processing, not a general periodic risk assessment program across the PIMS scope.\n\nCategory 17: The law requires implementing measures to mitigate risks ((37), (41), (47)(7)) but does not require a formal privacy risk treatment plan implementation and retention of documented results of risk treatment (ISO 27701 clause 8.3). It lacks explicit requirements for selecting controls, tracking treatment actions, and documenting treatment outcomes as a management-system artifact.\n\nCorrective measures by the Authority ((65)\u2013(66)) are enforcement tools, not an internal risk treatment planning and documentation requirement.\n\nCategory 18: The law requires continuous verification/evaluation of security measures ((37), (47)(3)) and the Authority keeps statistics on breaches ((76)(14)), but it does not require organizations to define what to monitor/measure, methods, timing, analysis/evaluation, and to retain documented evidence of monitoring results for PIMS effectiveness (ISO 27701 clause 9.1).\n\nThe law\u2019s focus is compliance and security controls rather than a comprehensive privacy performance measurement framework (KPIs, effectiveness evaluation, trend analysis) at the organizational level.\n\nCategory 19: The law mentions audits by accredited auditors and inspections ((47)(14), (54)(3), (76)(4)) and sanctions for not allowing audits ((68)(13), (69)(3)). However, it does not require organizations to conduct internal audits at planned intervals, establish an internal audit program, define audit criteria/scope, ensure auditor impartiality, report results to management, and retain documented audit evidence (ISO 27701 clause 9.2).\n\nExternal/authority-driven audits are not equivalent to an internal audit function required by ISO management systems.\n\nCategory 20: The law does not require top management to perform management reviews of the PIMS at planned intervals with specified inputs/outputs and documented evidence (ISO 27701 clause 9.3). The Authority must present annual reports evaluating the law to the public (General Provision Fifth), but that is a regulator obligation, not an organization\u2019s management review.\n\nThere is no mandated internal review of PIMS suitability/adequacy/effectiveness, nor required decisions/actions resulting from such reviews.\n\nCategory 21: The law encourages continuous review of mechanisms under accountability ((10)(k)) and requires continuous evaluation of security measures ((37)), but it does not require continual improvement of the PIMS as a system (ISO 27701 clause 10.1). The improvement language is tied to security efficiency/effectiveness and accountability, not to a full management system.\n\nIt also lacks requirements to identify improvement opportunities through audits, monitoring, and management review cycles (since those are not mandated internally).\n\nCategory 22: The law provides corrective measures and sanctions by the Authority ((65)\u2013(66), (67)\u2013(72)) and allows complaints and administrative procedures ((62)\u2013(64)). However, it does not require organizations to manage \u201cnonconformity and corrective action\u201d internally in the ISO sense: reacting, root-cause analysis, preventing recurrence, reviewing effectiveness, updating the PIMS, and retaining documented evidence (ISO 27701 clause 10.2).\n\nRegulatory enforcement is not the same as an internal corrective action process; the law does not compel organizations to maintain a CAPA system with documented nonconformities and corrective action records."
