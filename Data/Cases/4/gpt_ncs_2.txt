"Category 0: The law defines roles (controller/processor) and territorial/material scope ((2)-(4)), but it does not require organizations to systematically determine and document internal/external issues affecting a PIMS (e.g., governance, policies, contractual requirements, judicial/administrative decisions) as an ongoing management-system activity. It also does not include the ISO requirement to consider whether climate change is a relevant issue.\n\nCategory 1: While it identifies system actors ((5)) and grants extensive data subject rights ((12)-(24)), it does not require an organization to identify all \u201cinterested parties\u201d and their requirements (e.g., customers, subcontractors, supervisory authorities, partners) and decide which requirements will be addressed through a PIMS. The framework is rights/obligations-based, not a stakeholder-expectations determination process.\n\nCategory 2: The law sets legal applicability boundaries ((2)-(3)), but it does not require each organization to define and maintain a documented PIMS scope statement (boundaries/applicability) as \u201cdocumented information,\u201d nor to review scope when context/requirements change. Legal scope \u2260 management-system scope.\n\nCategory 3: The law imposes compliance obligations and accountability ((10)(k), (47)), but it does not compel establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a formal privacy information management system with defined processes and interactions (i.e., a PIMS as a management system). References to policies and measures ((47)(4)) do not equate to a full PIMS requirement.\n\nCategory 4: There is no explicit requirement for \u201ctop management\u201d leadership and commitment to a PIMS (integration into business processes, ensuring resources, promoting continual improvement) as in ISO 27701 clause 5. The law assigns obligations to the controller/processor and creates a DPO role ((47)-(50)), but does not mandate executive leadership behaviors.\n\nCategory 5: The law requires implementing \u201cpolicies\u201d ((47)(4)) and empowers the Authority to issue guidance on policy content ((76)(12)), but it does not require top management to establish a privacy policy with ISO elements (framework for objectives, commitment to meet requirements, commitment to continual improvement) and to communicate/make it available as documented information.\n\nCategory 6: The DPO role is defined and required in certain cases ((48)-(50)), but ISO\u2019s broader requirement to assign and communicate responsibilities/authorities for all relevant PIMS roles (including responsibility for PIMS conformity and reporting to top management) is not mandated. The law focuses on legal roles (controller/processor/DPO) rather than a full responsibility matrix for a PIMS.\n\nCategory 7: The law requires risk analysis/management methodologies ((47)(5), (40)-(41)) and DPIAs for high-risk processing ((42)), but it does not require a PIMS planning process to address \u201crisks and opportunities\u201d to achieve intended PIMS outcomes, nor risk acceptance criteria, consistent repeatable assessments, a statement of applicability, risk owners, and a documented risk treatment plan as ISO specifies. It also does not require integrating planned actions into PIMS processes and evaluating their effectiveness as a management-system planning cycle.\n\nCategory 8: There is no requirement to set measurable privacy objectives, monitor them, communicate them, update them, and keep them as documented information, nor to plan actions/resources/responsibilities/timelines to achieve them. The law\u2019s accountability principle ((10)(k)) and obligations ((47)) do not translate into ISO-style objectives and planning.\n\nCategory 9: The law does not require planned management of changes to the PIMS. It requires DPIAs \u201cprior to the start\u201d of certain processing ((42)) and security measures \u201ccontinuous\u201d evaluation ((37), (47)(3)), but it does not establish a structured change-planning requirement for the PIMS itself.\n\nCategory 10: The law does not require determining and providing resources for establishing/maintaining/improving a PIMS. It requires enabling the DPO with resources ((50)(2)) and implementing measures ((47)(2)), but not a general resource management requirement tied to a PIMS.\n\nCategory 11: Competence requirements are only indirectly addressed (e.g., training the DPO ((50)(3)) and promoting employee training in binding corporate rules ((58)(11))). There is no general requirement to determine competence needs for all personnel affecting privacy performance, ensure competence, and retain documented evidence.\n\nCategory 12: The law promotes education/digital literacy for the public and children ((23)-(24)) and requires confidentiality commitments ((47)(10)), but it does not require organizational awareness for all persons under the organization\u2019s control about the privacy policy, their contribution to PIMS effectiveness, and implications of nonconformity (ISO 7.3).\n\nCategory 13: The law mandates transparency to data subjects ((12)) and breach notifications ((43), (46)), but it does not require an organization to determine and manage internal/external communications relevant to a PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a systematic communication plan.\n\nCategory 14: The law requires certain records/registrations (e.g., National Registry reporting ((51), (47)(12))) and \u201cdocumented\u201d guarantees for international transfers ((59)), but it does not establish a comprehensive documented information control system (creation/update, approval, version control, access, retention/disposition) for PIMS documentation as ISO 7.5 requires.\n\nCategory 15: The law imposes operational obligations for processing (lawful basis ((7)), processor contracts ((34)-(35)), privacy by design/default ((39)), security measures ((37))), but it does not require operational planning and control in the ISO sense (process criteria, controlled processes, documented information to ensure processes carried out as planned, control of planned changes, and control of externally provided processes as part of a PIMS). Some elements exist for processors/third parties via contracts, but not as a general PIMS operational control framework.\n\nCategory 16: The law requires risk analysis and DPIAs ((40)-(42)) and continuous evaluation of security measures ((37)), but it does not require privacy risk assessments at planned intervals or upon significant changes, based on defined criteria from a formal risk assessment process, with retained documented results in the ISO 8.2 sense. DPIA triggers are narrower and not framed as periodic risk assessment.\n\nCategory 17: The law requires implementing measures to mitigate risks ((47)(7)) and corrective measures by the Authority ((65)-(66)), but it does not require an ISO-style privacy risk treatment plan, implementation of that plan, and retention of documented results of risk treatment (ISO 8.3). Enforcement/corrective measures are not equivalent to a planned risk treatment lifecycle.\n\nCategory 18: The law requires continuous verification/evaluation of security measures ((37), (47)(3)) and the Authority to keep statistics on breaches ((76)(14)), but it does not require organizations to define what to monitor/measure, methods, timing, analysis/evaluation, and to evaluate privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness with documented evidence (ISO 9.1). The focus is compliance and security, not performance measurement of a PIMS.\n\nCategory 19: The law allows/compels audits or inspections by accredited auditors and the Authority ((47)(14), (54)(3), (76)(4)), and penalizes non-cooperation ((68)(13), (69)(3)). However, it does not require organizations to run an internal audit program at planned intervals with defined objectives/criteria/scope, auditor independence, reporting, and documented evidence as an internal PIMS audit function.\n\nCategory 20: The law does not require top management to conduct management reviews of a PIMS at planned intervals with specified inputs/outputs and documented evidence. Annual evaluation reports are assigned to the Authority about the law itself ((Disposiciones Generales Quinta)), not to each organization reviewing its PIMS.\n\nCategory 21: Although the law references continuous review/improvement of mechanisms ((10)(k), (37), (47)(3)) and provides corrective measures and sanctions ((65)-(72)), it does not require continual improvement of the suitability/adequacy/effectiveness of a PIMS as a management system. Continuous security evaluation is narrower than continual improvement of a PIMS.\n\nCategory 22: The law provides for corrective measures by the Authority ((65)-(66)) and sanctions for infringements ((67)-(72)), but it does not require an organization-level nonconformity and corrective action process (react, correct, root-cause analysis, prevent recurrence, review effectiveness, update PIMS) with documented evidence as ISO 10.2 requires. The framework is regulator-driven enforcement rather than an internal corrective action system."
