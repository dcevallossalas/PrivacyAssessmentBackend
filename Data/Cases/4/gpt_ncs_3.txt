"Category 0: Understanding the organization and its context (4.1)  \nThe LOPDP is a legal framework that defines rights, principles, roles and obligations for PII processing (e.g., controller/processor concepts in (4) \u201cResponsable/Encargado\u201d, and territorial/material scope in (2)\u2013(3)), but it does not require an organization to perform a structured \u201ccontext analysis\u201d as a management-system activity. There is no obligation to identify and document internal/external issues, governance context, contractual requirements, applicable judicial/administrative decisions, or to determine whether climate change is a relevant issue (ISO requirement explicitly mentions this). The law focuses on compliance duties (e.g., (47), (37)\u2013(42)) rather than a systematic context determination process.\n\nAlso, while the law defines \u201cresponsable\u201d and \u201cencargado\u201d (4) and regulates their relationship via contracts (34)\u2013(35), it does not require organizations to explicitly determine and document their role \u201cper processing instance\u201d or to separate controls when acting in both roles (controller and processor) as ISO requires. The law assumes role allocation but does not impose the ISO-style scoping/role-mapping exercise.\n\nCategory 1: Understanding the needs and expectations of interested parties (4.2)  \nThe LOPDP recognizes key actors (titular, responsable, encargado, destinatario, authority, DPO) in (5) and grants extensive data subject rights (12)\u2013(24), but it does not require an organization to identify all \u201cinterested parties\u201d relevant to a PIMS (e.g., customers, subcontractors, joint controllers, supervisory authorities as stakeholders with requirements) and to decide which requirements will be addressed through the PIMS. The law is prescriptive about minimum rights/obligations; it does not mandate a stakeholder-requirements analysis or mapping exercise.\n\nAdditionally, ISO expects capturing requirements from contracts and self-imposed objectives (and even climate-change-related expectations). The LOPDP mentions contracts mainly as a legal basis (7)(5) and as a controller\u2013processor governance tool (34)\u2013(35), but it does not require systematically eliciting and managing stakeholder expectations beyond legal compliance and the rights framework.\n\nCategory 2: Determining the scope of the privacy information management system (4.3)  \nThe law defines scope of applicability of the *law* (material and territorial) in (2)\u2013(3), but it does not require an organization to define the boundaries and applicability of a *PIMS* (management system) and keep it as documented information. ISO requires a deliberate scoping decision considering context (4.1) and interested parties (4.2); the LOPDP does not impose such a management-system scoping process.\n\nWhile the law requires certain registers and documentation (e.g., the National Registry reporting in (51) and registration duty in (47)(12)), these are compliance artifacts, not a requirement to define and publish the PIMS scope including all PII processing activities.\n\nCategory 3: Privacy information management system (4.4)  \nThe LOPDP requires organizations to implement privacy measures (e.g., security measures (37), privacy by design/default (39), DPIA (42), policies (47)(4)), but it does not require establishing, implementing, maintaining, and *continually improving* a formal PIMS with defined processes and interactions as ISO/IEC 27701 requires. The law is outcome/obligation-oriented rather than management-system-structure-oriented.\n\nIn other words, compliance with (47) and related duties can be achieved without a documented system of processes, governance cycles, and continual improvement mechanisms; ISO requires that system explicitly.\n\nCategory 4: Leadership and commitment (5.1)  \nThe LOPDP imposes obligations on \u201cresponsable/encargado\u201d and introduces the DPO role (48)\u2013(50), but it does not explicitly require top management to demonstrate leadership and commitment to a PIMS, integrate PIMS requirements into business processes, ensure resources, or promote continual improvement. The closest elements are accountability/proactive responsibility (10)(k) and the duty to implement policies (47)(4), yet these do not assign explicit responsibilities to \u201ctop management\u201d nor require leadership behaviors.\n\nAlso, the law does not require top management to ensure the PIMS achieves intended results or to communicate the importance of privacy management internally (ISO 5.1). It focuses on legal compliance and enforcement (65)\u2013(72), not leadership governance.\n\nCategory 5: Privacy policy (5.2)  \nThe law requires implementing \u201cpolicies of personal data protection\u201d (47)(4) and empowers the Authority to issue guidelines for policy design/content (76)(12). However, it does not require a single overarching privacy policy meeting ISO criteria (framework for objectives, commitment to meet requirements, commitment to continual improvement), nor does it require that policy to be communicated internally and made available to interested parties as documented information.\n\nIn practice, (47)(4) can be satisfied with operational policies without the ISO management-system policy attributes (e.g., explicit continual improvement commitment). The law also does not require policy alignment with strategic direction (ISO 5.2 + 5.1 linkage).\n\nCategory 6: Roles, responsibilities and authorities (5.3)  \nThe LOPDP defines roles (5) and mandates a DPO in certain cases (48), with functions and protections (49)\u2013(50). But it does not require top management to assign and communicate responsibilities and authorities for PIMS conformance and reporting to top management (ISO 5.3). The DPO has advisory/supervisory functions, yet the law does not establish an internal governance model where someone is accountable for PIMS conformity and performance reporting.\n\nAdditionally, while the law requires contracts with processors/third parties (34)\u2013(35) and confidentiality agreements (47)(10), it does not require an organization-wide responsibility matrix or role communication plan across all functions affecting privacy performance.\n\nCategory 7: Actions to address risks and opportunities / Privacy risk assessment (6.1)  \nThe LOPDP includes risk-related duties: security principle (10)(j), continuous evaluation of measures (37), risk/threat/vulnerability analysis (40), determination of measures (41), and DPIA (42). However, ISO 6.1 requires a broader \u201crisks and opportunities\u201d planning for the PIMS (including integration into processes and evaluation of effectiveness) and a formal privacy risk assessment process with risk criteria, risk acceptance criteria, consistency/comparability, statement of applicability, risk treatment plan approval, and residual risk acceptance by risk owners. The law does not require these management-system artifacts (risk criteria, acceptance criteria, SoA, residual risk acceptance).\n\nMoreover, ISO explicitly includes physical and environmental security and encourages mapping controls to Annex A; the LOPDP mentions resilience including physical aspects (37)(3) but does not require a structured control selection/justification process or a statement of applicability. The Authority may request a risk report for legitimate interest (9)(c), but that is case-specific and not a full ISO risk governance cycle.\n\nCategory 8: Privacy objectives and planning to achieve them (6.2)  \nThe LOPDP does not require organizations to set measurable privacy objectives, monitor them, communicate them, update them, and document them as ISO requires. The law mandates compliance actions (e.g., (47), (37)\u2013(43)) and accountability (10)(k), but it does not translate these into an objectives-and-plans framework with assigned responsibilities, timelines, resources, and evaluation methods.\n\nEven where the law promotes education and awareness at societal level (23)\u2013(24), it is not an organizational requirement to define internal privacy objectives and plans.\n\nCategory 9: Planning of changes (6.3)  \nThe law requires privacy by design/default (39) and DPIA prior to high-risk processing (42), which indirectly touches change management for new processing. However, it does not require that changes to a PIMS be carried out in a planned manner (ISO 6.3), nor does it require a formal change-control process for privacy management system changes (e.g., organizational restructuring, new vendors, new technologies) beyond the specific triggers like DPIA.\n\nThus, the LOPDP addresses \u201cbefore starting processing\u201d in certain cases (42) but not systematic PIMS change planning and documentation.\n\nCategory 10: Resources (7.1)  \nThe LOPDP implies resource needs (e.g., implementing measures (37), appointing DPO (48), maintaining registry (51), enabling audits (47)(14)), but it does not explicitly require an organization to determine and provide resources for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a PIMS. ISO requires a proactive resource planning obligation; the law is silent on resource adequacy as a management-system requirement.\n\nThe DPO support obligations (50)(2) mention providing resources for the DPO\u2019s work, but that is narrower than overall PIMS resourcing.\n\nCategory 11: Competence (7.2)  \nThe law includes training/education themes (23)\u2013(24) and requires training the DPO (50)(3), but it does not require an organization to determine competence needs for all personnel affecting privacy performance, ensure competence via education/training/experience, and retain evidence of competence (ISO 7.2). The LOPDP is not a competence management framework.\n\nTherefore, beyond the DPO-specific training duty (50)(3), there is no general requirement for competence management across the workforce and contractors.\n\nCategory 12: Awareness (7.3)  \nThe LOPDP promotes digital education broadly (23) and requires providing information/capacitation for children and guardians (24), but it does not require internal awareness for persons under the organization\u2019s control about the privacy policy, their contribution to PIMS effectiveness, and implications of nonconformity (ISO 7.3). The law focuses on informing data subjects (12) and enabling rights, not internal staff awareness programs.\n\nHence, ISO\u2019s internal awareness obligations are not directly covered by (23)\u2013(24), which are societal/rights-oriented rather than organizational awareness controls.\n\nCategory 13: Communication (7.4)  \nThe law mandates transparency to data subjects (10)(c), detailed information duties (12), and breach notifications to authority and data subjects (43)\u2013(46). However, ISO 7.4 requires the organization to determine internal and external communications relevant to the PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a planned communication process. The LOPDP imposes specific communications but does not require a communication plan covering all PIMS-relevant communications (e.g., internal escalation, supplier communications, audit communications).\n\nSo, while (12) and (43)\u2013(46) cover key external communications, the broader ISO communication management requirement is not addressed.\n\nCategory 14: Documented information (7.5)  \nThe LOPDP requires certain documentation/records (e.g., registry reporting (51), contracts with processors/third parties (34)\u2013(35), evidence of security measures (37), DPIA (42) implicitly, and accountability (10)(k)). But it does not establish a general documented-information control system: document identification, versioning, approval, controlled distribution/access, retention/disposition, and control of external documents (ISO 7.5.2\u20137.5.3).\n\nIn short, the law requires *some* documents to exist, but not a management-system discipline for controlling documented information lifecycle.\n\nCategory 15: Operational planning and control (8.1)  \nThe LOPDP requires implementing controls (security measures (37), privacy by design/default (39), contracts (34)\u2013(35), breach response notifications (43)\u2013(46)), but it does not require operational planning and control in the ISO sense: establishing process criteria, controlling processes against criteria, controlling planned changes, reviewing unintended changes, and controlling externally provided processes as part of a PIMS operational control framework.\n\nVendor/third-party control exists mainly through contractual requirements (34)\u2013(35) and ensuring processor guarantees (47)(11), but ISO expects a broader operational control model with documented process criteria and change control.\n\nCategory 16: Privacy risk assessment (8.2)  \nThe law requires risk/threat/vulnerability analysis (40) and DPIA for high-risk processing (42), but it does not require performing privacy risk assessments at planned intervals or when significant changes occur, nor retaining documented results as a recurring management activity (ISO 8.2). DPIA is triggered by \u201chigh risk\u201d or Authority request (42), not by a periodic schedule.\n\nAlso, the law does not define a repeatable risk assessment methodology producing consistent/comparable results (ISO 6.1.2/8.2 linkage), beyond the general requirement to use a methodology (40).\n\nCategory 17: Privacy risk treatment (8.3)  \nThe LOPDP requires implementing measures to mitigate risks (37), determining measures based on analysis (41), and taking measures to prevent/mitigate vulnerabilities (47)(7). However, it does not require a formal privacy risk treatment plan, implementation tracking, and retention of documented results of risk treatment (ISO 8.3). The law is \u201cdo the measures\u201d rather than \u201cplan\u2013implement\u2013record\u2013verify\u201d in a management-system sense.\n\nAdditionally, ISO expects explicit residual risk acceptance and risk owner approval (6.1.2(g)); the law does not address this governance step.\n\nCategory 18: Monitoring, measurement, analysis and evaluation (9.1)  \nThe law requires continuous evaluation of security measure effectiveness (37) and annual evaluation/review reports by the Authority (General Provision Fifth), but it does not require each organization to define what to monitor/measure, methods, timing, analysis/evaluation, and to evaluate privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness with documented evidence (ISO 9.1). The LOPDP\u2019s monitoring is mainly about security controls and regulatory oversight, not organizational performance measurement.\n\nThus, ISO\u2019s performance evaluation framework (KPIs, measurement plans, trend analysis) is not mandated by (37) or other provisions.\n\nCategory 19: Internal audit (9.2)  \nThe LOPDP mentions audits/inspections by accredited auditors and the duty to allow them (47)(14), and the Authority can perform/delegate audits (76)(4). But ISO 9.2 requires the organization to conduct *internal* audits at planned intervals, define an audit program, ensure auditor objectivity, report results to management, and retain documented evidence. The law focuses on external/regulatory or accredited audits, not an internal audit program.\n\nTherefore, the internal audit mechanism (self-audit governance) is not required by (47)(14) or (76)(4).\n\nCategory 20: Management review (9.3)  \nThe LOPDP does not require top management to periodically review the PIMS for suitability/adequacy/effectiveness, nor to consider inputs like audit results, nonconformities, changes in context, and opportunities for improvement, with documented outputs (ISO 9.3). The only \u201creview\u201d obligation is the Authority\u2019s annual evaluation of the law (General Provision Fifth), which is not an organizational management review.\n\nEven though the DPO must have direct relationship with top executive level (50)(5), that does not equate to a structured management review process.\n\nCategory 21: Continual improvement (10.1)  \nThe law includes a notion of continuous evaluation and improvement of mechanisms under accountability (10)(k) and continuous evaluation of security measures (37). However, ISO 10.1 requires continual improvement of the *PIMS* as a system (suitability, adequacy, effectiveness). The LOPDP does not require a continual improvement cycle across governance, objectives, audits, management reviews, and corrective actions\u2014only continuous review of \u201cmechanisms\u201d mainly tied to compliance and security.\n\nSo, while (10)(k) and (37) support improvement in practice, they do not fully align with ISO\u2019s management-system continual improvement requirement.\n\nCategory 22: Nonconformity and corrective action (10.2)  \nThe LOPDP provides corrective measures imposed by the Authority (65)\u2013(66) and sanctions for infringements (67)\u2013(72), but it does not require an organization to manage nonconformities internally: react, correct, analyze root causes, prevent recurrence, review effectiveness, and update the PIMS, with documented evidence (ISO 10.2). The law\u2019s \u201ccorrective\u201d approach is primarily regulatory enforcement, not an internal corrective action system.\n\nIn addition, ISO expects identification of similar nonconformities and systemic prevention; the LOPDP does not mandate internal CAPA processes, even though it penalizes failures and can impose measures (65)(3)."
