"Category 0: The LFPDPPP defines roles and scope at a legal level (e.g., \u201cresponsible\u201d, \u201cprocessor/encargado\u201d, \u201cthird party\u201d) (2)(XII)-(XVII), but it does not require an organization to systematically determine and document its internal/external context, applicable obligations, and role per processing instance as a management-system activity (ISO 27701/27001 context determination). There is no explicit requirement to identify and track applicable legal/regulatory/contractual requirements as \u201ccontext\u201d inputs, nor to separate controller/processor roles per processing activity.\n\nCategory 1: While the law is centered on the data subject (\u201ctitular\u201d) and ARCO rights (21)-(34), it does not require identifying all \u201cinterested parties\u201d relevant to a PIMS (e.g., customers, partners, regulators, subcontractors) and determining their requirements as an input to the system. It also lacks a requirement to decide which interested-party requirements will be addressed through a formal privacy management system.\n\nCategory 2: The law applies broadly to private entities processing personal data (1) and defines key concepts (2), but it does not require an organization to define and maintain a documented scope for a privacy information management system, including boundaries, interfaces, and exclusions. There is no obligation to publish/retain documented information describing the PIMS scope.\n\nCategory 3: The LFPDPPP imposes substantive privacy obligations (5)-(20), ARCO processes (21)-(34), transfers (35)-(36), and enforcement (38)-(61), but it does not require establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a \u201cprivacy information management system\u201d with defined processes and interactions. The law is compliance-oriented rather than management-system-oriented (no explicit PDCA/PIMS requirement).\n\nCategory 4: There is no explicit \u201ctop management leadership and commitment\u201d requirement comparable to ISO management system clauses (e.g., ensuring integration into business processes, providing resources, promoting continual improvement). The closest is the obligation to \u201cfoster\u201d privacy internally and designate a person/department (29), but it does not map to top-management accountability and leadership behaviors.\n\nCategory 5: The law requires a privacy notice (aviso de privacidad) with specific content (14)-(17), but it does not require a top-management-approved privacy policy that provides a framework for objectives and includes commitments to meet requirements and continual improvement. The \u201caviso\u201d is a transparency instrument to data subjects, not an internal policy statement for a PIMS.\n\nCategory 6: The law requires designation of a person/department to handle ARCO requests (29), and recognizes \u201cencargado\u201d (2)(XII), but it does not require defining and communicating responsibilities/authorities for all PIMS roles (e.g., risk owners, audit roles, management review responsibilities). There is no requirement to assign authority for ensuring PIMS conformity and reporting performance to top management.\n\nCategory 7: The law requires security measures considering risk factors (18) and breach notification (19), but it does not require a formal \u201crisks and opportunities\u201d planning process, risk acceptance criteria, statement of applicability, or a documented risk treatment plan as in ISO 27701/27001. It also lacks explicit requirements to integrate risk actions into processes and evaluate effectiveness as a management planning activity.\n\nCategory 8: The LFPDPPP does not require setting measurable privacy objectives, monitoring them, communicating them internally, and planning actions/resources/timelines to achieve them. While it mandates principles and duties (5)-(20), it does not translate them into an objectives-and-planning framework.\n\nCategory 9: There is no requirement to plan changes to the privacy management system or to carry out changes in a controlled manner. The law does not address change management for privacy controls, systems, or processes (beyond requiring new consent for new purposes (11)).\n\nCategory 10: The law does not require determining and providing resources for establishing/maintaining a PIMS. It imposes obligations (e.g., security measures (18)) but does not require resource planning, budgeting, or ensuring availability of resources for privacy governance.\n\nCategory 11: The law does not require determining competence needs, ensuring competence via training/experience, and retaining evidence of competence. It contains no explicit competence management requirement for personnel involved in processing or privacy functions (beyond general internal fostering (29)).\n\nCategory 12: The law does not require awareness programs ensuring personnel understand the privacy policy, their contribution, and implications of nonconformity. Confidentiality duties exist (20), but there is no structured awareness requirement.\n\nCategory 13: The law mandates providing privacy notices and communicating breaches to affected individuals (14)-(17), (19), but it does not require an organization to determine internal/external communications relevant to a PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a systematic communication plan. It is event- and notice-driven rather than a communications management requirement.\n\nCategory 14: The law requires certain documented artifacts (e.g., privacy notice content (15), mechanisms for revocation (7), confidentiality controls (20)), but it does not require a documented information control system (creation, review/approval, versioning, retention/disposition, protection) for PIMS documentation. There is no equivalent to ISO\u2019s documented information governance requirements.\n\nCategory 15: The law does not require operational planning and control of PIMS processes with criteria, controlled changes, and control of externally provided processes/services as a system requirement. While it regulates transfers and third parties (35)-(36) and requires security measures (18), it does not impose process-control requirements comparable to ISO 27701 clause 8.1.\n\nCategory 16: The law does not require periodic privacy risk assessments, nor documented results at planned intervals or upon significant changes. It references considering risk and consequences for security measures (18) and breach handling (19), but not a repeatable risk assessment process with documented outputs.\n\nCategory 17: The law does not require implementing a documented privacy risk treatment plan, tracking treatment actions, and retaining documented results. It mandates security measures (18) but does not require a structured risk treatment lifecycle aligned to ISO.\n\nCategory 18: The law does not require defining what to monitor/measure, methods, frequency, analysis, and evaluation of privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness. Enforcement and verification are handled by the authority (e.g., verification procedures (54)-(55)), but the organization is not required to run internal monitoring/measurement programs.\n\nCategory 19: The law does not require internal audits of a PIMS, audit programs, auditor independence, audit criteria/scope, and retention of audit evidence. Government verification exists (54)-(55), but that is not an internal audit requirement.\n\nCategory 20: The law does not require top management to conduct management reviews at planned intervals with specified inputs/outputs (audit results, performance trends, nonconformities, opportunities). There is no management review mechanism comparable to ISO 27701/27001.\n\nCategory 21: The law does not require continual improvement of a PIMS. It includes enforcement, sanctions, and some corrective expectations (e.g., breach notification (19), confidentiality (20)), but no explicit continual improvement cycle or requirement to improve suitability/adequacy/effectiveness over time.\n\nCategory 22: The law provides sanctions and enforcement processes (56)-(61) and defines infractions (58), but it does not require an internal nonconformity and corrective action process (root cause analysis, corrective action effectiveness review, documented evidence) as a management system requirement. Regulatory penalties are not equivalent to an internal CAPA process aligned to ISO."
