"Category 0: The LFPDPPP defines roles and scope at a legal level (e.g., \u201cresponsable\u201d, \u201cencargado\u201d, \u201csujetos regulados\u201d in (2)), but it does not require an organization to determine and document its internal/external context, applicable requirements inventory, or role-by-processing-instance determination as a management-system activity (ISO 27701/27001 clause 4.1 style). There is no requirement to identify \u201cissues\u201d affecting the PIMS outcome, nor to determine separate scopes/controls when acting in multiple roles across different processing operations.\n\nCategory 1: The law focuses on the data subject (\u201ctitular\u201d) rights and notices (e.g., (21)\u2013(34), (15)), but it does not require a structured determination of \u201cinterested parties\u201d and their requirements (e.g., customers, partners, processors/subprocessors, regulators) as inputs to a PIMS (ISO clause 4.2). It also does not require documenting which interested-party requirements will be addressed through the management system, beyond compliance with the law itself.\n\nCategory 2: The LFPDPPP applies generally to private entities processing personal data (with exceptions in (1)), but it does not require an organization to define and maintain a documented scope for a privacy information management system, including boundaries, interfaces, and applicability criteria (ISO clause 4.3). There is no explicit requirement to publish/retain a \u201cscope statement\u201d covering processing activities, locations, systems, and third parties.\n\nCategory 3: The law imposes substantive privacy obligations (principles in (5)\u2013(13), security in (18)\u2013(20), rights in (21)\u2013(34)), but it does not require establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a formal PIMS with defined processes and interactions (ISO clause 4.4). It lacks management-system architecture requirements (process mapping, system governance, continual improvement cycle) as a compulsory framework.\n\nCategory 4: While (29) requires designating a person/department to handle ARCO requests, the law does not impose \u201ctop management\u201d leadership and commitment duties (policy alignment with strategy, integration into business processes, ensuring resources, promoting continual improvement) as required by ISO clause 5.1. There is no explicit accountability requirement for senior leadership to ensure PIMS effectiveness.\n\nCategory 5: The law requires an \u201caviso de privacidad\u201d (14)\u2013(17) with specific content (15), but it does not require a top-management-approved privacy policy that provides a framework for objectives and includes commitments to meet requirements and continual improvement (ISO clause 5.2). Notice obligations are transactional and outward-facing; they are not equivalent to an internal policy governing the PIMS.\n\nCategory 6: Beyond appointing a person/department for ARCO handling (29), the law does not require defining and communicating roles, responsibilities, and authorities for a PIMS (e.g., privacy governance roles, risk owners, audit roles, management review responsibilities) as in ISO clause 5.3. It also does not require assigning responsibility for ensuring PIMS conformity and reporting performance to top management.\n\nCategory 7: The LFPDPPP requires \u201cmedidas de seguridad\u201d and to consider risk factors (18) and breach notification (19), but it does not require a formal risk-and-opportunity planning process for the PIMS (ISO 6.1.1), nor a defined privacy risk assessment methodology with criteria, risk acceptance, statement of applicability, and treatment plan (ISO 6.1.2). The law\u2019s risk language is not tied to a repeatable, documented risk management lifecycle.\n\nCategory 8: The law does not require establishing measurable privacy objectives, monitoring them, communicating them, and planning actions/resources/owners/timelines to achieve them (ISO clause 6.2). Obligations exist (e.g., (18) security, (15) notice content), but there is no requirement to translate them into documented objectives and plans.\n\nCategory 9: The law does not require planned management of changes to the PIMS (ISO clause 6.3). While it addresses changing purposes requiring renewed consent (11) and notice changes (15)(VI), it does not require controlled change management for privacy controls, processes, systems, or organizational changes affecting the PIMS.\n\nCategory 10: The law requires implementing security measures (18) and internal fostering of protection (29), but it does not require determining and providing resources for establishing/maintaining/improving a PIMS (ISO 7.1). There is no explicit resource planning requirement (budget, tools, staffing) tied to a management system.\n\nCategory 11: The law does not require determining competence needs, ensuring competence via training/experience, and retaining evidence of competence for personnel affecting privacy performance (ISO 7.2). It mentions organizational fostering and designation (29) but not competence management or documented evidence.\n\nCategory 12: The law does not require awareness programs ensuring personnel understand the privacy policy, their contribution to PIMS effectiveness, and implications of nonconformity (ISO 7.3). Confidentiality duty exists (20), but awareness as a structured requirement is not mandated.\n\nCategory 13: The law requires providing notices and communicating certain items to data subjects (14)\u2013(17) and breach notification (19), but it does not require determining internal/external communications relevant to a PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a systematic communication plan (ISO 7.4). It lacks requirements for internal reporting lines, regulator communication planning, or processor/subprocessor communication governance.\n\nCategory 14: The law does not require a documented information control framework for the PIMS (creation, updating, approval, version control, retention/disposition, protection) as in ISO 7.5. While it requires maintaining security measures (18) and confidentiality (20), it does not mandate documented procedures and controls for PIMS documentation lifecycle.\n\nCategory 15: The law imposes operational obligations (e.g., security measures (18), ARCO handling timelines (31), transfers (35)\u2013(36)), but it does not require operational planning and control of PIMS processes with defined criteria, controlled changes, and control of externally provided processes/services (ISO 8.1). It also lacks explicit requirements to ensure outsourced processes relevant to privacy are controlled as part of a management system (beyond transfer/third-party obligations in (35)).\n\nCategory 16: The law does not require performing privacy risk assessments at planned intervals or upon significant changes, nor retaining documented results (ISO 8.2). Although (18) requires considering risk, consequences, sensitivity, and technology, it does not compel periodic, repeatable assessments with documented outputs.\n\nCategory 17: The law does not require implementing a privacy risk treatment plan and retaining documented results (ISO 8.3). It mandates security measures (18) but does not require a formal treatment plan linked to assessed risks, risk owners, acceptance of residual risk, or documented treatment outcomes.\n\nCategory 18: The law does not require monitoring, measurement, analysis, and evaluation of privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness (ISO 9.1). It provides enforcement mechanisms via the authority (e.g., verification and sanctions in (54)\u2013(61)), but it does not require the organization itself to define KPIs, monitoring methods, schedules, and documented evidence of evaluation.\n\nCategory 19: The law does not require internal audits of a PIMS at planned intervals, with an audit program, criteria, scope, auditor objectivity, reporting, and retained evidence (ISO 9.2). It provides for authority verification (54)\u2013(55), but external regulatory verification is not equivalent to an internal audit requirement.\n\nCategory 20: The law does not require top management to conduct management reviews of the PIMS at planned intervals with specified inputs/outputs and documented evidence (ISO 9.3). There is no requirement for periodic executive review of privacy performance, audit results, nonconformities, or improvement opportunities.\n\nCategory 21: The law does not require continual improvement of the PIMS (ISO 10.1). While it allows/encourages self-regulation schemes with corrective measures (37) and the authority may issue criteria/recommendations (39), it does not compel an organization-wide continual improvement cycle for privacy management.\n\nCategory 22: The law provides enforcement and sanctions (56)\u2013(61) and requires breach notification (19), but it does not require a nonconformity and corrective action process aligned to ISO 10.2 (react, correct, address consequences, root-cause analysis, implement actions, review effectiveness, update the PIMS, retain documented evidence). Sanctioning by the authority is not the same as requiring internal corrective action management and documented evidence of corrective actions."
