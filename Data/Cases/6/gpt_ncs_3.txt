"Category 0: The legal framework defines roles like \u201cresponsable\u201d and \u201cpersona encargada\u201d (e.g., LGPDPSO (3)(XXV)-(3)(XXII); LFPDPPP (2)(XIV)-(2)(XII)), but it does not require an organization to perform a structured \u201ccontext analysis\u201d of internal/external issues affecting a PIMS (e.g., governance context, judicial/administrative decisions, contractual requirements) as a management-system input. It also does not require determining, per processing instance, whether the organization acts as controller, joint controller, or processor with separate role-specific controls (ISO 27701 4.1), beyond the general legal definitions.\n\nIt also does not address the ISO 27701:2025 explicit requirement to determine whether climate change is a relevant issue for the PIMS. No equivalent obligation appears in the cited laws (e.g., LFPDPPP (1)-(4); LGPDPSO (1)-(9)).\n\nCategory 1: The framework recognizes data subjects (\u201ctitular/persona titular\u201d) and establishes rights and procedures (LGPDPSO (37)-(51); LFPDPPP (21)-(34)), but it does not require the organization to systematically identify *all* \u201cinterested parties\u201d relevant to a PIMS (e.g., customers, other controllers/processors, subcontractors, supervisory authorities) and determine their requirements as inputs to the management system (ISO 27701 4.2). The laws focus on legal duties and rights, not on a stakeholder-requirements mapping exercise.\n\nAdditionally, while it mandates notices and transparency (LGPDPSO (20)-(22); LFPDPPP (14)-(17)), it does not require deciding which interested-party requirements will be addressed through the PIMS (a planning/selection step), nor does it mention climate-change-related expectations of interested parties.\n\nCategory 2: The laws define applicability and scope in a legal sense (e.g., LFPDPPP (1) exceptions; LGPDPSO (4) applicability), but they do not require the organization to define and maintain a documented *PIMS scope statement* considering context and interested parties (ISO 27701 4.3). There is no obligation to document boundaries, interfaces, and applicability of the privacy management system as such.\n\nAlso, while \u201ctreatment\u201d is broadly defined (LGPDPSO (3)(XXXI); LFPDPPP (2)(XIX)), the framework does not require scoping to explicitly include all PII processing activities in a management-system boundary document.\n\nCategory 3: The framework imposes substantive compliance obligations (e.g., principles, notices, security, ARCO rights: LFPDPPP (5)-(20), (21)-(34); LGPDPSO (10)-(36), (37)-(51)), but it does not require establishing, implementing, maintaining, and *continually improving* a formal Privacy Information Management System with defined processes and interactions (ISO 27701 4.4). Compliance can be achieved without a management-system architecture.\n\nIn particular, there is no requirement for process interaction mapping, system-level governance, or continual improvement cycles as a system requirement (beyond some security \u201csystem of management\u201d language in LGPDPSO (28), which is limited to security management for personal data, not a full PIMS).\n\nCategory 4: The laws do not impose explicit \u201ctop management leadership and commitment\u201d duties to integrate a PIMS into business processes, ensure resources, communicate importance, and promote continual improvement (ISO 27701 5.1). They assign responsibilities to \u201cresponsable\u201d and require internal mechanisms (e.g., LGPDPSO (24), (27)-(31); LFPDPPP (29)), but not the ISO-style leadership behaviors and accountability to PIMS outcomes.\n\nAlso, there is no requirement that top management ensures privacy objectives and policy are compatible with strategic direction, nor that leadership supports other roles to demonstrate leadership (ISO 27701 5.1).\n\nCategory 5: The framework requires privacy notices (LGPDPSO (20)-(22); LFPDPPP (14)-(17)) but does not require a *privacy policy* meeting ISO 27701 5.2 characteristics (framework for objectives, commitment to meet requirements, commitment to continual improvement), nor that it be maintained as documented information and communicated internally and to interested parties as appropriate.\n\nNotices are directed to data subjects and focus on informing about processing; they are not equivalent to an organization-wide privacy policy statement with management-system commitments.\n\nCategory 6: The laws require designation of certain functions (e.g., LFPDPPP requires a person/department to handle ARCO: (29); LGPDPSO provides for Unidad de Transparencia and possibly an \u201coficial\u201d (79)), but they do not require top management to assign and communicate PIMS roles/responsibilities/authorities to ensure PIMS conformity and report PIMS performance to top management (ISO 27701 5.3). The legal framework is more operational/rights-handling oriented than management-system governance oriented.\n\nAlso, the framework does not require defining \u201cprivacy risk owners\u201d or similar role concepts tied to risk treatment approval and residual risk acceptance (ISO 27701 6.1.2(g)).\n\nCategory 7: The framework includes risk-related elements (e.g., security measures must consider risk: LGPDPSO (26)-(27); LFPDPPP (18)), but it does not require a formal PIMS planning process to identify \u201crisks and opportunities\u201d to achieve intended PIMS outcomes, plan actions, integrate them, and evaluate effectiveness (ISO 27701 6.1.1). Legal compliance does not equal a structured risk/opportunity planning cycle.\n\nWhile LGPDPSO requires risk analysis, gap analysis, and a plan of work for security (27)(IV)-(VI), it does not require the ISO 27701 privacy risk assessment outputs such as risk acceptance criteria, consistent comparable assessments, statement of applicability against Annex A controls, explicit justification for inclusion/exclusion, and formal risk owner approval/acceptance of residual risks (ISO 27701 6.1.2(a)-(h)).\n\nCategory 8: The laws do not require establishing measurable privacy objectives at relevant functions/levels, monitoring them, communicating them, updating them, and keeping them as documented information (ISO 27701 6.2). They require compliance with principles and duties (e.g., LFPDPPP (5); LGPDPSO (10)), but not objective-setting.\n\nThey also do not require planning to achieve objectives (what will be done, resources, responsibilities, timelines, evaluation) as a structured management planning artifact.\n\nCategory 9: The framework does not require \u201cplanning of changes\u201d to the PIMS (ISO 27701 6.3). Although it addresses changes to notices (LFPDPPP (15)(VI), (17); LGPDPSO (21)(IX)) and security document updates upon events (LGPDPSO (30)), it does not require planned change management for the overall privacy management system, including assessing consequences of changes and ensuring planned implementation.\n\nThus, change control is partial and mostly tied to notices/security documentation, not PIMS-wide change planning.\n\nCategory 10: The laws require implementing measures and, in some cases, \u201cdestinar recursos\u201d for data protection programs in the public sector (LGPDPSO (24)(I)), but they do not require a general determination and provision of resources for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a PIMS (ISO 27701 7.1), especially for private-sector controllers/processors under LFPDPPP.\n\nIn short, resource requirements exist as compliance expectations, but not as a management-system resource planning requirement across the organization.\n\nCategory 11: The framework mentions training/capacitation (e.g., LGPDPSO (24)(III), (84); LFPDPPP (39)(X)), but it does not require a competence management process: determining competence needs for roles affecting privacy performance, ensuring competence, taking actions to acquire competence, evaluating effectiveness, and retaining documented evidence (ISO 27701 7.2). Training obligations are not the same as competence assurance and evidence.\n\nAlso, there is no explicit requirement to maintain documented information as evidence of competence (ISO 27701 7.2), beyond general administrative expectations.\n\nCategory 12: The laws do not require an \u201cawareness\u201d program ensuring persons under the organization\u2019s control are aware of the privacy policy, their contribution to PIMS effectiveness, and implications of nonconformity (ISO 27701 7.3). They impose duties and sanctions (e.g., LFPDPPP (58)-(61); LGPDPSO (132)-(137)), but do not mandate awareness objectives and internal messaging.\n\nEven where training is required, awareness elements tied to a PIMS (policy, benefits, implications) are not explicitly required.\n\nCategory 13: The framework mandates certain external communications (privacy notice: LFPDPPP (14)-(17); LGPDPSO (20)-(22); breach notification: LFPDPPP (19); LGPDPSO (34)-(35)), but it does not require the organization to determine and manage *all* internal and external communications relevant to the PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a systematic communication plan (ISO 27701 7.4).\n\nCommunications are prescribed by law for specific events; ISO requires a broader, planned communication process for the management system.\n\nCategory 14: The laws require certain documents (e.g., privacy notice: LFPDPPP (15)-(16); security document: LGPDPSO (29); breach log: LGPDPSO (33)), but they do not require a comprehensive \u201cdocumented information\u201d control system for the PIMS (ISO 27701 7.5), including identification, version control, distribution/access/retrieval, retention/disposition, and control of external documents.\n\nSome document control is implied (e.g., security document updates: LGPDPSO (30)), but ISO\u2019s full documented-information lifecycle controls are not mandated.\n\nCategory 15: The framework does not require operational planning and control of PIMS processes via defined criteria, controlled processes, control of planned changes, and control of externally provided processes/services relevant to the PIMS (ISO 27701 8.1). It regulates certain operational obligations (e.g., contracts with processors: LGPDPSO (53); transfers: LFPDPPP (35)-(36)), but not a process-control regime.\n\nSupplier/outsourcing controls exist in specific contexts (cloud: LGPDPSO (57)-(58)), yet ISO requires broader operational control across all PII processing and supporting processes.\n\nCategory 16: The laws require risk analysis for security measures (LGPDPSO (27)(IV)) and impact assessments for intensive/relevant processing in the public sector (LGPDPSO (68)-(72)), but they do not require performing privacy risk assessments at planned intervals or upon significant changes, retaining documented results, based on criteria established in a PIMS risk process (ISO 27701 8.2). DPIA-like obligations are conditional and not equivalent to periodic risk assessment.\n\nFor private-sector processing under LFPDPPP, there is no explicit DPIA/PIA requirement comparable to ISO\u2019s recurring privacy risk assessment.\n\nCategory 17: The framework does not require implementing a formal privacy risk treatment plan with documented results (ISO 27701 8.3). While it requires implementing security measures and corrective actions after incidents (LGPDPSO (25), (31)), it does not require a structured risk treatment plan derived from a risk assessment, with traceability to selected controls and documented treatment outcomes.\n\nAlso missing are ISO elements like residual risk acceptance by risk owners and treatment effectiveness evaluation as part of the plan (ISO 27701 6.1.2(g), 8.3).\n\nCategory 18: The laws do not require defining what to monitor/measure, methods, timing, analysis/evaluation, and evaluating privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness (ISO 27701 9.1). They include some evaluation concepts (e.g., internal/external supervision/audits in LGPDPSO (24)(V); optional audits LGPDPSO (120)), but not a systematic monitoring and measurement program with documented evidence.\n\nIn particular, \u201cprivacy performance\u201d as a measurable management-system outcome is not required; compliance and enforcement are the focus.\n\nCategory 19: The framework does not require an internal audit program for the PIMS (planned intervals, objectives, criteria, scope, auditor impartiality, reporting, documented evidence) as per ISO 27701 9.2. It mentions audits as a possible mechanism in the public sector (LGPDPSO (24)(V)) and voluntary audits (LGPDPSO (120)), but not mandatory internal audits of a PIMS.\n\nThus, audit is either optional or enforcement-driven, not a required internal assurance function.\n\nCategory 20: The laws do not require top management to conduct periodic management reviews of the PIMS with specified inputs (changes in context, interested parties, performance trends, audit results, nonconformities) and outputs (decisions on improvement and changes) with documented evidence (ISO 27701 9.3). There is no management review construct in the legal framework.\n\nEven where committees exist (e.g., Comit\u00e9 de Transparencia: LGPDPSO (77)-(78)), their functions are not equivalent to top management review of a PIMS.\n\nCategory 21: The framework includes some improvement-like duties (e.g., update security document after improvements: LGPDPSO (30)(II); corrective/preventive actions after incidents: LGPDPSO (31)), but it does not require continual improvement of the *PIMS* suitability, adequacy, and effectiveness (ISO 27701 10.1). Improvements are reactive (incidents) or limited to security controls, not a system-wide continual improvement obligation.\n\nFor private-sector LFPDPPP, continual improvement is not required; compliance is the baseline.\n\nCategory 22: The laws provide sanctions and enforcement (e.g., LFPDPPP (58)-(61); LGPDPSO (132)-(137)), but they do not require an internal nonconformity and corrective action process aligned to ISO 27701 10.2 (react, correct, deal with consequences, root-cause analysis, prevent recurrence, review effectiveness, update PIMS, retain documented information). Enforcement is external and punitive; ISO requires internal corrective action management.\n\nWhile LGPDPSO requires analyzing causes of security breaches and implementing corrective/preventive actions (31), it is limited to \u201cvulneraciones\u201d (security incidents) and does not cover broader PIMS nonconformities (e.g., policy/process failures, audit findings, objective underperformance) nor require documented corrective action evidence across the PIMS."
