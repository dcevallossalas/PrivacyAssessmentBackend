"Category 0: The law defines controller/processor concepts ((4)(H), (4)(K)) and territorial scope (Decree (3)), but it does not require an organization to systematically determine and document internal/external issues affecting a PIMS outcome (e.g., governance, strategy, business model, technology landscape, supply chain, judicial/administrative decisions) as a management-system \u201ccontext\u201d exercise. It also does not require role determination per processing instance (controller vs processor) or separation of roles where both apply, beyond definitions.\n\nCategory 1: While it recognizes data subjects and an authority (URCDP) and provides rights and complaint channels ((13)-(17), (31)-(35), (37)-(45)), it does not require an organization to identify \u201cinterested parties\u201d broadly (customers, partners, subcontractors, regulators) and determine/track their requirements as inputs to a PIMS. There is no obligation to maintain a structured stakeholder-requirements register or to decide which requirements are addressed through a management system.\n\nCategory 2: The framework applies broadly to processing ((3); Decree (2)-(3)) and requires database registration ((29); Decree (15)-(20)), but it does not require an organization to define and maintain a documented scope statement for a PIMS (boundaries, applicability, interfaces, exclusions) as ISO management-system scope. Registration is not equivalent to defining PIMS scope across processes, sites, products/services, and outsourced processing.\n\nCategory 3: The law imposes substantive privacy obligations (principles in (5)-(12), rights in (13)-(17), security in (10); Decree (7)-(8)), but it does not require establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a formal privacy information management system with defined processes and interactions (PDCA structure). Compliance duties exist, yet the management-system architecture is not mandated.\n\nCategory 4: There is no requirement for \u201ctop management\u201d leadership and commitment to a PIMS (integration into business processes, ensuring resources, promoting continual improvement, accountability reporting) as described in ISO 27701 clause 5.1. The law assigns responsibility to the database controller ((12)) but does not impose governance behaviors or leadership actions.\n\nCategory 5: The framework does not require an organization to adopt a documented privacy policy meeting ISO elements (commitment to meet requirements and continual improvement, communication internally, availability to interested parties). It requires notice to data subjects at collection ((13)) and consent documentation ((9); Decree (5)-(6)), but that is not a management-level policy requirement.\n\nCategory 6: Although it defines roles (controller/processor) ((4)(H), (4)(K)) and imposes controller responsibility ((12)), it does not require assignment and communication of internal responsibilities/authorities for PIMS roles (e.g., PIMS manager, audit responsibility, reporting lines) or ensuring PIMS conformity and performance reporting to top management.\n\nCategory 7: The law requires security measures ((10); Decree (7)) and some accountability (controller liable in (12)), but it does not require a privacy risk management framework: defined risk criteria, repeatable assessments, statement of applicability, risk treatment plan approval by risk owners, and documented risk treatment process (ISO 6.1.1/6.1.2). \u201cSecurity\u201d is framed as safeguards, not as a structured risk-and-opportunity planning process.\n\nCategory 8: There is no obligation to set measurable privacy objectives aligned to a privacy policy, monitor them, communicate them, and plan actions/resources/timelines/evaluation (ISO 6.2). The law focuses on compliance with principles and rights ((5)-(17)) rather than objective-setting and planning.\n\nCategory 9: The framework does not require planned management of changes to the PIMS (ISO 6.3), such as assessing impacts of organizational/technical changes on privacy controls and updating documentation. Some update duties exist for data accuracy ((7)) and registry updates (Decree (20)), but not PIMS change planning.\n\nCategory 10: No requirement exists to determine and provide resources for establishing/maintaining/improving a PIMS (ISO 7.1). The law creates and resources a regulator ((31)-(35); Decree Title IV), but does not impose resource planning obligations on controllers/processors.\n\nCategory 11: The law does not require competence management (identify needed competence, ensure training/experience, keep evidence) for personnel affecting privacy performance (ISO 7.2). Confidentiality/secret obligations exist ((11)), but not competence assurance.\n\nCategory 12: There is no explicit requirement for awareness programs ensuring personnel understand the privacy policy, their contribution, and implications of nonconformity (ISO 7.3). Professional secrecy is mandated ((11)), but awareness as a management-system control is not.\n\nCategory 13: The framework mandates certain external communications to data subjects (collection notice (13); breach notice to affected individuals in Decree (8); telecom risk notice (20)) and regulator interactions (registration (29); Decree (15)-(20)), but it does not require an organization to determine and manage internal/external communications relevant to a PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a systematic communications plan (ISO 7.4). It is event/obligation-based rather than a managed communications process.\n\nCategory 14: While it requires documenting consent ((9); Decree (6)) and maintaining registry information (Decree (16), (20)), it does not require comprehensive documented information controls for a PIMS (creation/update approval, versioning, access control for documents, retention/disposition of PIMS documentation) as per ISO 7.5. The law regulates personal data handling, not management-system documentation governance.\n\nCategory 15: The law imposes operational constraints (purpose limitation (8), consent (9), processor limitations (30), security (10); Decree (7)), and requires control of outsourced processing in a limited contractual sense ((30)), but it does not require operational planning and control of PIMS processes with defined criteria, evidence that processes were carried out as planned, and systematic control of externally provided processes/products/services relevant to the PIMS (ISO 8.1). Contractual processor rules exist, yet broader supplier governance and process control are not specified.\n\nCategory 16: There is no requirement to perform privacy risk assessments at planned intervals or upon significant changes, nor to retain documented results (ISO 8.2). The framework\u2019s \u201csecurity\u201d duty ((10); Decree (7)) and breach notification (Decree (8)) do not substitute for periodic, criteria-based privacy risk assessments.\n\nCategory 17: The law does not require implementing a documented privacy risk treatment plan and retaining results (ISO 8.3). It mandates certain controls (e.g., security (10), international transfer restrictions (23), processor data destruction (30)), but not a risk-treatment lifecycle tied to assessed risks and residual risk acceptance.\n\nCategory 18: There is no obligation to monitor/measure/analyze/evaluate privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness with defined methods, timing, and documented evidence (ISO 9.1). The regulator can inspect and sanction ((34)(D), (35); Decree (31)), but that is external oversight, not internal performance evaluation.\n\nCategory 19: The framework does not require internal audits of a PIMS (audit program, objectives/scope/criteria, auditor impartiality, reporting, documented evidence) (ISO 9.2). URCDP inspections ((34)(D); Decree (31)) are not equivalent to an organization\u2019s internal audit function.\n\nCategory 20: There is no requirement for top management to conduct periodic management reviews of a PIMS with specified inputs/outputs and documented results (ISO 9.3). The law establishes regulatory governance (URCDP structure in (31)-(34); Decree Title IV) but not organizational management review.\n\nCategory 21: The law does not impose continual improvement of a PIMS (ISO 10.1). It provides sanctions and enforcement ((35); Decree (31)-(33)) and allows codes of conduct ((36); Decree (36)), but does not require organizations to continually improve suitability/adequacy/effectiveness of a management system.\n\nCategory 22: The framework provides sanctions and judicial remedies ((35), (37)-(45); Decree (31)), but it does not require an internal nonconformity and corrective action process (react, root-cause analysis, prevent recurrence, review effectiveness, update PIMS, keep documented evidence) as per ISO 10.2. Enforcement is externalized to URCDP and courts rather than requiring internal CAPA mechanisms."
