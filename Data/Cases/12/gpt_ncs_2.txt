"Category 0: Law 18.331 defines roles (controller/\u201cresponsable\u201d and processor/\u201cencargado\u201d) in (4)(H) and (4)(K), and sets territorial applicability in the Decree (3). However, it does not require an organization to systematically determine and document internal/external issues affecting a PIMS (e.g., governance, strategy, business model, technology landscape, supply chain, judicial/administrative decisions) as a management-system \u201ccontext\u201d exercise, nor to review it periodically (ISO 27701/27001 clause-4 style). It also does not require determining role per processing activity (controller vs processor per instance) beyond definitions.\n\nCategory 1: The framework recognizes data subjects (\u201ctitular\u201d) and establishes an authority (31) and its functions (34), but it does not require an organization to identify and manage \u201cinterested parties\u201d and their requirements as an ongoing management-system input (e.g., customers, partners, subcontractors, regulators, employees) nor to decide which requirements will be addressed through a PIMS. It focuses on legal rights/obligations (13\u201317) rather than stakeholder expectation management, and does not impose a structured process to capture/track those expectations.\n\nCategory 2: The law applies broadly to processing (3) and sets exclusions (3) and territorial scope in the Decree (3), but it does not require an organization to define and maintain a documented scope of a privacy information management system (boundaries, interfaces, locations, products/services, processing activities) as required by ISO management system clauses. There is no obligation to publish/retain \u201cscope\u201d as documented information for a PIMS.\n\nCategory 3: The framework is a compliance law with principles (5) and obligations (e.g., security (10), registration (29)), but it does not require establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a PIMS with defined processes and interactions (the management system itself). It lacks requirements for PDCA-style system operation, process ownership, and continual improvement mechanisms as a system.\n\nCategory 4: There is no requirement for \u201ctop management\u201d leadership and commitment to a PIMS (e.g., integrating PIMS into business processes, ensuring resources, promoting continual improvement). The law assigns legal responsibility to the database controller (12) and creates a regulator (31\u201335), but does not impose governance duties on senior leadership comparable to ISO clause 5.1.\n\nCategory 5: While the law requires informing data subjects at collection (13) and consent documentation (9), it does not require top management to establish and maintain a documented privacy policy with commitments (compliance + continual improvement) and to communicate it internally and to interested parties as appropriate. The framework is principle-based (5) but not a policy-management requirement.\n\nCategory 6: The law defines roles (4)(H), (4)(K) and imposes duties on \u201cresponsable/usuario\u201d (10) and \u201cencargado\u201d (Decree 7\u20138; law 30), but it does not require an organization to assign and communicate internal responsibilities/authorities for a PIMS (e.g., PIMS manager, audit roles, reporting lines). There is no explicit requirement for accountability structure beyond legal liability (12).\n\nCategory 7: The framework requires security measures (10; Decree 7) and sets principles (5), but it does not require a formal privacy risk assessment process with defined criteria, repeatability, risk owners, risk acceptance, statement of applicability, and a documented risk treatment plan (ISO 27701 clause 6.1.2). It also does not require planning actions to address risks/opportunities for the PIMS as a system; it is largely obligation-driven rather than risk-management-system driven.\n\nCategory 8: There is no requirement to set measurable privacy objectives, monitor them, communicate them, and plan actions/resources/timelines to achieve them (ISO clause 6.2). The law sets compliance duties and rights (5\u201323; Decree 5\u201314) but not organizational objective-setting and planning.\n\nCategory 9: The framework does not require planned management of changes to the PIMS (or to privacy controls) when changes are needed (ISO clause 6.3). While registration updates are required (Decree 20) and databases must be registered/updated, this is not equivalent to planned change control for the management system and its processes.\n\nCategory 10: The law requires adopting security measures (10; Decree 7) but does not require determining and providing resources for establishing/maintaining/continually improving a PIMS (ISO clause 7.1). It does not address resourcing for governance, training, audits, monitoring, or improvement activities as a system.\n\nCategory 11: There is no explicit requirement to determine competence needs, ensure competence via training/experience, and retain evidence of competence for personnel affecting privacy performance (ISO clause 7.2). The law imposes confidentiality duties (11) but does not mandate competence management.\n\nCategory 12: The framework does not require awareness programs ensuring personnel understand the privacy policy, their contribution to PIMS effectiveness, and implications of nonconformity (ISO clause 7.3). It imposes secrecy obligations (11) but not structured awareness requirements.\n\nCategory 13: Although it mandates communications to data subjects at collection (13), and breach notification to affected individuals in the Decree (8), it does not require an organization to determine and manage internal/external communications relevant to a PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a planned communication process (ISO clause 7.4). Communications are prescribed in specific scenarios rather than governed by a communication plan.\n\nCategory 14: The law requires documenting consent (9) and maintaining registration information (29; Decree 16), but it does not establish a general documented-information control system (creation, review/approval, versioning, retention/disposition, access control) for PIMS documentation (ISO clause 7.5). Requirements are fragmented (e.g., proof of consent in Decree 6) and do not cover comprehensive document control.\n\nCategory 15: The framework includes operational obligations (e.g., purpose limitation (8), security (10), processor limits (30), registration (29)), but it does not require operational planning and control of PIMS processes with criteria, controlled changes, and control of externally provided processes/services in a management-system sense (ISO clause 8.1). Processor provisions (30) are narrow and do not amount to full supplier/process control for all externally provided services relevant to privacy.\n\nCategory 16: There is no requirement to perform privacy risk assessments at planned intervals or upon significant changes, nor to retain documented results (ISO clause 8.2). The law focuses on compliance principles and security measures (10; Decree 7) without mandating periodic, documented risk assessment cycles.\n\nCategory 17: The framework does not require implementing a documented privacy risk treatment plan and retaining results (ISO clause 8.3). While it requires security measures (10; Decree 7) and allows regulator sanctions (35), it does not impose structured risk treatment planning, approval, and documentation.\n\nCategory 18: The law provides for regulator inspections and oversight (34)(D) and sanctions (35), but it does not require the organization to monitor, measure, analyze, and evaluate privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness with defined metrics/methods/frequency and documented evidence (ISO clause 9.1). Compliance is enforced externally rather than requiring internal performance evaluation.\n\nCategory 19: The framework empowers the authority to inspect (34)(D) and request information (34)(E), but it does not require organizations to conduct internal audits at planned intervals, define audit programs, ensure auditor objectivity, and retain audit evidence (ISO clause 9.2). External supervision is not a substitute for internal audit requirements.\n\nCategory 20: There is no requirement for top management to conduct periodic management reviews of the PIMS, with specified inputs (audit results, performance trends, changes in context/needs) and documented outputs (ISO clause 9.3). The law establishes governance for the regulator (31\u201334; Decree 21\u201328) but not management review within regulated organizations.\n\nCategory 21: The law does not require continual improvement of a PIMS (ISO clause 10.1). It includes general principles (5) and sanctions (35) and allows codes of conduct (36), but does not impose a continual improvement cycle for organizational privacy management.\n\nCategory 22: The framework provides sanctions (35) and judicial remedies (37\u201345), but it does not require an organization to manage nonconformities and corrective actions in a structured way (react, root-cause analysis, prevent recurrence, review effectiveness, update PIMS) with documented evidence (ISO clause 10.2). Enforcement and litigation mechanisms do not equate to an internal corrective action system."
