"Category 0: The law defines controller/processor-like roles ((4)(H), (4)(K)) and sets territorial applicability in the decree (Decreto 414/2009 (3)), but it does not require an organization to systematically determine and document its internal/external context, applicable \u201crequirements universe\u201d (laws, regulations, judicial/administrative decisions, contracts), or to separate and document controller vs processor roles per processing activity as a management-system context exercise (ISO 27701/27001 clause-4 style). There is also no requirement to assess whether climate change is a relevant issue (explicitly present in ISO/IEC 27701:2025 clause 4.1).\n\nCategory 1: While the framework is strongly oriented to data subjects (\u201ctitular\u201d) rights ((13)-(17); Decreto 414/2009 (9)-(14)) and establishes a supervisory authority (URCDP) ((31)-(35); Decreto 414/2009 (21)-(28)), it does not require the organization to identify all \u201cinterested parties\u201d relevant to a PIMS (e.g., customers, partners, subcontractors, joint controllers, employees as internal interested parties) and determine their requirements, nor to decide which requirements will be addressed through a PIMS (a clause-4.2 management-system requirement). The law provides obligations and rights, but not a structured stakeholder/expectations determination process.\n\nCategory 2: The law applies broadly to personal data processing ((3); Decreto 414/2009 (2)) and requires database registration with detailed content ((29); Decreto 414/2009 (15)-(20)), but it does not require an organization to define and maintain a documented \u201cscope\u201d of a privacy information management system (boundaries, applicability, interfaces, exclusions) as required by ISO management system clauses. Registration is not equivalent to defining PIMS scope across processes, locations, products/services, and roles.\n\nCategory 3: The framework imposes substantive privacy obligations (principles in (5)-(12); rights in (13)-(17); security in (10); processor restrictions in (30); security measures in Decreto 414/2009 (7)-(8)), but it does not compel establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a formal PIMS with defined processes and interactions (clause 4.4). There is no requirement for a management system lifecycle, governance model, or continual improvement loop as a system.\n\nCategory 4: There is no explicit requirement for \u201ctop management\u201d leadership and commitment to a PIMS (integration into business processes, ensuring resources, promoting improvement, etc.). The law assigns legal responsibility to the \u201cresponsable\u201d ((12)) and creates a regulator ((31)-(35)), but does not impose internal executive leadership duties comparable to ISO clause 5.1.\n\nCategory 5: The law requires informing data subjects at collection ((13)) and sets principles (including responsibility (12) and security (10)), but it does not require top management to establish and maintain a documented privacy policy, communicate it internally, and make it available to interested parties as appropriate (ISO clause 5.2). Codes of conduct are optional ((36); Decreto 414/2009 (36)), not a mandatory organizational privacy policy.\n\nCategory 6: Although it defines roles such as \u201cresponsable\u201d and \u201cencargado\u201d ((4)(K), (4)(H)) and regulates processor behavior ((30); Decreto 414/2009 (14)), it does not require the organization to assign and communicate internal responsibilities and authorities for PIMS roles (e.g., PIMS manager, audit roles, risk owners) or require reporting on PIMS performance to top management (ISO clause 5.3). The framework focuses on legal accountability rather than internal role governance.\n\nCategory 7: The law includes general principles and security obligations ((5)(E), (10); Decreto 414/2009 (7)), but it does not require a structured process to identify risks/opportunities, define privacy risk criteria, perform consistent repeatable privacy risk assessments, produce a statement of applicability, create a risk treatment plan, obtain risk owner acceptance, and retain documented information (ISO clause 6.1 and 6.1.2). The regulator can inspect and sanction ((34)(D), (35); Decreto 414/2009 (31)), but that is external enforcement, not internal risk management planning.\n\nCategory 8: There is no requirement to set measurable privacy objectives, monitor them, communicate them, and plan actions/resources/responsibilities/timelines to achieve them (ISO clause 6.2). The law mandates compliance with principles and rights ((5)-(17)) but does not require objective-setting as a management practice.\n\nCategory 9: The framework does not require planned management of changes to the PIMS (ISO clause 6.3). While it requires registration updates (Decreto 414/2009 (20)) and sets deadlines for database adequacy ((46); Decreto 414/2009 (17)), it does not establish a change-management process for privacy controls, processes, or system changes.\n\nCategory 10: The law requires adopting necessary security measures ((10); Decreto 414/2009 (7)) but does not require determining and providing resources for establishing/maintaining/improving a PIMS (ISO clause 7.1). Security measures are an outcome obligation, not a resourcing requirement for a management system.\n\nCategory 11: There is no requirement to determine competence needs, ensure competence via training/experience, take actions to acquire competence, and keep evidence (ISO clause 7.2). The framework creates an authority and procedures, but does not impose workforce competence management on controllers/processors.\n\nCategory 12: The law imposes confidentiality duties ((11)) and requires certain notices to data subjects ((13)), but it does not require internal awareness for persons under organizational control about the privacy policy, their contribution to PIMS effectiveness, and implications of nonconformity (ISO clause 7.3). Professional secrecy is not the same as PIMS awareness.\n\nCategory 13: The framework mandates specific external communications to data subjects (collection notice (13); breach notice to affected individuals in Decreto 414/2009 (8); telecom risk notice (20)) and provides regulator publicity rules (Decreto 414/2009 (25)), but it does not require the organization to determine and plan internal/external communications relevant to a PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a systematic communication process (ISO clause 7.4). Communications are prescribed in limited scenarios rather than managed comprehensively.\n\nCategory 14: The law requires documenting consent ((9); Decreto 414/2009 (6)) and registration information ((29); Decreto 414/2009 (16)), but it does not require a documented-information control system (creation/update controls, approval, versioning, retention/disposition, protection, control of external documents) for the PIMS (ISO clause 7.5). Registry obligations are not equivalent to organization-wide document control.\n\nCategory 15: The framework regulates processing operations and imposes constraints (purpose limitation (8), consent (9), security (10), processor limitations (30), international transfers (23); Decreto 414/2009 (7), (34)-(35)), but it does not require operational planning and control of PIMS processes with defined criteria, controlled changes, and control of externally provided processes/services as part of a PIMS (ISO clause 8.1). Processor rules exist, yet broader supplier/outsourcing control as a PIMS operational control requirement is not mandated.\n\nCategory 16: There is no requirement to perform privacy risk assessments at planned intervals or upon significant changes, nor to retain documented results (ISO clause 8.2). The law relies on compliance with principles and regulator oversight ((34)(D); Decreto 414/2009 (31)) rather than mandating periodic internal risk assessment.\n\nCategory 17: The law does not require implementing a privacy risk treatment plan and retaining documented results (ISO clause 8.3). It mandates certain controls (e.g., security (10); Decreto 414/2009 (7)) but does not require a risk-based selection and documented treatment of risks.\n\nCategory 18: The framework provides enforcement and sanctions ((35); Decreto 414/2009 (31)-(33)) and requires some operational responses (e.g., correct inaccurate data (7); update credit data (22); breach notification (Decreto 414/2009 (8))), but it does not require the organization to define what to monitor/measure, methods, timing, analysis/evaluation, and to evaluate privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness with documented evidence (ISO clause 9.1). Compliance duties are not a monitoring-and-measurement program.\n\nCategory 19: There is no requirement for internal audits of a PIMS, audit program planning, auditor independence, reporting, and documented evidence (ISO clause 9.2). The regulator can inspect ((34)(D); Decreto 414/2009 (31)(A)), but external inspection is not an internal audit function.\n\nCategory 20: The law does not require top management to conduct periodic management reviews of the PIMS, consider specified inputs (changes in context, interested parties, performance trends, audit results), and document outputs (ISO clause 9.3). Regulatory governance (URCDP structure in (31)-(34); Decreto 414/2009 (21)-(24)) is not an organization\u2019s internal management review.\n\nCategory 21: Although the law aims to protect data and allows codes of conduct ((36)) and requires databases to adapt ((46)), it does not require continual improvement of a PIMS\u2019s suitability/adequacy/effectiveness (ISO clause 10.1). The framework is compliance-oriented, not improvement-oriented.\n\nCategory 22: The framework provides sanctions and procedures for enforcement ((35); Decreto 414/2009 (31)) and judicial remedies (habeas data (37)-(45)), but it does not require an internal nonconformity and corrective action process (react, root-cause analysis, prevent recurrence, review effectiveness, update PIMS) with documented evidence (ISO clause 10.2). Legal liability and external remedies do not substitute for an internal corrective-action system."
