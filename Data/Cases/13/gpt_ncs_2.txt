"Category 0: Law 25.326 defines roles such as \u201cresponsable\u201d and \u201cusuario\u201d (2) but it does not require an organization to formally determine and document its context, internal/external issues, or whether it acts as controller vs processor per processing activity (as ISO 27701/management-system clause 4.1 expects). It also does not require identifying applicable \u201cadministrative/judicial decisions\u201d or contractual requirements as part of a context analysis, nor any separation of roles when acting in multiple capacities.\n\nCategory 1: While it recognizes data subjects (\u201ctitular\u201d) and creates a supervisory authority (\u201c\u00f3rgano de control\u201d) (2), (29), it does not require an organization to identify all \u201cinterested parties\u201d and their requirements, nor to decide which requirements will be addressed through a PIMS (4.2). The law is rights/obligations-based (e.g., (6), (14)-(16)) but lacks a structured stakeholder/expectations determination process.\n\nCategory 2: The law applies broadly to personal data in files/banks and certain actors (1), (2), (44), but it does not require an organization to define and maintain a documented scope for a privacy information management system, including boundaries, interfaces, and applicability criteria (4.3). Registration requirements (21) are not equivalent to defining PIMS scope.\n\nCategory 3: Law 25.326 imposes substantive privacy obligations (e.g., (4)-(12), (25)) but does not require establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a management system with defined processes and interactions (4.4). There is no PDCA-style system requirement, only compliance duties and enforcement mechanisms.\n\nCategory 4: There is no requirement for \u201ctop management\u201d leadership, integration into business processes, resource commitment, or promotion of continual improvement (5.1). The law assigns obligations to \u201cresponsable/usuario\u201d (9), (10) but does not impose governance expectations on senior leadership.\n\nCategory 5: The law requires notice to data subjects about purposes, recipients, controller identity, and rights (6), but it does not require an internal, documented privacy policy with commitments and a framework for objectives, communication within the organization, and availability to interested parties as a policy artifact (5.2). Notice/consent mechanics (5)-(6) are not the same as a management policy.\n\nCategory 6: The law does not require defining and communicating internal roles, responsibilities, and authorities for a PIMS (5.3), nor assigning responsibility for system conformance and reporting to top management. It defines external legal roles (2) but not internal accountability structures (e.g., DPO-like function, audit responsibility).\n\nCategory 7: Security obligations exist (9) and some purpose/quality principles reduce risk (4), but the law does not require a formal risk-and-opportunity planning process, risk criteria, repeatable privacy risk assessments, a statement of applicability, risk owners, or documented risk treatment planning/acceptance (6.1.1\u20136.1.2). The law is prescriptive (e.g., (12) adequacy for international transfers) rather than requiring systematic risk management.\n\nCategory 8: The law does not require setting measurable privacy objectives, monitoring them, planning actions/resources/owners/timelines, or maintaining documented objectives (6.2). Compliance duties (e.g., (9), (21)) are not framed as objectives with planning and evaluation.\n\nCategory 9: There is no requirement to plan changes to the privacy management system (6.3). Some lifecycle rules exist (e.g., destruction when no longer necessary (4)(7); destruction after service completion (25)(2)), but not change management for processes, controls, or the PIMS.\n\nCategory 10: The law does not require determining and providing resources for a PIMS (7.1). It mandates outcomes (e.g., adopt technical/organizational measures (9)(1)) without requiring resource planning, allocation, or evidence of adequacy.\n\nCategory 11: There is no competence requirement (7.2): no obligation to identify needed skills, train personnel, evaluate effectiveness, or keep evidence. Confidentiality duties (10) do not address competence management.\n\nCategory 12: The law does not require awareness programs (7.3) ensuring personnel understand the privacy policy, their contribution, and implications of nonconformity. It imposes secrecy (10) and penalizes certain acts (32) but does not require structured awareness.\n\nCategory 13: The law requires specific external communications to data subjects at collection (6) and provides mechanisms for access/rectification requests (14)-(16), but it does not require an organization to determine and manage internal/external communications relevant to a PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a systematic communication plan (7.4). It also does not address internal escalation/communication channels.\n\nCategory 14: Some documentation is implied/required (e.g., consent recorded (5)(1); registration content (21); public creation rules for public databases (22)), but the law does not require a documented-information control system (creation, review/approval, versioning, retention/disposition, protection, control of external documents) for the PIMS (7.5). Registration is not equivalent to controlled PIMS documentation.\n\nCategory 15: The law imposes operational constraints (purpose limitation (4)(3), data minimization (4)(1), security (9), processor-like limits (25)(1)-(2)), but it does not require operational planning and control of PIMS processes with criteria, controlled changes, and control of externally provided processes/services in a management-system sense (8.1). For example, (25) restricts service providers but does not require broader supplier governance, monitoring, or documented operational controls.\n\nCategory 16: The law does not require performing privacy risk assessments at planned intervals or upon significant changes, nor retaining documented results (8.2). Security duties (9) and transfer restrictions (12) are not framed as periodic, criteria-based risk assessments.\n\nCategory 17: The law does not require implementing a documented privacy risk treatment plan and retaining results (8.3). It mandates certain treatments (e.g., security measures (9), destruction (4)(7), (25)(2)) but lacks a risk-treatment methodology, selection/justification of controls, residual risk acceptance, and evidence of implementation outcomes.\n\nCategory 18: There is no requirement for monitoring/measurement/analysis/evaluation of privacy performance or PIMS effectiveness (9.1), nor to keep documented evidence. The supervisory authority can inspect and enforce (29)(1)(d)-(f), but that is external oversight, not an internal performance evaluation system.\n\nCategory 19: The law provides for supervisory control and judicial actions (29), (33)-(43), but it does not require internal audits planned at intervals, audit programs, auditor independence, reporting, and audit records (9.2). External enforcement is not a substitute for internal audit.\n\nCategory 20: The law does not require top management management reviews at planned intervals with specified inputs/outputs and documented evidence (9.3). Nothing in (29)-(31) compels organizations to conduct internal management reviews of a PIMS.\n\nCategory 21: While the law encourages compliance and allows codes of conduct (30) and imposes sanctions (31)-(32), it does not require continual improvement of a privacy information management system (10.1). Obligations like data updating (4)(4) are about data accuracy, not system improvement.\n\nCategory 22: The law provides sanctions and judicial remedies (31)-(32), (33)-(43), but it does not require an internal nonconformity and corrective action process (10.2): identifying nonconformities, root-cause analysis, implementing corrective actions, reviewing effectiveness, updating the PIMS, and retaining documented evidence. Legal liability and enforcement do not equate to a structured corrective-action mechanism."
