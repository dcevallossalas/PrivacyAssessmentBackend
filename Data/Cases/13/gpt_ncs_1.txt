"Category 0: Law 25.326 defines roles like \u201cresponsable\u201d and \u201cusuario\u201d (2), but it does not require an organization to systematically determine its internal/external context, applicable obligations, and role per processing activity (controller vs processor) as a management-system exercise. It also does not require separating roles when acting in multiple capacities, nor any structured context review mechanism (e.g., periodic reassessment of legal/judicial/contractual requirements). Climate change relevance is not addressed.\n\nCategory 1: While the law recognizes data subjects (\u201ctitular\u201d) and provides rights (13\u201320), it does not require an organization to identify and document \u201cinterested parties\u201d and their requirements (e.g., customers, processors/subprocessors, supervisory authorities) as inputs to a PIMS. There is no obligation to determine which interested-party requirements will be addressed through a management system, nor to maintain an ongoing expectations/requirements register.\n\nCategory 2: The law applies broadly to personal data in files/banks (1) and requires registration of certain databases (21, 24), but it does not require an organization to define and document the scope/boundaries of a privacy information management system (PIMS) (e.g., business units, locations, products, processing activities). It also does not require scope determination to consider internal/external issues and interested-party requirements as a formal scoping exercise.\n\nCategory 3: Law 25.326 imposes substantive privacy obligations (e.g., consent (5), security (9), confidentiality (10)), but it does not require establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a PIMS with defined processes and interactions. There is no management-system requirement comparable to ISO\u2019s clause 4.4.\n\nCategory 4: The law does not impose \u201ctop management\u201d leadership and commitment duties for a PIMS (e.g., integration into business processes, ensuring resources, promoting continual improvement). Obligations are framed as legal compliance duties of \u201cresponsable/usuario\u201d (9, 10, 25) rather than governance commitments by leadership.\n\nCategory 5: Although the law requires notice to data subjects about purposes, recipients, controller identity, and rights (6), it does not require an internal, documented \u201cprivacy policy\u201d set by top management, communicated internally, and made available to interested parties as appropriate. The notice duty (6) is not equivalent to a management policy framework for objectives and continual improvement.\n\nCategory 6: The law does not require assignment and communication of internal roles, responsibilities, and authorities for operating a PIMS (e.g., accountability for conformity, reporting to top management). It defines external legal roles (2) and creates a supervisory authority (29), but does not mandate internal governance structures (e.g., privacy officer responsibilities).\n\nCategory 7: The law requires security measures (9) and limits processing (4, 5, 7, 11, 12), but it does not require a formal process to identify risks/opportunities, define risk criteria, perform repeatable privacy risk assessments, produce a statement of applicability, or create/approve a risk treatment plan. There is no requirement to document risk acceptance criteria, risk owners, or residual risk acceptance.\n\nCategory 8: The law does not require organizations to set measurable privacy objectives, monitor them, communicate them internally, or plan resources/timelines for achieving them. Compliance duties exist (e.g., data quality (4), security (9)), but there is no objectives-and-planning framework.\n\nCategory 9: The law does not require planned management of changes to the PIMS or to processing operations (e.g., change control, assessing privacy impacts of changes). Some obligations touch lifecycle (e.g., destruction when no longer necessary (4)(7), post-contract destruction (25)(2)), but not systematic change planning.\n\nCategory 10: The law mandates adopting \u201ctechnical and organizational measures\u201d for security (9)(1), but it does not require determining and providing resources for establishing/maintaining/continually improving a PIMS. Resource planning is not addressed as a management requirement.\n\nCategory 11: The law imposes confidentiality duties on persons involved in processing (10), but it does not require determining competence needs, ensuring competence via training/experience, or keeping documented evidence of competence for personnel affecting privacy performance.\n\nCategory 12: The law does not require awareness programs ensuring personnel understand the privacy policy, their contribution to PIMS effectiveness, and implications of nonconformity. Confidentiality obligations (10) are not the same as structured awareness requirements.\n\nCategory 13: The law requires specific external communications to data subjects at collection (6) and provides mechanisms for access/rectification (14\u201316), but it does not require an organization to determine and manage internal/external communications relevant to a PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a systematic communications plan.\n\nCategory 14: The law requires database registration content (21)(2) and imposes certain record-like duties (e.g., notifying rectification to transferees (16)(4)), but it does not require a documented-information control system (creation, review/approval, version control, retention/disposition, protection) for PIMS documentation. It also does not require maintaining documented information for many management-system elements (risk assessment, audits, reviews, objectives).\n\nCategory 15: The law compels operational controls for processing (purpose limitation (4)(3), consent (5), security (9), processor limits (25), international transfers (12)), but it does not require process criteria, controlled implementation, documented evidence that processes were carried out as planned, or control of externally provided processes/services as part of a PIMS operational planning framework. Processor provisions (25) are narrow and do not establish broader supplier governance expected by ISO management controls.\n\nCategory 16: The law does not require periodic privacy risk assessments or assessments upon significant change, nor retention of documented results. It focuses on compliance rules and rights enforcement (14\u201316, 33\u201343) rather than proactive, repeatable risk assessment.\n\nCategory 17: The law does not require implementing a documented privacy risk treatment plan, tracking treatment results, or retaining documented evidence of treatment effectiveness. Security measures are required (9), but not within a risk-treatment lifecycle aligned to ISO 27701.\n\nCategory 18: The law does not require monitoring/measurement/analysis/evaluation of privacy performance or PIMS effectiveness, nor documented evidence of monitoring results. Oversight is primarily external via the control authority (29) and sanctions (31\u201332), not internal performance evaluation.\n\nCategory 19: The law provides for external control and inspections by the authority (29)(1)(d)-(h), but it does not require organizations to conduct internal audits at planned intervals, define audit programs, ensure auditor objectivity, report results to management, or retain audit documentation.\n\nCategory 20: The law does not require top management to perform periodic management reviews of a PIMS, consider inputs (audit results, performance trends, changes in context), and document decisions/actions. There is no internal review cycle comparable to ISO clause 9.3.\n\nCategory 21: While the law encourages compliance and provides enforcement and remedies (31\u201332, 33\u201343) and allows voluntary codes of conduct (30), it does not require continual improvement of a PIMS\u2019s suitability/adequacy/effectiveness. The legal framework is compliance-oriented rather than continuous-improvement oriented.\n\nCategory 22: The law provides sanctions (31\u201332) and judicial remedies (33\u201343), but it does not require an internal nonconformity and corrective action process (root-cause analysis, preventing recurrence, effectiveness review, documented evidence). It lacks the management-system corrective-action loop expected by ISO 27701."
