"Category 0: The law defines controller/processor roles (\u201cresponsable\u201d and \u201ctercero mandatario o encargado\u201d) (2)(n), (2)(x), and territorial scope (1 bis), but it does not require an organization to perform a structured \u201ccontext\u201d determination (internal/external issues, governance, contractual requirements, applicable decisions) as an input to a management system (ISO 4.1). There is no obligation to document such context analysis or to separate roles per processing instance (e.g., where the same entity is controller for some processing and processor for others), beyond definitional role allocation.\n\nCategory 1: While the law is strongly rights-based and recognizes data subjects (\u201ctitular\u201d) and the supervisory authority (Agencia) (4)-(11), (30), it does not require an organization to systematically identify all \u201cinterested parties\u201d and their requirements (e.g., customers, partners, subcontractors, employees, regulators) and decide which requirements are addressed through a PIMS (ISO 4.2). The law mandates certain communications to the public/data subjects (14 ter) and breach notifications (14 sexies), but not a broader stakeholder-needs determination process.\n\nCategory 2: The law sets legal scope of application (1), territorial scope (1 bis), and applies to processing generally, but it does not require an organization to define and maintain a documented PIMS scope statement (boundaries/applicability) as ISO 4.3 expects. There is no explicit requirement to document scope, include all PII processing in scope, or manage scope changes as a management-system artifact.\n\nCategory 3: The law imposes substantive privacy obligations (principles (3), duties (14\u201315 ter), security (14 quinquies), DPIA (15 ter), etc.) and creates enforcement (30\u201343), but it does not compel establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a \u201cprivacy information management system\u201d with defined processes and interactions (ISO 4.4). The optional \u201cmodelo de prevenci\u00f3n\u201d (49) is voluntary and framed as compliance/mitigation, not a mandatory PIMS.\n\nCategory 4: There is no explicit \u201ctop management leadership and commitment\u201d requirement comparable to ISO 5.1 (e.g., integrating PIMS into business processes, ensuring resources, promoting continual improvement). Some governance expectations exist for public bodies\u2019 heads (44) and for certain autonomous bodies to issue internal policies (54), but private-sector top management leadership is not mandated as a management-system duty.\n\nCategory 5: The law requires a publicly available \u201cpol\u00edtica de tratamiento\u201d and related disclosures (14 ter(a)), but it does not require top management to establish a privacy policy meeting ISO 5.2 elements (framework for objectives, commitment to continual improvement, commitment to meet applicable requirements) nor to ensure internal communication and controlled availability as documented information. The policy is treated mainly as transparency content, not as a management-system policy.\n\nCategory 6: The law provides for a DPO/delegate role (50) and optional compliance model elements (49(a)-(b)), but it does not require organizations to assign and communicate responsibilities/authorities for all relevant PIMS roles (ISO 5.3), including responsibility for PIMS conformance and reporting to top management. The delegate is optional (\u201cpodr\u00e1 designar\u201d) (50), and even where adopted, the law does not require a full RACI-style allocation across the organization.\n\nCategory 7: The law is risk-aware (security measures proportional to risk (14 quinquies), DPIA for high risk (15 ter), breach notification based on \u201criesgo razonable\u201d (14 sexies)), but it does not require a comprehensive \u201crisks and opportunities\u201d planning process for the PIMS (ISO 6.1.1), nor documented risk criteria, risk acceptance criteria, statement of applicability, risk owners\u2019 approval, and a formal risk treatment plan as ISO 6.1.2 describes. DPIA (15 ter) is narrower than an organization-wide privacy risk management framework.\n\nCategory 8: The law does not require setting measurable privacy objectives, monitoring them, communicating them, updating them, and documenting them (ISO 6.2). Even the voluntary compliance program (49) focuses on preventing infringements and internal protocols, not on establishing objectives and plans with evaluation criteria.\n\nCategory 9: The law does not require planned management of changes to a PIMS (ISO 6.3). While \u201cprivacy by design and by default\u201d (14 qu\u00e1ter) implies considering privacy during design, it does not impose a formal change-management process for the management system or for processing changes beyond triggering DPIA in certain cases (15 ter).\n\nCategory 10: The law does not require determining and providing resources for establishing/maintaining a PIMS (ISO 7.1). It requires the controller to adopt measures (14 quinquies) and to provide the delegate with sufficient means if appointed (50), but there is no general resource-planning obligation tied to a management system.\n\nCategory 11: The law references competence indirectly (delegate must have \u201cidoneidad, capacidad y conocimientos\u201d (50)) and training/formation as a delegate function (50)(d), but it does not require the organization to determine competence needs for all personnel affecting privacy performance, ensure competence, and retain evidence (ISO 7.2). Training is not mandated organization-wide; it is contingent on having a delegate and is not framed with documented evidence requirements.\n\nCategory 12: The law does not impose a general awareness program (ISO 7.3) ensuring personnel understand the privacy policy, their contribution, and implications of nonconformity. Some internalization exists via requiring compliance-program rules to be incorporated into employment/service contracts or internal regulations (49, paragraph after (49)(g)), but the program itself is voluntary and not equivalent to an awareness requirement for all organizations.\n\nCategory 13: The law mandates certain external communications (public transparency (14 ter), breach reporting to Agency and sometimes data subjects (14 sexies), contact channels (10), (14), (14 ter(c))), but it does not require an organization to determine and plan internal/external communications relevant to a PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a systematic process (ISO 7.4). Communications are prescribed as legal notices rather than a managed communications plan.\n\nCategory 14: The law requires keeping some records/evidence (e.g., proof of responding to rights requests (11), breach notification records (14 sexies), written data transfer/cession contracts (15), processor contracts (15 bis)), but it does not establish a general documented-information control framework (creation, updating, approval, version control, retention/disposition) for a PIMS (ISO 7.5). Requirements are fragmented and do not cover the full lifecycle control of PIMS documentation.\n\nCategory 15: The law imposes operational duties (lawful basis (12)-(13), transparency (14 ter), security (14 quinquies), processor controls (15 bis), DPIA (15 ter)), but it does not require \u201coperational planning and control\u201d in the ISO 8.1 sense (process criteria, controlled processes, documented confidence that processes were carried out as planned, control of planned/unintended changes, and control of externally provided processes as part of a PIMS). Processor contracting (15 bis) is a partial analogue, but not a full operational-control clause.\n\nCategory 16: The law requires DPIA for high-risk processing (15 ter) and risk-based security (14 quinquies), but it does not require periodic privacy risk assessments at planned intervals or upon significant changes, using defined criteria, with retained documented results (ISO 8.2). DPIA is event-driven and limited to \u201calto riesgo\u201d scenarios; it is not a recurring, organization-wide risk assessment regime.\n\nCategory 17: The law requires implementing security measures (14 quinquies) and may require mitigation measures identified in DPIA guidance (15 ter), but it does not require a formal privacy risk treatment plan implementation with documented results (ISO 8.3). The compliance model (49) is voluntary and not explicitly tied to risk treatment outputs, residual risk acceptance, or treatment effectiveness documentation.\n\nCategory 18: The law does not require monitoring/measurement/analysis/evaluation of privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness (ISO 9.1). It provides enforcement and sanctions (34\u201338) and requires some recordkeeping (14 sexies), but it does not compel organizations to define KPIs, measurement methods, schedules, or evaluation of system effectiveness beyond legal compliance.\n\nCategory 19: The law does not require internal audits of a PIMS (ISO 9.2), audit programs, auditor independence, reporting, or retention of audit evidence. The Agency\u2019s external oversight (30 bis(c), (42)) is regulatory supervision, not an internal audit requirement.\n\nCategory 20: The law does not require top management to conduct management reviews of a PIMS at planned intervals with specified inputs/outputs and documented evidence (ISO 9.3). Some governance/accountability exists (e.g., Agency annual public account (30 ter(f)); public bodies\u2019 heads must ensure compliance (44)), but there is no organization-level management review obligation.\n\nCategory 21: The law does not impose continual improvement of a PIMS (ISO 10.1). It encourages prevention and compliance (48) and offers incentives/mitigation via certified models (49\u201353), but these do not equate to a mandatory continual improvement cycle; they are compliance and enforcement mechanisms.\n\nCategory 22: The law provides corrective measures through enforcement (Agency can order measures to remedy causes of sanctions (35, paragraph 2)) and allows suspension of processing as an accessory sanction (38), but it does not require an organization to manage \u201cnonconformity and corrective action\u201d internally per ISO 10.2 (react, correct, analyze root cause, prevent recurrence, review effectiveness, update PIMS, retain documented evidence). The law\u2019s approach is regulator-driven remediation rather than an internal corrective-action system."
