"Category 0 (Understanding the organization and its context): Law 21.719 defines legal roles (controller/processor) via \u201cresponsable\u201d and \u201ctercero mandatario o encargado\u201d (2)(n), (2)(x) and sets territorial applicability (1 bis), but it does not require an organization to *systematically determine and document* internal/external context issues for a PIMS (e.g., governance, policies, procedures, contractual requirements, administrative/judicial decisions) as a management-system activity. The law is compliance-oriented (principles/obligations in (3), (14), (15 bis)) rather than requiring a structured \u201ccontext analysis\u201d process.\n\nIt also does not address the ISO requirement to explicitly consider whether *climate change* is a relevant issue for the PIMS (no equivalent anywhere in (1), (1 bis), (3), (14 ter)). Finally, it does not require separation of roles \u201cper processing instance\u201d (ISO note) beyond definitional distinctions; it lacks an obligation to map processing activities to controller vs processor roles and manage them as separate control sets (only general role definitions in (2)(n), (2)(x) and processor contract rules in (15 bis)).\n\nCategory 1 (Understanding the needs and expectations of interested parties): The law strongly recognizes the data subject (\u201ctitular\u201d) and their rights (4)-(11), and creates a supervisory authority (30)-(32), but it does not require organizations to identify *all relevant interested parties* (customers, partners, processors/subprocessors, regulators, etc.) and determine their requirements as an input to a PIMS. It imposes duties toward the public (transparency in (14 ter)) and toward the Agency (e.g., breach reporting (14 sexies)), yet it does not mandate a stakeholder-requirements determination process.\n\nAdditionally, ISO expects deciding which interested-party requirements will be addressed through the PIMS; Law 21.719 does not require such prioritization or explicit linkage between stakeholder expectations and internal controls. Climate-change-related expectations of interested parties are also not contemplated (no parallel to ISO 4.2 note; see absence across (3), (14 ter), (15 ter)).\n\nCategory 2 (Determining the scope of the PIMS): The law defines scope of *legal applicability* (1) and territorial scope (1 bis), and it broadly covers \u201ctodo tratamiento\u201d (1), but it does not require an organization to define and maintain a *documented PIMS scope* (boundaries, applicability, interfaces, included processing) as ISO 4.3 requires. Legal scope \u2260 management system scope.\n\nThere is also no requirement to consider internal/external issues (ISO 4.1) and interested-party requirements (ISO 4.2) when setting that scope; the law instead sets mandatory applicability rules (1), (1 bis) without requiring an internal scoping exercise.\n\nCategory 3 (Privacy information management system): Law 21.719 imposes substantive privacy obligations (principles in (3), transparency (14 ter), security (14 quinquies), DPIA (15 ter), breach notification (14 sexies)), but it does not require establishing, implementing, maintaining, and *continually improving* a formal PIMS with defined processes and interactions (ISO 4.4). The closest concept is the optional compliance program/model (49)-(53), but it is voluntary and framed as \u201cmodelo de prevenci\u00f3n de infracciones,\u201d not a full ISO-style management system.\n\nTherefore, an organization could comply with the law without having a PIMS lifecycle (plan-do-check-act), process interaction mapping, or continual improvement mechanisms required by ISO 27701.\n\nCategory 4 (Leadership and commitment): The law assigns legal responsibility to the \u201cresponsable\u201d (3)(e) and creates accountability mechanisms and sanctions (33)-(38), but it does not require *top management* to demonstrate leadership/commitment to a PIMS, integrate PIMS requirements into business processes, ensure resources, or promote continual improvement (ISO 5.1). The law\u2019s governance expectations are mostly external (Agency oversight) rather than internal leadership duties.\n\nEven where a \u201cdelegado de protecci\u00f3n de datos\u201d may be designated (50) and a compliance model may exist (49), the law does not impose ISO-style leadership obligations (privacy objectives aligned with strategy, management support, resourcing) as mandatory requirements.\n\nCategory 5 (Privacy policy): The law requires public-facing information including the \u201cpol\u00edtica de tratamiento de datos personales\u201d and its version/date (14 ter)(a), but ISO 5.2 requires a *top-management-established privacy policy* that includes commitments to meet requirements and to continual improvement, and that is communicated internally and available to interested parties. Law 21.719 does not require the policy to include a continual improvement commitment, nor does it explicitly require internal communication/awareness of the policy (it focuses on public availability).\n\nAlso, (14 ter) is a transparency duty; it does not require the policy to function as a framework for measurable privacy objectives (ISO 5.2(b)).\n\nCategory 6 (Roles, responsibilities and authorities): The law defines controller/processor roles (2)(n), (2)(x) and regulates processor contracts and sub-delegation (15 bis), and it allows/structures a DPO-like role (50) within optional compliance models (49). However, ISO 5.3 requires top management to assign and communicate responsibilities/authorities for ensuring PIMS conformity and reporting PIMS performance to top management. Law 21.719 does not mandate an internal role assignment scheme for PIMS governance.\n\nMoreover, the \u201cdelegado\u201d is optional (\u201cpodr\u00e1 designar\u201d) (50), and even when present, the law does not require a formal responsibility matrix, reporting lines, or explicit authority to ensure PIMS conformity as ISO expects.\n\nCategory 7 (Actions to address risks and opportunities / privacy risk assessment process): The law includes risk-based concepts (security measures \u201cadecuadas al riesgo\u201d in (14 quinquies); DPIA for \u201calto riesgo\u201d in (15 ter); breach notification triggered by \u201criesgo razonable\u201d in (14 sexies)). But ISO 6.1 requires a *defined privacy risk assessment process* with risk criteria, repeatability/consistency, statement of applicability, risk treatment plan, risk owner approval, and documented information. Law 21.719 does not require risk acceptance criteria, a statement of applicability, or a formal risk treatment plan with approvals.\n\nDPIA (15 ter) is narrower than ISO\u2019s ongoing risk management: it is triggered by certain high-risk processing types and is \u201cprevio al inicio,\u201d but the law does not require periodic reassessment, consistent methodology, or integration with a broader PIMS planning cycle (ISO 6.1.1/6.1.2).\n\nCategory 8 (Privacy objectives and planning to achieve them): Law 21.719 does not require organizations to set *measurable privacy objectives* at relevant functions/levels, monitor them, document them, and plan actions/resources/responsibilities/timelines (ISO 6.2). The law focuses on compliance duties and rights (3), (4)-(11), (14)-(15 ter) rather than internal performance objectives.\n\nEven the compliance model (49) requires identifying risky processes and protocols, but it does not require measurable objectives, monitoring, or objective planning as ISO specifies.\n\nCategory 9 (Planning of changes): The law requires privacy by design and by default (14 qu\u00e1ter) and DPIA before high-risk processing (15 ter), but it does not require that *changes to the PIMS* be carried out in a planned manner (ISO 6.3), nor does it define change management for privacy controls, documentation, or processes. Compliance can be reactive (e.g., sanctions/remediation in (35)) rather than planned change governance.\n\nThus, there is no explicit obligation to manage organizational/process/technology changes through a structured PIMS change-planning process.\n\nCategory 10 (Resources): While the law requires adopting security measures (14 quinquies) and transparency mechanisms (14 ter), it does not require an organization to determine and provide resources for establishing/maintaining/continually improving a PIMS (ISO 7.1). The only explicit resourcing language is limited to ensuring the DPO has \u201crecursos materiales\u201d if appointed (50), and even that is conditional on designation.\n\nTherefore, ISO\u2019s systematic resource planning for the management system is not covered by the legal framework.\n\nCategory 11 (Competence): The law does not require determining competence needs, ensuring competence via training/experience, and retaining evidence (ISO 7.2). It mentions that the DPO must have \u201cidoneidad, capacidad y conocimientos espec\u00edficos\u201d (50), but that is limited to that role and does not establish an organization-wide competence management requirement for all personnel affecting privacy performance.\n\nAlso, (49) requires internal protocols and sanctions, but not competence evaluation or documented evidence of competence.\n\nCategory 12 (Awareness): Law 21.719 does not require personnel awareness of the privacy policy, their contribution to PIMS effectiveness, or implications of nonconformity (ISO 7.3). It imposes confidentiality duties (3)(h), (14 bis), and for public officials (46), but these are legal duties, not an internal awareness program requirement.\n\nThe compliance model can include internal rules in employment contracts/regulations (49), yet it still does not mandate awareness activities or tracking.\n\nCategory 13 (Communication): The law mandates certain external communications: transparency disclosures (14 ter), breach reporting to the Agency and sometimes to data subjects (14 sexies), and public bodies\u2019 monthly publication of data-sharing agreements (22). However, ISO 7.4 requires an organization to determine *internal and external communications* for the PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a planned communication process. Law 21.719 does not require a communication plan covering internal communications, escalation paths, or systematic stakeholder communication beyond specific legal notices.\n\nHence, communication is prescribed in discrete cases, not managed as a PIMS communication process.\n\nCategory 14 (Documented information): Law 21.719 requires some records (e.g., breach notification records in (14 sexies); evidence of response delivery in (11); written data transfer/cession contracts (15); processor contracts (15 bis)), but ISO 7.5 requires a controlled documented information system (creation, updating, approval, version control, retention/disposition, protection, control of external documents). The law does not establish document control requirements for a PIMS.\n\nEven where (14 ter)(a) asks for policy version/date, it does not require broader documentation governance (e.g., retention schedules for PIMS documents, change control, access control for documentation as such).\n\nCategory 15 (Operational planning and control): The law imposes operational obligations (security controls (14 quinquies), privacy by design (14 qu\u00e1ter), processor controls (15 bis), DPIA (15 ter)), but it does not require organizations to define operational criteria for PIMS processes, control processes against criteria, ensure documented evidence of operational control, or control unintended changes (ISO 8.1). The law is outcome/duty-based rather than process-control-based.\n\nIt also does not require systematic control of \u201cexternally provided processes\u201d as part of a PIMS beyond the processor contract requirements (15 bis), which are narrower than ISO\u2019s operational control expectations across all relevant outsourced processes.\n\nCategory 16 (Privacy risk assessment \u2013 performance at planned intervals): The law requires DPIA before certain high-risk processing (15 ter) and security measures proportionate to risk (14 quinquies), but it does not require performing privacy risk assessments at *planned intervals* or when significant changes occur, nor retaining documented results of periodic risk assessments (ISO 8.2). DPIA is event-triggered and limited to \u201calto riesgo\u201d processing types.\n\nTherefore, ISO\u2019s recurring risk assessment cadence and documentation expectations are not fully met by the legal framework.\n\nCategory 17 (Privacy risk treatment): Law 21.719 requires adopting measures to mitigate risks in DPIA context (15 ter mentions \u201cmedidas de mitigaci\u00f3n\u201d) and security measures (14 quinquies), but it does not require a formal *risk treatment plan* implementation and retention of documented results (ISO 8.3). There is no requirement for selecting controls, documenting treatment decisions, assigning risk owners, or tracking residual risk acceptance.\n\nRemediation is addressed mainly through enforcement (35) and optional compliance programs (49), not through a mandatory risk treatment lifecycle.\n\nCategory 18 (Monitoring, measurement, analysis and evaluation): The law provides for Agency oversight and sanctions (30 bis, 33-38) and requires some internal records (14 sexies, 11), but it does not require organizations to define what to monitor/measure, methods, frequency, analysis, and evaluation of privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness (ISO 9.1). Compliance is assessed externally (by the Agency) rather than through internal performance monitoring requirements.\n\nEven the optional compliance model (49) does not mandate metrics, monitoring plans, or documented evaluation results.\n\nCategory 19 (Internal audit): Law 21.719 does not require internal audits of a PIMS (ISO 9.2), audit programs, auditor independence, audit criteria/scope, or documented audit results. The closest analog is external \u201cfiscalizaci\u00f3n\u201d by the Agency (30 bis)(c) and internal supervision by a DPO if appointed (50)(c), but neither equals an ISO internal audit requirement.\n\nThus, an organization can comply with the law without any internal audit function for privacy management.\n\nCategory 20 (Management review): The law does not require top management to conduct periodic management reviews of a PIMS (ISO 9.3), including inputs (audit results, performance trends, changes in context) and outputs (decisions on improvements). The law\u2019s governance is primarily regulatory (Agency) and does not impose internal review cycles.\n\nEven where the DPO must develop an annual work plan and report results (50)(f), that is not a top-management management review of the whole system, and it is conditional on having a DPO.\n\nCategory 21 (Continual improvement): Law 21.719 does not impose a general obligation to continually improve a PIMS (ISO 10.1). It does require remediation measures after sanctions (35) and encourages prevention (48) and optional compliance models (49), but these are not framed as continual improvement of a management system, nor do they require systematic improvement cycles.\n\nTherefore, improvement is reactive (post-incident/post-sanction) or voluntary, not a mandatory continual improvement requirement.\n\nCategory 22 (Nonconformity and corrective action): The law provides enforcement, sanctions, and required corrective measures after sanctions (35), and it defines infringements (34 bis, 34 ter, 34 qu\u00e1ter). However, ISO 10.2 requires an internal process to react to nonconformities, determine causes, prevent recurrence, review effectiveness, and retain documented evidence. Law 21.719 does not require root-cause analysis, preventive action across the organization, or documented corrective-action management as an internal system.\n\nIn short, the law addresses \u201cnoncompliance\u201d mainly through regulatory procedures (41)-(43) and sanctions (35)-(38), not through an ISO-style corrective action process embedded in a PIMS."
