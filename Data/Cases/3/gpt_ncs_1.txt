"Category 0: The law defines controller/processor roles (\u201cresponsable\u201d and \u201ctercero mandatario o encargado\u201d) and territorial scope ((2)(n), (2)(x), (1 bis), (15 bis)), but it does not require organizations to perform a structured \u201ccontext\u201d determination as a management-system activity (e.g., identifying internal/external issues, governance, contractual requirements, applicable judicial/administrative decisions) nor to document that determination. It also does not address the ISO 27701:2025 explicit prompt to consider whether climate change is a relevant issue.\n\nCategory 1: While the law strongly recognizes data subjects (\u201ctitulares\u201d) and their rights ((4)\u2013(11)) and creates a supervisory authority (Title VI), it does not require an organization to systematically identify all \u201cinterested parties\u201d relevant to a PIMS (e.g., customers, suppliers/subprocessors, partners, employees, regulators) and determine which of their requirements will be addressed through the PIMS. The law focuses on legal duties and rights, but not on a management process for mapping stakeholder expectations and translating them into PIMS requirements.\n\nCategory 2: The law sets broad applicability and territorial scope ((1), (1 bis)) but does not require an organization to define and maintain a documented scope statement for a privacy information management system, including boundaries, interfaces, and applicability across business units, products, locations, and processing activities. There is no explicit requirement to make such scope available as documented information.\n\nCategory 3: The law imposes substantive privacy obligations (principles in (3), controller duties in (14)\u2013(15 ter), security and breach notification in (14 quinquies)\u2013(14 sexies)), but it does not compel establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving a formal PIMS with defined processes and interactions. \u201cModelos de prevenci\u00f3n/programas de cumplimiento\u201d are voluntary ((49)), so the management-system requirement is not mandated.\n\nCategory 4: There is no explicit requirement for \u201ctop management\u201d leadership and commitment to a PIMS (integration into business processes, ensuring resources, promoting continual improvement) comparable to ISO clause 5.1. Some governance expectations exist for certain public bodies ((54) second paragraph) and optional DPO autonomy ((50)), but these are not framed as top-management PIMS leadership obligations for all organizations.\n\nCategory 5: The law requires public-facing information including a \u201cpol\u00edtica de tratamiento de datos personales\u201d ((14 ter)(a)), but it does not require a privacy policy meeting ISO management-system characteristics (framework for objectives, commitment to continual improvement of the PIMS, internal communication, controlled documented information). The policy duty is primarily transparency-oriented, not a PIMS policy requirement.\n\nCategory 6: The law introduces roles like \u201cdelegado de protecci\u00f3n de datos\u201d (optional) and requires certain contact points ((14) second paragraph; (14 ter)(c); (50)), but it does not require assignment and communication of responsibilities/authorities for all relevant PIMS roles, nor a designated management representative accountable for PIMS conformity and reporting to top management. The DPO is optional ((50)) and tied mainly to compliance support, not to PIMS governance.\n\nCategory 7: The law contains risk-based concepts (security measures \u201cadequate to risk\u201d in (14 quinquies); DPIA for high-risk processing in (15 ter); risk criteria for breach notification in (14 sexies)), but it does not require a comprehensive \u201crisks and opportunities\u201d planning process for the PIMS, including integration into processes and evaluation of effectiveness. It also does not require a statement of applicability, risk acceptance criteria, risk owners, or a documented risk treatment plan as ISO 27701 expects.\n\nCategory 8: The law does not require organizations to set measurable privacy objectives, monitor them, communicate them, update them, and document plans to achieve them. Even the voluntary compliance program elements in (49) do not mandate objectives at functions/levels or measurable targets; they focus on controls/protocols and reporting mechanisms.\n\nCategory 9: There is no requirement to plan changes to a PIMS in a controlled manner. The law addresses changes implicitly through ongoing compliance and DPIA \u201cprior to start\u201d for high-risk processing ((15 ter)), but it does not establish a general change-management requirement for privacy management system changes.\n\nCategory 10: The law requires adopting security measures ((14 quinquies)) and, for DPOs, providing \u201cmeans and faculties\u201d ((50)), but it does not require determining and providing resources for establishing/maintaining/continually improving a PIMS. Resource allocation is not framed as a management-system requirement across the organization.\n\nCategory 11: The law references training/formation as a DPO function ((50)(d)) and requires internal incorporation of compliance obligations into contracts or internal regulations ((49) second paragraph), but it does not require determining competence needs, ensuring competence, taking actions to acquire competence, and retaining evidence of competence for personnel affecting privacy performance. These are not mandatory, organization-wide competence controls.\n\nCategory 12: The law does not require awareness programs ensuring personnel are aware of the privacy policy, their contribution to PIMS effectiveness, and implications of nonconformity. Some awareness is implied by internal sanctions/procedures in a voluntary program ((49)(g)), but ISO-style mandatory awareness requirements are not established.\n\nCategory 13: The law mandates external transparency and communication to data subjects and the public ((14 ter), (5), (10)\u2013(11), (14 sexies)), but it does not require an organization to determine and plan internal/external communications for the PIMS (what/when/with whom/how) as a management-system process. Communications are prescribed as legal notices and procedures, not as a PIMS communication plan.\n\nCategory 14: The law requires keeping certain records/evidence (e.g., proof of responses to data subject requests in (11); breach notification records in (14 sexies); written contracts for processor arrangements in (15 bis); written data transfers/assignments in (15); DPIA performance in (15 ter)), but it does not require a documented-information control system (creation/update, approval, versioning, access control, retention/disposition) for PIMS documentation. The \u201cpolicy\u201d must show date/version ((14 ter)(a)), yet broader ISO 7.5 controls are not mandated.\n\nCategory 15: The law imposes operational obligations (lawful basis (12)\u2013(13), purpose limitation (3)(b), minimization/retention (3)(c), security (14 quinquies), processor controls (15 bis), DPIA (15 ter)), but it does not require operational planning and control as a PIMS process with defined criteria, controlled changes, and documented evidence \u201cto the extent necessary\u201d for confidence in process execution. Control of externally provided processes is partially addressed via processor contracts ((15 bis)), but not as a general PIMS operational control framework.\n\nCategory 16: DPIAs are required for high-risk processing ((15 ter)), and security measures are risk-based ((14 quinquies)), but ISO 27701\u2019s \u201cprivacy risk assessment\u201d is broader and requires defined criteria, repeatability, comparability, and documented results at planned intervals or upon significant changes. The law does not require periodic privacy risk assessments across processing activities, nor a consistent methodology beyond DPIA triggers and Agency guidance.\n\nCategory 17: The law requires implementing security measures and mitigation in DPIA context ((14 quinquies), (15 ter)) and recording breach handling ((14 sexies)), but it does not require a formal privacy risk treatment plan, approval by risk owners, acceptance of residual risks, and retention of documented information about treatment results as ISO 8.3 expects. Mitigation is implied, not structured as risk treatment governance.\n\nCategory 18: The law provides for Agency oversight and sanctions (Title VI\u2013VII) and requires some internal reporting mechanisms in voluntary programs ((49)(f)), but it does not require organizations to define what to monitor/measure, methods, timing, analysis, and evaluation of privacy performance and PIMS effectiveness. Compliance is enforced externally rather than through mandated internal performance evaluation.\n\nCategory 19: The law does not require internal audits of a PIMS, an audit program, auditor independence, reporting, or retention of audit evidence. The Agency can audit/fiscalize ((30 bis)(c)), but that is regulatory supervision, not an internal audit requirement under ISO 27701.\n\nCategory 20: There is no requirement for top management to conduct periodic management reviews of a PIMS with specified inputs/outputs and documented evidence. Some governance duties exist for certain constitutional bodies to issue internal policies and controls ((54) second paragraph), but not an ISO-style management review cycle.\n\nCategory 21: The law does not mandate continual improvement of a PIMS. It creates enforcement, sanctions, and optional compliance models ((49)\u2013(53)), and it allows the Agency to require corrective measures after sanctions ((35) second paragraph), but this is not the same as a required continual improvement process for suitability/adequacy/effectiveness of a PIMS.\n\nCategory 22: The law provides corrective-type consequences through Agency-imposed measures to \u201csubsanar\u201d causes after sanctions ((35) second paragraph) and allows suspension of processing for repeated severe infringements ((38)), but it does not require an internal nonconformity and corrective action process (root cause analysis, preventing recurrence, effectiveness review, documented evidence) as a management-system obligation. The framework is enforcement-driven rather than requiring organizations to run ISO 10.2-style corrective action workflows."
